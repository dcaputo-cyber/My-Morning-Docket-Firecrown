[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up: California. If you operate any property that touches a California resident, and at a media company with national reach the answer is always yes, the California Privacy Protection Agency has told the regulated community, in effect, that audit season is here. The Agency's Audits Division, a new unit created last year, has said it expects to begin CCPA compliance audits in 2026, and those audits can be both announced and unannounced. This follows the largest CCPA settlement to date, which dropped last July: one point five five million dollars from the California Attorney General against an online health information publisher. The alleged conduct will sound familiar to anyone who has ever inherited a cookie banner from a prior vendor. The AG said the publisher failed to honor consumer opt-out requests, improperly shared personal data with third parties, and maintained a cookie banner that did not actually work. That is not a technology failure. That is a legal failure that happens to ride on technology. The practical takeaway: if you have not, in the last ninety days, personally watched someone click the "Do Not Sell or Share" link on each of your properties and then verified in a network panel that downstream pixels actually stop firing, that is the exercise for this quarter. The Agency is telling you what it plans to do. Believe it.

Second headline: ad tech, and this one is big. Earlier this month, the Federal Trade Commission, joined by a coalition of state attorneys general, filed an action accusing WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu of colluding, since 2018, to impose common "brand safety" standards across the digital advertising ecosystem. The FTC's theory, stripped of the legal vocabulary, is that the three largest agency holding companies in the world agreed with each other on what publishers their clients would and would not spend money with, and that this coordination distorted the modern public square. Whatever you think of the merits, and there is a lot to think about on the merits, the implication for a media company is immediate. If you have ever been quietly cut from a plan, or had a client media team tell you, off the record, that a GARM score or a Sincera flag tanked your inclusion, the FTC has now given that conversation a new and potentially discoverable vocabulary. Expect subpoenas for any documentation related to brand safety determinations affecting your properties. If you have audience data, brand safety scorecards, or vendor correspondence on these decisions, now is the time to make sure legal holds are appropriately scoped, and that your sales and ad ops teams know what to do if they get a preservation request.

Third: publishing economics. A data point worth noting. The Mail Plus product, the Daily Mail's paid digital offering, crossed four hundred thousand paying subscribers on April second. That is a meaningful milestone, but the story behind the number is more instructive. Across the broader industry, publishers have stopped chasing raw subscriber growth and are now, overwhelmingly, focused on retention and on moving existing subscribers into higher-priced tiers and bundles. CivicScience data from their 2026 subscription landscape report shows that Americans with two or more publisher subscriptions are up fifty percent over five years, and the hundred-to-two-hundred-dollar-a-year spend tier has surged fifty-seven percent. The category winners, in order of consumer willingness to pay, are entertainment, sports, national news, local news, and business. That ranking should inform how you think about any bundle or cross-subsidy arrangement across a portfolio of titles. From a legal perspective, more tiers and more bundles mean more automatic renewal disclosures, more state-specific cancellation flows, and more exposure under laws like California's Automatic Renewal Law and the FTC's so-called Click-to-Cancel rule, the reach of which continues to be litigated. If your portfolio has added tiers or bundles in the last six months, add a subscription compliance check to your next quarterly review.

Fourth: the United Kingdom. Three items, quickly. One, the Data Use and Access Act 2025, the DUAA, is now substantively in force as of February. It rationalizes parts of the UK GDPR and the PEC Regulations and, importantly, introduces a new formal complaints-handling requirement that begins in June 2026. From that date forward, data subjects must raise a complaint with the controller first, and only escalate to the ICO if unsatisfied. That means you need a documented, monitored, and responsive complaints process by June. If your UK-facing privacy inbox is a shared mailbox with no workflow, that is a June problem. Two, the ICO continues to enforce hard against kids-privacy failures. In February, Reddit was fined fourteen point four seven million pounds. Last fall, MediaLab was hit with nearly a quarter million for Imgur. Age assurance is not a marketing exercise anymore. It is a compliance one. Three, the ICO's Tech Futures Report on agentic AI, published in January, flags something you should care about: when an AI agent acts on behalf of a user or a business, the controllership question — who is the data controller, for what processing, with what liability — gets genuinely hard. If you are deploying, or allowing your business units to deploy, agentic tools, make sure you are thinking about this now, not after the fact.

Fifth: on the SEC side, a brief note for those of you watching public company disclosure, and relevant for anyone thinking about capital markets activity this year. The SEC's comprehensive review of Regulation S-K, announced by the Chairman in January, had its public comment period close on April thirteenth. The Commission is soliciting views on how to narrow disclosure down to what is genuinely material and strip out what is not. This is a rare, wide-open conversation about the architecture of Reg S-K, and it is happening concurrently with the Commission's April agenda item on rationalization of disclosure practices, and proposed amendments to Rule 144 and to Exchange Act Rule 14a-8, the shareholder proposal rule. The cumulative direction of travel at the Commission this year is lighter, more focused, more capital-formation-oriented. If you advise on any public company matters, or if you are planning around a potential public transaction, the Reg S-K review is the one to watch.

Sixth and final headline: enterprise AI products had a busy week. Snowflake rolled out updates to Snowflake Intelligence and Cortex Code and is explicitly positioning its platform as, quote, "the agentic enterprise" control plane, with native connections to Gmail, Google Calendar, Jira, and Salesforce. C3 AI made its C3 Code product generally available earlier this month, pitching natural-language enterprise application building. Equinix launched Fabric Intelligence, an AI-native networking layer. And Stanford's HAI 2026 AI Index landed with the headline that eighty-eight percent of organizations now use AI in at least one business function, and that generative AI has hit fifty-three percent population adoption in three years — faster than the personal computer or the internet at the same stage. OpenAI disclosed that enterprise now exceeds forty percent of its revenue and is on track to match consumer by year-end. Anthropic's disclosed run rate has gone from nine billion at the end of last year to thirty billion now. [DEEP DIVE — AI AND COPYRIGHT]

Last summer, in June of 2025, Judge William Alsup in the Northern District of California issued the first federal ruling that meaningfully applied the fair use doctrine to the question of AI training on copyrighted books. The case was Bartz versus Anthropic. The ruling was split, and the split matters.

On one hand, Judge Alsup held that the act of training the model — copying books for the purpose of teaching an AI system to generate new text — was fair use. His reasoning drew on three pillars. First, he called generative AI "quintessentially transformative." Second, he found that copying entire books was "reasonably necessary" for the training to work. Third, he concluded that the model's outputs did not displace market demand for the original books. If you squint, this is Sega versus Accolade and Authors Guild versus Google, updated for large language models. The transformation is not what the model outputs; it is what the model becomes.

On the other hand, Judge Alsup held that Anthropic's decision to download pirated copies of books and retain them in a so-called central library was not protected by fair use — even though only parts of that library were ultimately used for training. That finding is what drove Anthropic's class settlement: one point five billion dollars in August 2025, the largest copyright recovery in United States history.

Now, the meaning of that split has taken a while to settle, because a second case told a very different story. Thomson Reuters versus Ross Intelligence, decided by a court in Delaware, involved a legal research startup that had used Westlaw headnotes to train an AI legal research tool. The court granted summary judgment for Thomson Reuters. It found the use was not transformative, it was commercial, and it directly harmed the market for the original work. Ross is now defunct.

So if you are trying to reconcile the two decisions, and you are, because any intellectually honest GC would be, the picture that has emerged over the last eight to ten months is this: legal sourcing matters more than the act of training itself. Pirated books, even in a research library, trigger liability. Legitimately licensed content, used to train a model whose outputs do not substitute for the original work, may be defensible as fair use.

That gives you three practical operating principles.

Principle one: for your own properties, assume that your content is valuable training data and that the market for that data is real. The days of letting crawlers consume your archives for free, and hoping it produced reciprocal referral traffic, are over. Reciprocity was a 2010s bargain. In 2026, your archives are an asset. The licensing market for that asset is nascent but real. News Corp licensed its content to OpenAI for over two hundred fifty million dollars. Others have followed. If you do not have a documented policy on AI crawler access at the robots.txt and TDMRep level, and if you cannot tell me, off the top of your head, which AI vendors have purchased a license from Firecrown to ingest FreightWaves, Trains, FLYING, Boating, or any other property, that is a gap to close this quarter. There are no prizes for being the last publisher to figure out their content is worth money.

Principle two: for vendors who sell you AI products, contractually require disclosure of the provenance of training data. This is the one piece of AI diligence that is currently being under-litigated by in-house counsel, because it feels technical. It is not technical. It is contractual. Your vendor should be required, in writing, to represent that their training data was lawfully sourced, and to indemnify you for third-party IP claims arising from outputs. Bartz tells you that pirated inputs are recoverable damages. Ross tells you that commercial substitution is recoverable damages. If you are signing an AI contract in 2026 without these two provisions, you are signing a contract that assumes risk no prudent counterparty would assume.

Principle three: for your own employees, and this is the subtler point — internal policies on what they can paste into a public LLM need to be more specific than a generic "do not share confidential information" line. Consider, specifically, pre-publication manuscripts, source lists, unsigned contracts, and internal legal advice. Those four categories should be explicitly called out in your AI use policy, and training on that policy should be distributed company-wide, with acknowledgment tracking. You do not want to be the GC who has to explain to a board why a draft story, or a reporter's source list, ended up embedded in a third party's training corpus because someone used a consumer AI tool to clean up grammar.

No further fair use rulings are expected before the summer. The Second Circuit has multiple appeals pending, and consensus among watchers is that nothing dispositive lands before late summer or early fall. Which gives you a window. Use it.

[GC BENCH]

Two developments in legal AI that crossed my desk this week, and one recommendation.

First: Lexis has rebranded and expanded what used to be called Lexis Plus AI. The new product is Lexis Plus with Protege. As of February, Protege deploys four specialized agents, which in practice means that what you previously did by prompting a single model — drafting, reviewing, researching, summarizing — is now being handled by task-specific agents underneath a unified interface. Thomson Reuters is doing something similar with CoCounsel Legal and its own agentic workflows. The practical implication for an in-house team the size of a single general counsel, which I imagine describes many of you listening, is that agentic tools are most valuable not for the speed of any single task, but for the reliability of a chained workflow: pull the latest draft, check it against the playbook, flag deviations, propose redlines, generate a cover email. In 2025, that workflow took five tools and a human to run. In 2026, it is collapsing into one.

Second: a note on GC AI, which is purpose-built for in-house legal and now reports more than fifteen hundred in-house teams across fifty-three countries. I mention it not as an endorsement but as a market signal. The proliferation of tools built specifically for in-house counsel, as opposed to law firm workflows retrofitted for in-house use, is the real story of 2026 in legal tech. For a general counsel of your profile, the right question is not "which tool should I buy." It is "which two workflows in my department, if automated well, would free up the most of my time." Contract review intake, NDA triage, and privacy policy Q-and-A are the three most commonly cited answers, and all three are plausible fits for agentic tooling available today.

And the recommendation, because I promised one: spend twenty minutes this week with the Corporate Compliance Insights piece on AI risk for the general counsel in 2026. The thesis, briefly, is that the governance conversation has shifted from "can this tool make us faster" to "can this tool withstand scrutiny if challenged." If your department does not have an AI governance memo that addresses the second question, the first is increasingly irrelevant.

[INDUSTRY ANGLE]

Quickly now, because these matter for your verticals.

Freight. Dry van spot rates rose faster than contract rates in February, the largest spread move since February 2022. The market is, in the language of the FreightWaves team, finally transitioning out of the oversupply conditions that defined the last three and a half years. Capacity is contracting. Rate floors are resetting higher. Craig Fuller's public posture on this has been consistent: the back half of 2026 is likely to reward carriers more than shippers, and the structural driver is the continued decline in the driver population. Volumes are leveling off, not surging, but the tighter capacity is doing real work on the rate side. If FreightWaves has a revenue exposure question for the quarter, it is whether SONAR bulk trucking rates, launched earlier this year as the first integrated global van, reefer, flatbed, and bulk platform, is being adopted by the enterprise customers most motivated by tightening.

Rail. The Association of American Railroads reported that March was the strongest single month for U.S. rail carload volumes since October 2022, averaging just over two hundred thirty thousand carloads per week. Intermodal, notably, is not participating in the recovery — rates there remain anchored near cycle lows. That divergence is the story worth watching for Trains readers and advertisers.

Aviation. Two items for FLYING. The FAA's new NOTAM system comes online this Friday, April eighteenth, with a four-hour transition window between midnight and four a.m. Eastern. Expect a short period of disruption and monitor pilot community channels for feedback. And AOPA is running a month-long pilot mental health initiative at Sun 'n Fun and through May, tying to Mental Health Awareness Month. That is a story with genuine editorial depth for FLYING, and a sensitive one — pilot mental health intersects with medical certification policy, and anything your editorial team writes will land in front of FAA physicians and AME networks.
