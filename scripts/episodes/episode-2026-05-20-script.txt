[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. The Take It Down Act. The Federal Trade Commission's enforcement authority over Section Three of the law commenced at midnight Eastern yesterday morning. Three operational points. First, the statute requires every covered platform — defined to include social-media services, messaging services, image-or-video-sharing services, and any comparable consumer-facing platform — to remove a reported non-consensual intimate image or video, including digital forgeries created using artificial intelligence, within forty-eight hours of receiving a valid takedown request. The clock is hard. Second, the agency's pre-commencement stakeholder letter, addressed to sixteen of the largest covered platforms, made clear that the agency considers the forty-eight-hour duty operational from minute one. The civil penalty is fifty-three-thousand-eighty-eight dollars per offense. Third, the agency has signaled that enforcement actions against non-compliant operators are expected within the current calendar quarter. The operational implication for a publisher that operates user-generated-content surfaces — comments, forums, video uploads — is that the takedown intake and the forty-eight-hour processing pipeline both need to be live this morning. We will return to the intake architecture on the deep dive.

Second. The United Parcel Service hearing. The Board's investigative hearing convened at eight o'clock Eastern yesterday at the L'Enfant Plaza facility in Washington. The Day-One record produced two substantive developments. First, on the mechanical question. The spherical-bearing component in the engine-mount pylon that failed on Flight twenty-nine seventy-six had failed on four prior occasions on other aircraft. Boeing issued a service bulletin in 2011 — fifteen years before the Louisville crash — advising operators of the problem and characterizing the failure as not posing a threat to flight safety. The Board's questioning yesterday focused on how that judgment was reached, on the maintenance-records evidence, and on the airworthiness-directive escalation pathway. Second, on the crewing question. The flight crew aboard Flight twenty-nine seventy-six was not the originally scheduled crew for that airframe. An aircraft swap earlier in the operational day routed the originally scheduled crew to a different airframe and routed the lost crew onto the aircraft that subsequently failed. The hearing reconvenes at eight o'clock Eastern this morning and concludes at one o'clock this afternoon. The full hearing record will be the foundational document for the parallel civil litigation in Kentucky state court, where fifteen additional suits were filed earlier this month by Louisville families and businesses.

Third. The Federal Trade Commission and StubHub. The Commission announced a settlement earlier this month resolving allegations that StubHub deceptively advertised ticket prices in violation of the Commission's Fee Rule, which took effect in May 2025. The settlement obligates StubHub to disclose total prices, inclusive of all mandatory fees, at the point of advertising — not at the point of checkout — and includes a civil penalty in the eight-figure range. Separately, on the same docket, the Commission has issued a notice of proposed rulemaking that would extend the Fee Rule's coverage to online food and grocery delivery services. The Fee Rule is the most important consumer-protection rulemaking of the current administration's first year, and the StubHub settlement is the first significant penalty under it. For any publisher that runs a subscription or commerce surface with ancillary charges — service fees, processing fees, convenience fees, premium-tier upcharges — the StubHub settlement establishes the operational standard. The total price, inclusive of all required charges, must be the advertised price. Late-stage fee disclosure is now a deceptive practice under federal rule.

Fourth. The Securities and Exchange Commission. The comment window on Chairman Atkins's January thirteenth Regulation S-K comprehensive review closed on April thirteenth. The staff in the Division of Corporation Finance is in the comment-evaluation phase. The Commission has not signaled a specific notice-of-proposed-rulemaking date, but the staff's working guidance — communicated through speeches, through private convenings, and through the May Item Four-Oh-Two roundtable record — places a proposed rule on executive-compensation disclosure on a second-half-of-this-year calendar. The direction of travel, consistent with the Chairman's December 2025 New York Stock Exchange address, is a materiality-driven, principles-based regime with scaled requirements for smaller and newly public issuers. The 2027 proxy season is the operative target. Any media company with a public parent should be in the planning conversation between finance, communications, and legal this quarter — not next quarter. The Compensation Discussion and Analysis, the Summary Compensation Table, the peer-group selection methodology, and the related benchmarking process are all likely to look meaningfully different.

Fifth. The United Kingdom Information Commissioner's Office. Section One-Oh-Three of the Data Use and Access Act 2025 — the mandatory complaints-procedure obligation — takes effect on Friday, June nineteenth. Thirty days. The substantive requirement is straightforward. A data subject who wishes to complain about a controller's handling of personal data must first submit that complaint to the controller. The controller must acknowledge receipt within thirty days, must investigate the complaint, and must provide a substantive response. Only after the controller has had a reasonable opportunity to respond may the data subject escalate to the Information Commissioner's Office. The Commissioner has signaled, in published February guidance, that the Office will triage complaints against four criteria: the breadth of improvement to data-protection rights that an investigation could produce, the necessity of the data flow to the consumer relationship, the alignment of the complaint with strategic priorities, and the public interest. The Office's October enforcement record — the fourteen-million-pound penalty against Capita — remains the largest fine in the Office's history. The operational obligation for any media company operating in the United Kingdom is to have the complaints inbox staffed, the tracking system operational, and the acknowledgement-and-response templates live before June nineteenth.

Sixth. The California Privacy Protection Agency. The Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform — D-R-O-P — becomes operational for compliance on August first, seventy-two days from this morning. From that date, every registered data broker must access the platform at least once every forty-five days, must process consumer deletion requests within ninety days, and must report request status back into the system. The Agency's Data Broker Strike Force, announced in March, has been issuing administrative actions against unregistered operators at a sustained cadence — registration enforcement is the agency's first enforcement priority, and the per-day-per-violation fine for unregistered operation is running approximately two-hundred dollars per day. The gating question for any audience-data operation that is uncertain whether it meets the statutory definition of a data broker is whether to register before August first. The uncertainty does not survive August first. Resolve the question before then.

[DEEP DIVE]

The deep dive this morning is a theme that runs through three of the headlines — Take It Down, the United Kingdom Section One-Oh-Three complaints procedure, and the California Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform. Three jurisdictions. Three substantive subject matters. One common operational architecture. The new compliance obligation in 2026 is no longer principally about what you collect or what you publish. It is about how your business receives, processes, and responds to a structured intake from a regulator or from a consumer acting under a regulator's authority. I want to give you the architecture, the three operational consequences, and three concrete actions.

The architecture first. Each of the three frameworks imposes a different substantive obligation. The Take It Down Act requires a covered platform to remove a non-consensual intimate image or digital forgery within forty-eight hours of a valid request. The Data Use and Access Act requires a controller in the United Kingdom to acknowledge a data-subject complaint within thirty days and provide a substantive response within a reasonable period. The Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform requires a registered data broker to access the platform every forty-five days, to process consumer deletion requests within ninety days, and to report status back. The substantive obligations are different. The architectural common element is the same. Each framework converts a substantive right held by an individual into an operational deadline that runs against your organization. The deadline is short — forty-eight hours, thirty days, forty-five-day cadence. The deadline is unforgiving — the regulator does not pause it because your intake inbox was understaffed. And the deadline is auditable — the regulator can, in each of the three frameworks, demand evidence of the timing, the responsiveness, and the disposition of every request your business has received.

That is the architecture. Now the three operational consequences.

First. The intake surface is now the regulator's principal entry point. The pre-2026 enforcement model — agencies issuing subpoenas, agencies running on-site reviews, agencies opening investigative dockets on their own initiative — has not gone away. But the dominant enforcement model in each of these three frameworks is downstream of a consumer-initiated intake. The regulator does not need to discover a problem at your company. The consumer files a request. The clock starts. The clock either runs against you and produces a violation, or it does not. The regulator's discovery cost has dropped to near zero. The discovery work is being done, in effect, by the consumer.

Second. The consequence of an intake failure is structurally different from the consequence of a substantive failure. A substantive privacy failure — a data breach, an inappropriate disclosure, a consent failure — generally requires the regulator to prove harm, to prove materiality, and in some frameworks to prove intent. A timing failure under any of these three frameworks requires the regulator to prove only the timestamp. The platform either took the image down within forty-eight hours or it did not. The controller either acknowledged the complaint within thirty days or it did not. The broker either accessed the platform on the forty-fifth day or it did not. The evidentiary burden has been substantially reduced. The defensive posture has correspondingly narrowed.

Third. The intake architecture is the new audit surface. The agencies will examine the design of the intake. The user-facing entry point. The internal routing. The case-management discipline. The escalation pathway. The closure protocol. The retention of evidence. The reporting back to the requester. The audit of the audit. The agencies — and this is most explicit in the California Privacy Protection Agency's December 2025 enforcement advisory on intake-design requirements — have begun publishing operational guidance on what an intake architecture must look like to be defensible. The Information Commissioner's Office's February guidance on complaints handling does the same. The Federal Trade Commission's pre-commencement letter on Take It Down does the same. The published guidance is the de facto compliance template. Build to the template.

Now the three concrete actions for a media-company general counsel.

Action one. Build the consolidated intake map. The map identifies, for every property your business operates, every regulatorially recognized intake category — Take It Down requests, data-subject access requests, deletion requests, opt-out requests, complaints, biometric-data inquiries, child-protection notifications, copyright takedown notices under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act. Each category has a different timer. Each category has a different escalation pathway. The map is the precondition to operational defensibility. If the map is incomplete, the operational gaps are invisible until the regulator finds them.

Action two. Stand up the unified intake routing. The current state in most publisher operations is that the intake categories are routed to different teams. Privacy requests go to the privacy team. Copyright notices go to the legal-operations team. Trust-and-safety requests go to community management. Each team has its own queue, its own response template, its own service-level expectation. The 2026 enforcement architecture does not respect that internal organization. A regulator examining the timing record will treat any intake category as the timing surface. The unified routing — a single intake intake — solves the operational risk that a request lands in the wrong queue and the timer expires before the right team has acted. If you have not yet consolidated the routing, the operational case for the consolidation is the regulator's growing willingness to count days.

Action three. Document the response chain. The agencies, in each of the three frameworks, will treat the absence of documentation as a presumption against the controller. The evidentiary burden, once the regulator has the timestamp, falls on the platform to demonstrate that the request was received, was processed, was resolved, and was reported. The documentation is the defense. If the documentation does not exist, the defense does not exist. The case-management system — the platform that records each request, the team that received it, the action taken, the time-to-resolution, and the closure communication — is now the principal compliance asset. Invest in it accordingly.

One additional note on jurisdiction. The intake-and-response architecture is, in 2026, the most exportable element of any major privacy or content framework. The California model is being studied by Oregon, Connecticut, Colorado, and Texas. The United Kingdom model is being studied across the European Economic Area. The Take It Down architecture, which is federal and applies across all platforms, is the United States analog. A general counsel who builds the intake-and-response infrastructure once, at the highest specification, has built a compliance asset that will translate across the jurisdictions your business operates in. The investment is durable. The fragmented build — one intake for California, one for the United Kingdom, one for federal — is not.

[GC BENCH]

The legal-technology item this morning is the post-Bartz watch and the Concord-Music Digital-Millennium-Copyright-Act count.

The Bartz versus Anthropic fairness hearing closed last Thursday, May fourteenth. The matter is under submission. Judge Martínez-Olguín's order is expected within thirty to sixty days, which puts the outside date in mid-July. The per-work payout — approximately two-thousand-nine-hundred-thirty-one dollars — and the four-hundred-eighty-two-thousand-four-hundred-sixty work list remain the operative reference points. For any media company holding content in the affected dataset corpora, the operational posture is unchanged this morning. The Bartz number remains the floor reference point for content-owner negotiation with any frontier-model developer. If the court approves the settlement, the floor hardens. If the court modifies or remands, the floor moves, but the direction of negotiation does not.

The element that warrants attention this morning is the Concord-Music supplemental complaint against Anthropic in the Northern District of California. The supplemental complaint, filed in January, names a total of twenty-one-thousand-two-hundred-thirty-one compositions and is the largest individual music-publisher claim against a frontier-model developer to date. The statutory-damages exposure, at the maximum amount per work, exceeds three billion dollars. The element that is structurally novel in the complaint is the Digital Millennium Copyright Act count. The complaint pleads that Anthropic stripped rights-management information from the compositions during the BitTorrent acquisition that built the training corpus. The Bartz matter did not include a Digital Millennium Copyright Act stripping count. The Concord-Music supplemental complaint does. A successful stripping count would expand the statutory-damages architecture available to rights-holders in every parallel matter — including the publisher complaint against Meta filed two weeks ago, the consolidated Times-OpenAI matter, and the Disney-Universal complaint against Midjourney. For a media company general counsel who is licensing content into the frontier-model market or who is building an internal model on licensed corpora, the stripping count is the part to watch. The procedural calendar in the Anthropic consolidated matter has not been set.

One additional note on legal-technology procurement. The Claude For Legal launch that I covered Monday continues to draw attention from the broader market. Thomson Reuters' CoCounsel, LexisNexis Protégé, and the Harvey workflow surface are all repositioning against the new substrate offering. For any general counsel running a procurement evaluation in the second half of this year, the substrate-versus-workflow distinction is now the operative procurement question. Anchor on the substrate first. Layer the workflow second.

[INDUSTRY ANGLE]

A short freight note before the closing brief.

FreightWaves' tender-rejection series — the operational signal for capacity tightness in the truckload spot market — is now running above thirteen percent on the national index, with commentary from FreightWaves chief executive Craig Fuller over the weekend characterizing the print as the cleanest signal of a freight-cycle turn in three years. Werner Enterprises chief executive Derek Leathers, on the carrier earnings call earlier this month, described the rejection-rate environment as approaching pandemic-era levels — when the index regularly exceeded fifteen percent and at times climbed above twenty. The current print is not at that level, but the rate of change is comparable. National truckload spot rates are holding at approximately two-dollars-eighty cents per mile inclusive of fuel, up twenty-three percent from the same point a year ago. Less-than-truckload bids are running twelve-and-a-half percent above the prior year and twenty-nine percent above May 2021.

The Association of American Railroads' weekly traffic report continues to confirm the trucking signal. United States intermodal volume for the week ending May ninth was two-hundred-eighty-four-thousand-one-hundred-sixty-three containers and trailers, the fourth consecutive week of mid-single-digit year-over-year growth. The cumulative intermodal number for the first eighteen weeks of 2026 is up six-tenths of a percent against the prior-year cumulative, which had been negative through most of the first quarter.

For Trains and FreightWaves editorial, the through-line continues to firm. The volume direction first appeared in late March. The rate direction first appeared in late April. The tender-rejection inflection on May fourteenth is the third independent confirmation. The aviation read remains the United Parcel Service hearing concluding this afternoon, where the substantive question is mechanical and the editorial demand is at its peak. The marine read remains the mixed-conditions guidance from the National Marine Manufacturers Association — recreational powerboat sales projected on par to slightly up against a 2025 base of two-hundred-fifteen to two-hundred-twenty-five thousand units, with the value-segment aluminum-fishing category continuing to outperform the premium categories.
