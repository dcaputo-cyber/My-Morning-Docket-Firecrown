[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up, and the anchor for today: the new COPPA Rule. On April twenty-second, last Wednesday, the amendments to the Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule took effect for the vast majority of covered operators. The final rule, which the Commission finalized in January of 2025 and which has been subject to a compliance runway since, imposes several material changes to how operators can collect, use, and monetize personal information from children under thirteen. Three items in particular. First, separate, verifiable parental consent is now required before an operator can disclose a child's personal information to a third party for targeted advertising. General consent to data collection is not enough. Second, operators must limit data retention to only what is reasonably necessary to fulfill the specific purpose for which the data was collected — no indefinite archives. Third, operators must implement a written information security program reasonably designed to protect children's personal information, and must conduct an annual review. On top of those three, the FTC issued a separate policy statement on February twenty-fifth of this year addressing the use of age-verification technologies, providing limited enforcement discretion where data is collected strictly for age-gating purposes and not used for other reasons. I will unpack all of that in the deep dive. For now, put the date on your calendar: April twenty-second, 2026 — COPPA, as amended, is live.

Second: Texas. On April twenty-first, Attorney General Ken Paxton's office filed suit against Allstate and its telematics subsidiary, Arity, alleging that the companies unlawfully collected, used, and sold data about the location and movement of more than forty-five million Americans through software secretly embedded in consumer mobile apps, including Life360, GasBuddy, and others. The complaint alleges violations of the Texas Data Privacy and Security Act, the Texas Data Broker Law, and the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act. The theory, in plain English, is that when a consumer downloads a third-party app like GasBuddy, the embedded Arity SDK harvests granular location and driving-behavior data, which Arity then packages and sells to insurance companies. No settlement figure is announced — this is a freshly filed complaint, not a resolution. But this is the fourth major privacy action Paxton's office has filed in the past twelve months, after the one-point-three-seven-five-billion-dollar Google settlement last May, the one-point-four-billion-dollar Meta biometric settlement in July, and the data broker warning letters to more than a hundred companies on April fifteenth. The Texas AG is now the most active state-level privacy enforcer in the United States. If your portfolio involves any SDK integration with a consumer mobile app, this case is the one your engineering and product teams need to read before Wednesday.

Third: the federal-state artificial intelligence preemption fight. On December eleventh of last year, President Trump issued an executive order titled "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence," directing the Attorney General to establish a task force to challenge state AI laws deemed unconstitutional or preempted, and directing the Secretary of Commerce to publish an evaluation, by March eleventh, 2026, of, quote, onerous state AI laws, end quote, that conflict with federal policy. The Commerce Department evaluation landed in March, and it named specific state laws in California, Colorado, Texas, and Utah. In response, on March thirtieth, Governor Gavin Newsom of California issued what his office called a first-of-its-kind state executive order on AI protections, signaling the state's intent to defend its AI Transparency Act, SB fifty-three, and the state's Generative AI Training Data Transparency Act against preemption challenges. We now have, in plain sight, a coming Supremacy Clause litigation posture between the federal executive branch and the most consequential state AI regulator in the country. If your company operates AI tools, or integrates third-party AI products, the practical impact is a compliance landscape that could be rewritten on short notice by a district-court ruling. I'll note this only to flag it — the deep regulatory analysis belongs in a segment of its own, which we'll do later this week.

Fourth: aviation. On April fourteenth, the United States House of Representatives passed the Airspace Location and Enhanced Risk Transparency Act — the ALERT Act, House Resolution seventy-six-thirteen — by a vote of three hundred and ninety-six to ten, under suspension of the rules. The bill requires Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast In, ADS-B In, together with a corresponding collision-prevention technology, to be equipped and operating on virtually all aircraft already required to have ADS-B Out, with a compliance deadline of December thirty-first, 2031. The National Transportation Safety Board publicly endorsed the bill, noting that it addresses shortcomings identified in the agency's investigation of the January twenty-ninth, 2025, midair collision at Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport. The NTSB has also calendared a two-day investigative hearing on May nineteenth and twentieth to present findings on that collision. For FLYING's editorial side, this is the most consequential aviation safety legislation since the 2018 Aircraft Certification and Oversight Reform Act. For legal, the FAA rulemaking that follows the bill's enactment will require significant equipment-manufacturer and operator input. Put May nineteenth on your calendar.

Fifth: the rails. On April seventeenth, the Association of American Railroads published weekly traffic data for the week ending April eighteenth, 2026. Total U.S. carloads were two hundred and thirty thousand, seven hundred and forty-nine — up three percent year over year. U.S. weekly intermodal volume was two hundred and seventy-seven thousand, five hundred and fifty-four containers and trailers — up two-point-two percent year over year. That is the first positive intermodal week since mid-March and the strongest combined performance of the year to date. Eight of the ten carload commodity groups posted increases, with grain up eighteen hundred carloads, petroleum up fifteen hundred, and nonmetallic minerals up nearly fourteen hundred. Coal and miscellaneous carloads both declined. Year to date through fifteen weeks, cumulative carloads are up three-point-nine percent and intermodal is essentially flat at negative point one percent. The intermodal turn is the story. If the next two weeks confirm it, the divergence that has defined the first quarter is closing. For Trains readers, for FreightWaves advertisers, and for any editorial treatment of the industrial economy, this is the data point of the week.

Sixth: the FTC and the holding companies. A status update on a story we covered in depth last Wednesday. The April fifteenth proposed consent orders against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu, filed in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas, remain under review. The court must determine whether the proposed orders are in the public interest before entering them as final judgments. The public-comment window is the mechanism through which affected parties — in our case, publishers that allege they were excluded under coordinated brand-safety criteria — can ask the court to impose additional conditions or, less commonly, reject the settlement as insufficient. I will watch the docket carefully. If you are in-house at a publisher that operated any property between 2020 and 2024 that was categorically excluded from a major brand-safety list, and your outside counsel has not yet opened a file on this, open one this week. The statute of limitations under Section Four of the Clayton Act, which allows treble damages for antitrust injuries, is four years from the date of injury. You have less time than you think.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to COPPA. This is where I want you to spend the next ten or twelve minutes, because the Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule, as amended, is the most consequential children's privacy change since the original rule in 2000, and because publishers have a pattern, going back twenty years, of underestimating its reach.

Let me set the scene. COPPA, the statute, was enacted in 1998. The FTC's implementing rule followed in 2000 and was amended materially in 2013. In January of 2025, the Commission finalized a further round of amendments, which were published in the Federal Register on April twenty-second, 2025, with a one-year compliance runway. That runway expired five days ago. The rule, as amended, is now the governing text for any operator of a website or online service that is directed to children under thirteen, or that has actual knowledge it is collecting personal information from children under thirteen.

If you are thinking right now that your property is not a children's property and therefore this rule does not apply to you, I want you to pause. The scope question in COPPA is not whether your property is primarily for children. It is whether any material portion of your property is directed to children, or whether you have actual knowledge of child users. For a multi-vertical publisher, the answer is more often yes than no. Trains has a significant youth-enthusiast readership and a model-railroading community that skews substantially under thirteen. FLYING publishes content around young-pilot programs and the Civil Air Patrol. Boating and Sailing has junior-sailing content. Even FreightWaves' truck-driving community has parent-and-child content around career-day features. The COPPA analysis is done property by property, and in some cases section by section within a property. I would not feel confident at any meaningful publisher that the answer is zero.

Now to what changed. Six substantive amendments, four of which I think matter most for a media company.

First. Expanded definition of personal information. The amended rule explicitly includes biometric identifiers and government-issued identifiers within the scope of personal information subject to COPPA. That matters because age-verification technologies increasingly use biometric signals — and now, if your age gate processes a child's biometric data, you have triggered COPPA obligations around the processing itself, separate from the question of whether the child ultimately registered. The February twenty-fifth policy statement provides some limited enforcement discretion for age-verification-specific use, but the policy statement is not a safe harbor. It is an enforcement-priorities document. Your legal exposure is still defined by the statute and the rule.

Second, and in my view most operationally consequential. Separate, verifiable parental consent is required before an operator can disclose a child's personal information to a third party for targeted advertising. General consent to data collection is not enough. That means, in practical terms, that the consent architecture most publishers have used — a single parental-consent step at the point of account creation, followed by default sharing with ad-tech intermediaries — no longer works. You need a specific, affirmative parental decision about third-party advertising. If your current flow does not isolate that decision, redesign it.

Third. Data retention limitation. Operators must retain children's personal information only for as long as is reasonably necessary to fulfill the specific purpose for which it was collected. No indefinite retention. No generic cross-purpose retention. If you have a five-year-old database of former child users of a contest, a summer program, or an interactive feature — and many publishers do — you have a deletion exercise ahead of you. That deletion exercise is also, helpfully, an enforcement exhibit: if the Commission opens a matter, your ability to show clean, documented, purpose-tied retention will matter.

Fourth. Written information security program. Operators must implement a written information security program reasonably designed to protect children's personal information, and must conduct an annual review. This one sounds administrative, but it is substantive. The requirement is for a written program, with designated responsibility, with an annual review documented. If you have never reduced your children's-data protections to a single written document, that document is this month's work. The Commission's historic enforcement pattern in programmatic failures — think the Designated Program Manager obligation under the Safeguards Rule — is that where the written program is absent, liability attaches regardless of whether actual harm occurred.

Two more I will cover briefly. Fifth, expanded safe harbor for schools. Educational technology providers operating under authorization from schools now have clearer pathways. Sixth, new notice requirements in privacy policies, including specific disclosures about third-party recipients and retention periods.

So. Four practical actions for an in-house GC at a publisher with any potential child-audience exposure.

One. Do a child-directed content audit, this quarter. Not a legal-opinion audit. A practical one. Ask product: which sections of which properties would a reasonable parent describe as, quote, for kids, end quote. Then ask engineering: on those sections, what personal information are we collecting, and to whom are we sending it. Compare. Gaps on that comparison are compliance exposure. The Disney settlement for ten million dollars in September of last year — which I did not cover at the time but which is now the baseline — turned on precisely this gap. Disney's child-directed YouTube videos were collecting personal data from child viewers without parental notice and consent.

Two. Audit your age-gate technology. If you use age verification, determine whether the vendor's technology collects or processes biometric signals, government-ID images, or other categories now explicitly within COPPA's scope. Your vendor contract almost certainly does not allocate COPPA liability to the vendor. Amend it or replace the vendor.

Three. Rebuild the parental-consent architecture. If your consent flow bundles third-party advertising consent into a single check-the-box, unbundle it. The separate, verifiable-parental-consent requirement is where the Commission will find its cleanest enforcement cases over the next twenty-four months. Do not be in that docket.

Four. Document. Write the information security program. Write the retention schedule. Write the privacy policy amendments. Write the annual review memo you will produce twelve months from now. The written record is the evidence. The Commission's Staff Guidance, which has been updated to reflect the amendments, will be the measuring stick. Build to that measuring stick.

One final observation on COPPA before we move off the topic. The political valence of children's privacy is unusual. Both the outgoing and the incoming administrations at the Commission have signaled strong enforcement priorities in this area. Commissioner Mark Meador has been explicit that children's safety online is, quote, an area where I am willing and eager, end quote, to bring enforcement actions. In an enforcement environment that has been retrenched on other privacy questions — the SolarWinds cybersecurity case was voluntarily dismissed, Reg S-K is being streamlined, the Cyber and Emerging Technologies Unit at the SEC is narrower than its predecessor — COPPA enforcement is likely to be one of the few areas where activity intensifies. If you are planning 2026 privacy budget, weight it accordingly.

[GC BENCH]

Now to GC Bench. Two items and a docket note.

The first item. A legal-technology reference point. Bloomberg Law's most recent in-house ops survey, which we touched on last week, flagged a datum I want to draw out. Among in-house teams that have deployed AI workflows in production, the single most cited productivity gain is not drafting. It is not research. It is intake triage — specifically, the ability to classify, prioritize, and assign inbound legal requests without human intervention up front. Sixty-one percent of the teams surveyed identified intake triage as the workflow they would automate next if resources allowed. I think the intake answer is correct, and I think the reason it is correct is underappreciated. Most of a GC's time is not spent doing legal work. It is spent deciding what is legal work, what is a business question that has been mislabeled as a legal question, and what is a vendor question that should be routed to procurement. If your intake process turns on a shared inbox and a tired General Counsel, you have a bottleneck that can now be automated. The tools for this are improving quickly. Lexis Protege, CoCounsel, Harvey, and several of the purpose-built in-house platforms — Ironclad's Jurist, Spellbook's intake modules — all have credible intake-classification modules as of this quarter. If you are evaluating legal-ops budget for the back half of the year, this is the item I would move up.

The second item. The ad-tech data layer is consolidating. On April fifteenth, The Trade Desk's acquisition of Sincera closed, and last month Sincera Technologies — a separate telecom-focused company with the same name — was acquired by Virtusa, a portfolio company of EQT. I flag these two transactions together not because they are substantively related — they are not — but because the pattern is instructive. The companies that provide ad-tech data quality and measurement infrastructure are being absorbed into larger platforms at an accelerating rate. From a legal perspective, that means the independence of measurement has operational questions attached to it. If your publisher agreements, or your advertiser agreements, reference any of these vendors as independent third-party verifiers, that independence claim is worth re-reading. This is not a GC emergency. It is a contract-review item for the next renewal cycle.

The docket note. The FTC consent order against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu, pending in the Northern District of Texas, is in its public-comment period. The window for the court to consider third-party comments is open through the end of this month. If your company has documentary evidence of having been categorically excluded from an ad plan under coordinated brand-safety criteria during the 2020 to 2024 window, the consent-order comment mechanism is one forum for that evidence to surface. A formal comment to the court is a public record and a litigation-preservation event. It is also a moderate undertaking. Do not file lightly. But do not let the window close without at minimum opening a file and asking whether you have evidence that would make filing worthwhile.
