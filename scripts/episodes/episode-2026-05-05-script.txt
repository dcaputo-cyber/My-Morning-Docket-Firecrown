[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up. Texas. Attorney General Ken Paxton announced the finalization of a one point three seven five billion dollar settlement with Google over claims that the company unlawfully tracked and collected the geolocation data, the incognito-mode searches, and the biometric identifiers of Texans. This is the largest single-state privacy recovery against Google ever obtained. To put that number in context, the multistate coalition of forty attorneys general that pursued similar claims settled for three hundred ninety-one million dollars. Texas alone, on its own statutes — the Texas Data Privacy and Security Act and related authorities — recovered nearly a billion dollars more than that combined coalition. Two takeaways for the general counsel of a media company. The Texas attorney general's office is now, on a per-statute basis, the most consequential privacy enforcer in the United States, ahead of the California Privacy Protection Agency on settlement size and very arguably ahead of the Federal Trade Commission. And the theory of recovery — undisclosed geolocation collection, undisclosed search retention, undisclosed biometric capture — is exactly the theory that applies to ad-tech vendors operating on a publisher's site. If your site fires a pixel that does any of those three things without notice, the Texas exposure is no longer hypothetical. It is one settlement old.

Second. The Federal Trade Commission's case against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu. On April fifteenth, the Commission, joined by the attorneys general of eight states — Florida, Indiana, Iowa, Montana, Nebraska, Texas, Utah, and West Virginia — filed suit in the Northern District of Texas, Fort Worth Division, alleging that the three holding companies, which together place a substantial fraction of all programmatic advertising in the United States, conspired beginning in 2018 to impose common brand-safety standards across the digital advertising industry. The mechanism the Commission identified was trade-association-led coordination on what the complaint calls a Brand Safety Floor. The named defendants entered a proposed consent order on the same day the complaint was filed. The order, if approved, permanently prohibits each of the three firms from entering into agreements that set common brand-safety standards or restrict advertising based on what the Commission characterizes as biased or politically motivated criteria. No monetary penalties were disclosed; the relief is behavioral. I'll spend more time on this in the deep dive.

Third. Bartz versus Anthropic. The fairness hearing in the largest copyright settlement in United States history is scheduled for May fourteenth at two o'clock Pacific time, in Courtroom Twelve of the San Francisco federal courthouse, before Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín. The settlement is one and a half billion dollars. Two numbers from the docket are worth knowing. Approximately one hundred twenty thousand authors filed claims by the deadline. And ninety-one point three percent of the eligible works in the certified class have been claimed — a participation rate that the Authors Guild calls roughly nine times the typical class action rate of around ten percent. What that means in practice is that the per-work payout will end up close to the originally projected three thousand dollars per work, rather than diluted across a smaller claim pool. Objectors have filed unsealed challenges, and class members may join the hearing by Zoom. The relevance for any publisher with a content library is direct. The Bartz settlement, combined with Judge Alsup's June 2025 ruling distinguishing lawfully acquired training data from pirated training data, establishes the floor for what a content owner can demand from a frontier-model developer. If you have ever found your archive in a dataset, the negotiating posture has changed.

Fourth. The California Privacy Protection Agency. The Agency is in an open preliminary comment period on data broker audits, which closes at five o'clock Pacific time this Thursday, May seventh. The comment topic is what the audit regime under the Delete Act will actually require — frequency, scope, the role of internal versus external auditors, and how the Agency will handle the data broker registry. The Agency has also been issuing enforcement decisions under the existing California Consumer Privacy Act regulations. In the most recent batch, the clothing retailer Todd Snyder, Inc. was assessed three hundred forty-five thousand one hundred seventy-eight dollars in fines and ordered to change its business practices. A separate respondent, Rickenbacher Data, doing business as Datamasters, was fined forty-five thousand dollars and ordered to stop selling Californians' personal information for failing to register as a data broker. If your media company monetizes audience data — and most of them do — the Delete Act registration question is not theoretical. It is the next enforcement front.

Fifth. The United Kingdom Information Commissioner's Office and the Data Use and Access Act. The Act came into force in stages beginning February fifth, 2026. The provisions still to commence include a mandatory complaints procedure, which becomes effective on June nineteenth. From that date, data subjects in the United Kingdom must first complain to the controller — the publisher, in our case — and may only escalate to the Information Commissioner's Office if the controller fails to respond, or responds inadequately, within a reasonable timeframe. That sounds procedural, and it is. But it has two operational consequences. Your data-protection inbox needs to be staffed and tracked, because the clock starts running on receipt. And the Information Commissioner's Office, under the same Act, has new enforcement powers under the Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations: the ability to compel witnesses to attend interviews, to require technical reports, and to issue fines of up to seventeen and a half million pounds, or four percent of global turnover. That is the GDPR ceiling, applied to cookie and tracking violations. If your United Kingdom cookie consent has been managed at the developer level rather than at the legal level, this is the year to fix that.

Sixth. The Securities and Exchange Commission. The comment period on Chairman Atkins's comprehensive review of Regulation S-K closed on April thirteenth. Over seventy unique comment letters were filed. Item Four Oh Two — executive compensation — is in the first wave of the review. The Commission is signaling a move toward a more principles-based, materiality-focused regime, with scaled requirements for smaller and newly public issuers. None of this affects 2026 proxy filings. But the parent company of any publicly traded media business should expect the executive-compensation tables, the Compensation Discussion and Analysis, and the related disclosures to look meaningfully different in the 2027 proxy season. If your finance counsel and your communications counsel haven't started the internal conversation about what a leaner Item Four Oh Two would mean for benchmarking and for shareholder engagement, that conversation should start this quarter.

A side note on subscription publishing, because the numbers are worth carrying around. The New York Times closed the first quarter at over twelve million digital subscribers. Mail Plus, in the United Kingdom, hit four hundred thousand paying subscribers on April second. The Reuters Institute's most recent journalism-trends report has subscriptions and memberships at seventy-six percent of publishers' revenue focus, ahead of display at sixty-eight percent and native at sixty-four percent. The pure metered model — the universally-applied free-articles-per-month approach that defined the 2010s — has fallen from thirty-five percent of publishers to nine percent. The trend is unambiguous: hard paywalls and dynamic, data-driven hybrid paywalls have taken over the category. The legal-side implication, for a general counsel, is that the consumer-protection footprint of a publisher's subscription product is now larger than the consumer-protection footprint of its advertising product. State attorney general enforcement on subscription cancellation, on negative-option marketing, and on automatic renewal disclosures — including the FTC's Click-to-Cancel posture and California's Automatic Renewal Law — is now the highest-volume consumer-side risk a publisher carries.

[DEEP DIVE]

The deep dive this morning is the Brand Safety Floor case. I want to give you the structure, the legal theory, the open questions, and three concrete actions for a media-company general counsel.

The structure first. On April fifteenth, the Federal Trade Commission filed a complaint, captioned as case number four twenty-six civil four sixty-nine in the Northern District of Texas, Fort Worth Division, against three defendants: GroupM Worldwide, which is the media-buying arm of WPP; Publicis, Inc.; and Dentsu U.S., Inc. Eight state attorneys general joined as co-plaintiffs. The complaint pleads two counts: a violation of Section One of the Sherman Act, and a violation of Section Five of the Federal Trade Commission Act. The conduct alleged is a horizontal agreement among competitors — the holding companies, plus their primary competitors Omnicom and Interpublic, who are not named as defendants — to adopt a common Brand Safety Floor for digital advertising. The mechanism, according to the complaint, was the Global Alliance for Responsible Media, hosted by the World Federation of Advertisers, and downstream coordination through the Interactive Advertising Bureau and adjacent trade bodies. The complaint alleges that the agreement excluded specific publishers from being eligible for the buying clients' advertising spend, that the exclusion was based in part on what the Commission characterizes as politically motivated criteria, and that the exclusion deprived those publishers of advertising revenue.

On the same day the complaint was filed, the three named defendants entered into a proposed consent order. That is significant. The order, if approved, runs for twenty years. It permanently prohibits the three firms from entering into agreements with competitors that set common brand-safety standards. It permanently prohibits the firms from restricting advertising based on what the order calls biased or politically motivated criteria. It does not impose monetary penalties. It does require ongoing compliance reporting to the Commission.

Now the legal theory, because this is where the case is interesting and where the open questions live. The Sherman Section One count is a horizontal-restraint case. Horizontal restraints among competitors are usually analyzed under the rule of reason, but where the agreement targets specific competitors of the buyers — that is, where buyers collude to exclude particular sellers — courts have sometimes treated the conduct as closer to a per-se group boycott. The complaint does not commit to one theory or the other; the consent order resolves it without a full trial. The Section Five count is broader. It captures conduct that is unfair, even if it does not technically violate the Sherman Act. The Commission has been more aggressive on Section Five under recent leadership, and this case is a textbook application of the post-2022 Section Five Statement of Enforcement Principles — that is, conduct that suppresses competition for non-competitive reasons.

Now the open questions. There are three.

The first is the status of the trade-association coordination itself. The complaint names the Global Alliance for Responsible Media and the Interactive Advertising Bureau as the venues through which the coordination occurred, but it does not name them as defendants. It does not need to, in order to obtain the consent order. But the publisher community should expect follow-on private litigation — by publishers who can show concrete revenue loss from being categorized below the Floor — and that follow-on litigation will likely name the trade associations.

The second is the status of the Floor itself, going forward. The consent order prohibits the named defendants from agreeing to a common standard. It does not, on its face, prohibit individual buying agencies from setting their own standards, applied unilaterally, on behalf of an individual advertiser. The line between unilateral application of advertiser preferences and coordinated industry-wide application of a Floor is a line that the agencies will now have to learn to walk. Expect short-term confusion, and expect agencies to over-correct toward unilateralism.

The third is the spillover. Two of the largest holding companies — Omnicom and Interpublic — were not named as defendants. The Commission has not publicly explained why. One reading is that the evidence against the named three was simply stronger. Another reading is that Omnicom and Interpublic remain under investigation. A third reading is that the merger between Omnicom and Interpublic, which has been pending, may be on a separate track at the Commission. None of these readings is definitive. A general counsel of a publishing company should not assume the case is over.

And now the three concrete actions for the general counsel of a media company.

Action one. Pull the brand-safety exclusion lists that have been applied to your inventory by the named agencies and by their competitors over the past five years. Your sales team can get them; your ad ops team can get them. If you have inventory that has been categorized as falling below a Brand Safety Floor — and you have lost advertising revenue as a result — you have a potential follow-on damages claim. The statute of limitations on Sherman Act claims is four years from the date of injury, with the discovery rule potentially extending that. A demand letter, even one that does not lead to litigation, is now a reasonable first move.

Action two. Update your sales contracts and your inventory representations. Any contract clause that promises advertisers compliance with industry-standard brand-safety frameworks needs to be revisited. The framework is now under federal antitrust scrutiny. Your contracts should not commit your sales side to a standard that the Commission has just sued the buy side for coordinating on.

Action three. Refresh your editorial-side documentation. The strongest defense against being categorized below any unilateral brand-safety standard is documented editorial process. Plead, in writing, the steps your newsroom takes to verify, source, and correct. That documentation has always been valuable; in a post-Brand-Safety-Floor world, it is the principal asset that distinguishes your inventory from the inventory of a publisher whose process is opaque.

One additional note on framing. The case has been characterized in the political press as a partisan win, because the named co-plaintiff state attorneys general are predominantly from one side of the aisle, and because the complaint's reference to politically motivated criteria has been read as a coded reference to coverage decisions about specific topics. That framing is not wrong, but it is not the operative framing for an in-house counsel. The operative framing is that horizontal coordination among buyers to exclude particular sellers is, and always has been, an antitrust concern. The political valence of the excluded sellers does not change the analysis. A general counsel who walks into a board meeting and frames this as a culture-war story is going to get a culture-war response. A general counsel who frames it as a competition-law story — as the Federal Trade Commission framed it in the complaint, count one of which is a Sherman Act count — is going to get a more useful response, and is going to be in a better position to make the case for action items one through three.

[GC BENCH]

A short note on the in-house tooling environment. Three surveys landed in the past sixty days, and the numbers tell the same story. The Association of Corporate Counsel and Everlaw, surveying six hundred fifty-seven in-house counsel across thirty countries, found that generative artificial intelligence adoption among United States corporate legal departments rose from twenty-three percent to fifty-two percent in a single year. FTI Consulting and Relativity, in their general-counsel report, put the number higher: eighty-seven percent of general counsel are now using artificial intelligence, up from forty-four percent the year before. LegalOn and In-House Connect, surveying a smaller pool of four hundred fifty-two practitioners, found that fifty-two percent of in-house teams are using or evaluating artificial intelligence specifically for contract review, with active use nearly quadrupling since 2024. The directional message across all three is that the question has shifted from whether to use the tools to which use cases produce defensible work product. For a media-company in-house team, the highest-leverage use cases right now are first-pass review of inbound nondisclosure agreements, first-pass review of advertising insertion orders against a master agreement, and triage of data-subject access requests under the state privacy laws and, increasingly, the Data Use and Access Act. The lowest-leverage and highest-risk use case continues to be unsupervised drafting of substantive memos that touch on outside counsel privilege.

[INDUSTRY ANGLE]

Now the freight read. Craig Fuller, the founder and chief executive of FreightWaves, has been on the air over the past two weeks calling the freight recession over. The data point he keeps returning to is the truckload tender rejection rate, which has been running near twelve point seven percent — a level not seen in the post-pandemic period. June, by Fuller's read, is on track to be the largest freight month of the year, with retail, produce, construction, and industrial production all peaking in the same window. There is a near-term overlay. The Commercial Vehicle Safety Alliance International Roadcheck — the annual three-day enforcement blitz across the United States, Canada, and Mexico — is expected to pull additional capacity off the road, and Fuller is predicting rejection rates spike into the sixteen-to-seventeen percent range during the enforcement window. For media companies that buy or sell freight-adjacent advertising, the practical implication is that ad-buying patterns out of the trucking, retail-supply-chain, and industrial sectors will reflect a tighter market through summer. The longer-term overlay, which Fuller is also calling, is broker liability and compliance enforcement — what he expects to be the dominant freight-legal story of summer 2026. The relevance for a media company general counsel is narrower but real: any FreightWaves, SONAR, or American Shipper coverage of broker liability will draw a more engaged audience than usual, and the editorial-side calls on what the company can and cannot publish about pending matters will be sharper than they were a quarter ago.

Two adjacent industry signals worth carrying. The Federal Aviation Administration spent April issuing a series of airworthiness directives against Boeing seven eighty-seven aircraft. One was prompted by reports of missing cargo-liner joint sealing tape that could allow a fire to propagate from the cargo hold into the cabin. A second was prompted by reports that sealant installations within the center wing fuel tank had not received the required visual and adhesion inspections during production. A third was prompted by reports of in-flight cabin and flight-deck temperatures the flight crew could not control. The comment periods on all three closed in the first half of April. The directives are a reminder that the post-Alaska-door-plug regulatory environment around Boeing has not relaxed. For aviation publishers — and for FLYING magazine, in particular — the editorial calendar is thick with material, and the legal-side advice to the newsroom is the same advice it has been since 2024: every story about a Boeing airworthiness directive needs to cite the docket number and quote from the public Federal Register text, not from a press summary, because the press summaries are routinely wrong on the scope of the affected fleet.

On the marine side, container rates on the Far-East-to-Mediterranean fronthaul came down twenty-five percent on long-term contracts entering validity in the first quarter, settling near two thousand three hundred dollars per forty-foot equivalent. Rates into North Europe came down ten percent. The pricing reflects the tentative return of Red Sea routings and a rebalancing of capacity that had been forced south around the Cape of Good Hope. The Panama Canal recovery from the 2024 drought has held; fiscal-year 2025 transits ran approximately nineteen percent above the prior year. For Boating and Marine editorial, the practical takeaway is that supply-chain copy needs to be refreshed for the new routing baseline. The legal-side concern is narrower: any subscription product that quotes specific shipping rates as recurring data needs to align with the underlying licensing agreements with the rate providers, because the providers are policing their data more aggressively than they were in 2024.
