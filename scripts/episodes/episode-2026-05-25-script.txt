[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. The Federal Trade Commission announced on Thursday afternoon, May twenty-first, a settlement with Cox Media Group, doing business as CMG Media Corporation, and with two smaller marketing vendors — MindSift, of New Hampshire, and 1010 Digital Works, of Wisconsin — over what the Commission characterized as deceptive marketing claims regarding an active-listening artificial-intelligence service. The Commission's complaint, filed concurrently with the settlement, alleged that the three companies represented to customers that they operated proprietary technology capable of detecting consumer conversations through smart-device microphones, applying a localization algorithm, and serving geographically targeted advertising on the basis of the captured audio. The Commission alleged that no such capability existed. The actual service, according to the Commission, was the resale of email lists purchased from third-party data brokers, at a markup, to advertisers who had been told a different story. The total monetary penalty is nine hundred thirty thousand dollars. Cox Media Group will pay eight hundred eighty thousand. MindSift and 1010 Digital each pay twenty-five thousand. The settlement order bars all three respondents from misrepresenting the features of any marketing service, from claiming the capability to collect voice data, and from claiming geographically tailored audience-targeting capabilities they cannot substantiate. The operational signal for in-house counsel at any publisher or marketing operation is in the specificity of the prohibition. The Commission is not adjacent to ad-tech marketing-claim enforcement any more. It is squarely inside it. The Commission's April action against the major media-buying agencies — the Weber-Publicis-Dentsu coordinated-brand-safety matter — was the structural case. The Cox Media Group matter is the substantive-misrepresentation case. The pattern across both is the Commission's growing willingness to treat the marketing claims an ad-tech vendor makes to its advertiser-customers as a federal consumer-protection matter, even when the consumer harm is mediated through the advertiser relationship rather than direct. The contractual flow-down language between a media company and any ad-tech vendor it engages — the warranties, the substantiation requirements, the audit rights — is the document the Commission's settlement effectively redrafts.

Second. The legacy-newsroom restructuring cycle continues. National Public Radio announced on Monday, May eighteenth, a voluntary-buyout program for up to thirty positions across its newsroom, with the deadline for voluntary elections set for Tuesday, May twenty-sixth — tomorrow. The organization has signaled that involuntary reductions will follow if voluntary elections do not reach the target. Columbia Broadcasting System News confirmed last Friday, the twenty-second, the closure of CBS News Radio after ninety-nine years of continuous operation, with affected positions across the network's radio-news bureaus. The closure was an immediate effective-date action, not a wind-down. The Daily Wire announced earlier in the month a restructuring affecting a portion of its editorial and production teams. Business Insider executed what is now its fourth distinct reduction in four years. The cumulative effect across the legacy and the digital-native categories is the most material change to the American newsroom labor market in a single quarter since the pandemic-era compression of 2020 and early 2021. The aggregate first-quarter number — more than five hundred eliminated newsroom positions across the tracked publishers — is on a pace that, if it holds through the back half of the year, exceeds the most aggressive prior projections. The deep dive will return to this story. The headline-level signal worth carrying this morning is that newsroom restructurings have moved from a story about the financial press into the regulatory and operational story of the second quarter.

Third. The Texas Attorney General's office confirmed on Wednesday, the twentieth, the opening of an investigative inquiry into Meta Platforms over the data-collection practices of its Ray-Ban-branded smart-glasses product. The inquiry was framed, in the Attorney General's public statement, as a biometric-privacy matter under the Texas Capture or Use of Biometric Identifier Act and as a children's-privacy matter under the Securing Children Online through Parental Empowerment Act. The Attorney General's office has not formally characterized the inquiry as an enforcement action. The cure-notice posture of the Texas Data Privacy and Security Act framework allows the Attorney General to issue thirty-day cure notices in lieu of immediate enforcement, and any media company that operates connected-device or wearable-product surfaces and that captures biometric or near-biometric data should treat the announcement as a signal that the Texas Attorney General's biometric portfolio is moving from the social-media adjacency into the consumer-product adjacency. The portfolio's reach into wearable devices is the second-quarter expansion of an enforcement program that, until recently, was concentrated on the social-media platforms themselves.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning runs longer than usual. The federal holiday gives us the room to examine something the daily news cycle does not allow us to examine carefully enough. The topic is the structural compression of the American newsroom, the wave of restructurings executed or announced in the first five months of 2026, and the legal-operations work that has now become a recurring and substantial line item on the in-house-counsel calendar at every media organization of meaningful scale.

I want to start with the data. The aggregate journalist-position elimination count across the tracked publishers — print, digital, broadcast, podcast — for the first quarter of 2026 exceeded five hundred. The Washington Post, in late January and through February, eliminated more than three hundred journalist positions, on the order of one-third of its eight-hundred-person newsroom. Columbia Broadcasting System News executed a reduction of approximately six percent of its eleven-hundred-person workforce in late March — more than sixty positions — and confirmed last Friday the closure of its ninety-nine-year-old radio-news service. National Public Radio is in the middle of a voluntary-buyout program for up to thirty positions, with the voluntary-election deadline tomorrow and involuntary reductions threatened to follow if the voluntary number is insufficient. The Daily Wire executed a restructuring earlier in May. Business Insider executed its fourth reduction in four years. Vox Media, Bustle Digital, BuzzFeed legacy properties, and Gannett's local-newspaper portfolio all executed reductions earlier in the year. The cumulative effect, projected forward at first-quarter pace, would exceed the record-setting year of 2024. The projected forward pace may, however, understate the second-quarter number, because the second-quarter cycle is structurally different from the first-quarter cycle. The first-quarter reductions were responses to fourth-quarter 2025 advertising results and to the 2026 budget cycle. The second-quarter reductions are responses to the first-quarter 2026 results and to the artificial-intelligence-driven traffic compression that the major search platforms and the answer-engine platforms have been progressively rolling out since the back half of 2025. The structural driver of the second-quarter reductions is qualitatively different from the structural driver of the first-quarter reductions, and the legal-operations work is correspondingly different.

I want to take the legal-operations work in five categories, because the work product divides cleanly along functional lines, and each function deserves separate attention.

The first category is the Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act analysis — what the federal statute and most of the state-law analogues call the WARN-and-state-WARN regime. The federal WARN Act covers employers of one hundred or more employees and requires sixty days of advance notice for a plant closing affecting fifty or more employees at a single site, or for a mass layoff affecting one-third of the workforce at a single site, or two-hundred-fifty or more employees regardless of percentage. California, New York, Illinois, and New Jersey impose more aggressive state-level requirements with broader covered-employer thresholds, shorter notice exceptions, and in some cases per-violation civil penalties that can stack with the federal exposure. For a multi-location publisher, the WARN-and-state-WARN analysis is the first analysis. The single-site definitional question — what constitutes a single site of employment under the federal regulations, and how to treat newsroom-wide reductions that span multiple physical bureaus — is the question on which the federal-court litigation is concentrated. The current operational posture, as the second-quarter cycle accelerates, is that the WARN analysis should be the document that runs in parallel with the restructuring plan, not the document the human-resources team produces after the restructuring plan is finalized. Documentation of the single-site analysis, contemporaneous with the workforce-reduction decision, is the principal defense against the wrongful-termination-of-notice litigation that the plaintiffs' bar has been pursuing more aggressively in 2026 than at any prior point in the WARN history.

The second category is the severance-program design. The structural shift in 2026 is the shift from individual-negotiated severance packages to standardized voluntary-departure programs with consideration calibrated to length of service, to position, and to release-and-non-disparagement architecture. The voluntary-departure program is the dominant tool. The reason it is the dominant tool is that the standardized program produces a release matrix that is enforceable across the affected population in a way that individual negotiation does not. The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act overlay on any program that affects employees over the age of forty — the twenty-one-day review period for individual releases, the forty-five-day review period for group releases under the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission disclosure regime, the seven-day revocation period, the disclosure requirements at the time of the offer — is the technical-compliance component that the in-house team operates under the Office of General Counsel and that the firm-level employment-law practice executes against. The new development in 2026 is the increased frequency of post-program release-validity litigation, in which a former employee — typically represented by the same plaintiffs' firm that has been active in the WARN-Act bar — challenges the validity of the release on the basis of inadequate disclosure under the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's forty-five-day-window rules. The cure is procedural and documentary. The cure is also expensive if the procedural and documentary work has not been done before the release is signed.

The third category is the intellectual-property-and-asset-recovery work product. This is the category that the in-house bar in 2026 is, in my observation across the briefings I have been reading, under-resourced for. The departing journalist is, in most media-company employment-agreement architectures, a contributor of intellectual property — the work-for-hire framework applies, the company owns the copyright in published work, the company owns the trademark in any branded vertical the journalist has developed. The contributor is also, in many cases, the holder of a substantial archive of unpublished work product — interview transcripts, source notes, draft material, photographic and audio assets. The retention of that archive is, under most well-drafted employment agreements, a company asset. The recovery of that archive at the time of the journalist's departure is, in practice, a process that requires active legal-operations attention. The journalist who walks out the door with the unpublished source notes is, depending on the source-confidentiality agreements made with the source, a contractual-liability problem and a potential litigation problem. The recovery of company-owned intellectual property at the time of departure is a checklist item. The checklist needs to exist. The checklist needs to be applied. The checklist needs to be documented. The cumulative cost of failing to do the recovery work at scale, across a three-hundred-position reduction, is the cost the in-house team is least able to recover after the fact.

The fourth category is the defamation-and-editorial-liability transition. The departing journalist is, with respect to her body of published work for the publication, a contingent litigation risk that follows the work product into perpetuity. The publication remains the defendant of record. The journalist, if she has departed, is a non-party witness whose cooperation the publication needs to secure through means short of an employment relationship. The well-drafted separation agreement includes a post-employment cooperation provision — the former journalist agrees, in consideration for the severance payment, to make herself available for testimony and for document discovery in any litigation arising from the work product produced during the employment. The cooperation provision is a standard provision in the major-publisher template. The cooperation provision is sometimes omitted in the standardized voluntary-departure-program release language because the standardized language was drafted for a non-journalist position. The omission is a problem worth catching in the review cycle, before the program goes live. The defamation-insurance overlay — the question of whether the publication's editorial-liability policy continues to cover work produced during the employment after the journalist has departed — is the second-order question, and the policy-by-policy answer is generally yes, but the answer is policy-by-policy.

The fifth category, and the one I want to spend the most time on, is the question of the work product that survives the reduction and the question of how the in-house counsel preserves the institutional knowledge — the source relationships, the editorial standards, the verification-and-fact-checking practice — that the departing positions take with them. This is not a legal-compliance question in the narrow sense. It is, however, a legal-operations question in a meaningful sense, because the post-reduction defamation exposure of a thinner newsroom is, on average, higher than the pre-reduction exposure of the fuller newsroom. The reasons are mechanical. The fact-checking layer is thinner. The editorial-review layer is thinner. The institutional-memory layer about source reliability and about the historical reasons for a particular editorial standard is thinner. The publication that has gone from a fuller newsroom to a thinner one is, in the year that follows, exposed to a higher rate of plausible defamation claims. The in-house counsel's work product in the months after a substantial reduction includes, or should include, a refreshed editorial-standards review, a refreshed source-verification practice document, and a refreshed fact-checking-protocol document — produced in coordination with the editorial leadership but archived under the legal department's document-management system. The work product is the operational defense against the defamation exposure the reduction produces.

Three concrete actions for an in-house counsel at a media company that has executed, or is contemplating, a workforce reduction in the second quarter. First, audit the WARN-and-state-WARN compliance posture against the contemplated reduction. The audit should be documented. The single-site analysis should be the principal document. The analysis should be in the file before the announcement, not after. Second, audit the standardized voluntary-departure-program release language against the journalist-specific provisions — the cooperation provision, the intellectual-property-recovery provision, the post-employment confidentiality provision, the source-archive-recovery provision. The standardized language was drafted for a different employee category. The journalist-specific provisions need to be added before the program goes live. Third, schedule a refreshed editorial-standards-and-defamation-exposure review for the back half of the year, with the editorial leadership, the in-house team, and the editorial-liability-insurance broker in the room. The post-reduction defamation exposure is the second-order consequence of the reduction. The first-order consequence — the cost reduction — is the consequence the chief financial officer has already calculated. The second-order consequence is the consequence the in-house team is positioned to manage, and the absence of which from the second-half calendar is a strategic omission worth correcting this week.

A closing observation on the cycle. The compression of the American newsroom in 2026 is not, in my reading of the data, a cyclical compression. The structural drivers — the answer-engine compression of search-referral traffic to publisher properties, the consolidation of audience attention onto a smaller set of subscription-supported titles, the durable reduction in display-advertising rates — are not cyclical drivers. The implication is that the legal-operations work I have described is not the work product of a single quarter. It is the work product of an ongoing operational rhythm at any media company of meaningful scale. The in-house team that builds the playbook, the templates, the documentation discipline, and the cross-functional coordination during the current cycle is the in-house team that has institutional infrastructure in place when the next cycle arrives.

[GC BENCH]
On GC Bench this morning, a brief note on a piece of legal-technology pricing news that landed Friday afternoon. The principal frontier-model vendor's enterprise-legal product team released, on Friday, the twenty-second, a revised pricing structure for its in-house-counsel and law-firm offerings. The structure moves from a per-seat licensing model to a hybrid per-seat-and-per-action consumption model, in which the workflow-agent and document-review actions are metered, and the base per-seat license is reduced to a lower entry point. The pricing change is, on the surface, a routine vendor announcement. The structural significance for an in-house technology budget is that the budgeting exercise for the back half of 2026 — the exercise that closes in September against a January start — is now structurally different from the budgeting exercise that closed last September. The static-cost-of-seats model is over. The variable-cost-of-actions model is the new baseline. The procurement question for the in-house technology team is the question of which legal-operations workflows are high-volume and predictable, which are episodic and unpredictable, and how to architect the consumption envelope around the predictable-versus-episodic distinction. The vendor that moves first sets the pricing template. The competing vendors have, in my observation of the announcements that have followed Friday's release, signaled that they will match the structure during the third quarter. The procurement conversation worth having between the in-house team and the chief financial officer during the second-half budget cycle is the conversation about which workflows the in-house team will commit to and which it will leave variable. The conversation is also the conversation with the panel firms about which work moves in-house against the new pricing and which work continues to be sent out. The panel firms that have moved their internal staffing to anticipate the in-house consumption-pricing shift are positioned. The panel firms that have not are exposed.
