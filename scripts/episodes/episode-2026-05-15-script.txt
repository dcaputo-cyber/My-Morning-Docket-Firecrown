First. Bartz versus Anthropic. The fairness hearing before Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín in the Northern District of California convened at two o'clock Pacific yesterday and ran for the balance of the afternoon. The hearing was carried on Zoom for class members and on the court's audio line for the public. The record reflects roughly one hundred twenty thousand author claimants, a ninety-one-point-three percent participation rate against the four-hundred-eighty-two-thousand-four-hundred-sixty work list, and a base-payout estimate that has held at approximately two-thousand-nine-hundred-thirty-one dollars per claimable work. The substantive objections that the court signaled it would weigh — the registration-collapsing methodology that compresses group-registered works into a single claim, the publisher-author split where some publishers have proposed taking the bulk of recoveries, and the precedent objection that a settlement at this multiple is too cheap relative to the conduct alleged — were aired in open court. The judge did not rule from the bench. The matter is under submission. For any media-company general counsel with content in the affected dataset corpora, the operational posture is unchanged this morning. The Bartz number remains the floor reference point for content-owner negotiation with any frontier-model developer, and the per-work payout remains the most concrete data point on the table. If the court approves, the floor hardens. If the court modifies or defers, the floor moves, but the direction of the negotiation does not. Plan against the existing posture.

Second. The Federal Trade Commission and Kochava. On May fourth, the Commission filed a proposed stipulated final order in the District of Idaho resolving the four-year-old action against Kochava and its data-broker subsidiary Collective Data Solutions. The order, which has now been formally noticed and is awaiting court approval, prohibits the companies from selling, licensing, transferring, sharing, or disclosing sensitive location data without the consumer's affirmative express consent. Sensitive location, under the order, is defined to include travel to medical facilities, places of worship, schools, places of work, places where domestic-violence shelters operate, and a long list of comparable categories. The Commission vote was two to nothing. The relevant question for a publisher is not whether your business looks like Kochava — most publisher businesses do not — but whether any of the third-party measurement, attribution, or audience-extension vendors on your site fire pixels that capture location data at that granularity. The Kochava order is the operational definition the Commission will now apply across the data-broker ecosystem and to any downstream business that purchases data from it. The compliance question to take to your vendor management team this morning is whether your ad-tech contracts already disclaim sensitive-location data and whether you have a downstream audit right.

Third. Thomson Reuters versus Ross Intelligence. The case is on appeal to the Third Circuit after Judge Stephanos Bibas's February 2025 ruling rejecting Ross's fair-use defense to the use of Westlaw headnotes in training a competitor legal-research product. As of Wednesday, both parties have filed supplemental briefing citing developments downstream of Judge Alsup's June 2025 Bartz summary-judgment ruling. Thomson Reuters argues that Judge Alsup's reasoning — that using a proprietary system to build a competing tool is not transformative — applies directly to Ross's use of Westlaw. Ross argues, in essence, that Bartz is doctrinally inapt because Anthropic's use was for training a foundation model with general capabilities, not a competing legal-research product. The Third Circuit panel has set oral argument for the fall calendar. The reason this matters for a media company is that Thomson Reuters versus Ross is, after Bartz, the second federal court of appeals to weigh in on the central fair-use question in artificial-intelligence training. The Second Circuit will eventually weigh in too — there are five active appeals in the pipeline — but the Third Circuit will be first. The opinion will carry persuasive weight in every parallel matter, including Disney and Universal against Midjourney, the New York Times against OpenAI, and the consolidated proceedings against Meta and Google. The brief schedule is the schedule to watch.

Fourth. The California Privacy Protection Agency. The Agency announced two enforcement resolutions in the past ten days that warrant carrying around. American Honda Motor Company paid six-hundred-thirty-two-thousand-five-hundred dollars and entered a stipulated order to change its business practices on consumer rights-request handling. The substantive defect, in plain language, was a verification process that imposed disproportionate identity-verification burdens on the consumer relative to the sensitivity of the rights request — which the Agency has now signaled is a recurring violation category. Separately, Tractor Supply Company paid one-million-three-hundred-fifty-thousand dollars and entered a stipulated order on a parallel theory. The rural-lifestyle retailer is now the largest single-respondent fine in the Agency's enforcement history other than the Disney and ABC matter we covered in February. The pattern across these two orders is consistent with the pattern across the Honda, Ford, PlayOn Sports, and Todd Snyder orders earlier in the year: the Agency is looking at the friction in the rights-request workflow, and it is treating friction as the violation. If your subject-access-request process requires a copy of a government-issued identification document, a notarized affidavit, or any equivalent friction layer that is not strictly proportionate to the data being requested, you are operating in a category the Agency is now actively enforcing against. The procedural review is worth running before the end of this quarter.

Fifth. Connecticut. The amendments to the Connecticut Data Privacy Act enacted as Senate Bill Twelve-Ninety-Five in June 2025 take effect on July first, forty-seven days from this morning. The provisions worth carrying. First, a categorical prohibition on processing minors' personal data for targeted advertising or sale, with the protected age range extended from sixteen to seventeen — that is, the prohibition now applies through the day before the consumer's eighteenth birthday. Second, the prohibition operates regardless of consent. Third, a separate prohibition on the use of system-design features that significantly increase, sustain, or extend the minor's use of the service. That third element is the engagement-pattern provision, and it tracks the architectural concerns the Federal Trade Commission and several state attorneys general have raised about social-media product design over the past three years. The applicability threshold is unchanged. Two-thirds of the major publisher properties operating in Connecticut will be within scope. If your Connecticut-resident audience reaches under-eighteens — and for any property with a youth-adjacent vertical, it almost certainly does — the targeted-advertising revenue from that cohort becomes legally unrecoverable on July first. The conversation between sales, ad operations, and legal should have closed by the end of next week.

Sixth. The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act. The Commission's enforcement powers commence on August second, seventy-nine days from this morning. The general-purpose artificial-intelligence Code of Practice — the voluntary instrument the Commission has signaled it will use as the presumption-of-compliance framework for providers — closed its second-round consultation in March, and the final version of the Code is expected to be published before the end of June. Separately, the Code of Practice on the marking and labeling of artificial-intelligence-generated content, implementing Article Fifty, is in stakeholder consultation now with a target publication date later this year. For any media company that operates in the European Union and deploys generative artificial intelligence in a content workflow — even a captioning workflow, even an image-resize workflow — the contracting question to put to your vendor before August second is whether the vendor will certify compliance with Article Fifty's marking and disclosure obligations, and whether the vendor will indemnify against enforcement risk arising from a failure of that compliance. If your contracting language does not address the August second commencement, this is the calendar to fix it on.

[DEEP DIVE]

The deep dive this morning is the Court of Justice of the European Union's ruling on Tuesday, May twelfth, in the Meta versus AGCOM matter. I want to give you the architecture of the ruling, the three operational consequences for a publisher, and three concrete actions for a general counsel.

The architecture first. The 2019 Digital Single Market Directive established, at the European Union level, a neighboring right for press publishers in their press publications — a right of remuneration for the use of those publications by online platforms. The Directive required member states to transpose the right into national law. Italy transposed in 2021. The Italian transposition vested AGCOM, the Italian communications regulator, with the authority to set procedural rules for the negotiation between press publishers and platforms; to obtain traffic and advertising-revenue data from platforms necessary to value the use of the publishers' content; to intervene as an arbitrator in stuck negotiations; and to fine platforms that did not comply with its orders. Meta — the parent of Facebook and Instagram — challenged the Italian transposition in the administrative tribunal in Rome, arguing that the Italian rules went beyond what the Directive permitted, that they imposed an obligation on the platforms inconsistent with European Union copyright law, and that the data-disclosure requirements were disproportionate. The administrative tribunal referred the matter to the Court of Justice for a preliminary ruling on the compatibility of the Italian regime with European Union law.

The Court ruled on Tuesday. The ruling, in summary. The Court found that the Digital Single Market Directive does not prohibit member states from establishing a procedural framework that empowers a national regulator to facilitate negotiation between press publishers and platforms, to compel platforms to disclose the data necessary to compute fair remuneration, and to impose sanctions for non-compliance with that framework. The Court found that the publishers' practical disadvantage in negotiating fair remuneration — the asymmetry of information between a platform that knows the engagement value of a snippet and a publisher that does not — justifies a procedural intervention by national law. The Court found that the data-disclosure requirements are proportionate to the publishers' legitimate need for the data, and that the platform's commercial confidentiality is adequately protected by the regulator's procedural rules. And the Court returned the matter to the Italian tribunal for application of those principles to the facts on the ground.

That is the ruling. Now the three operational consequences.

First. The procedural framework that Italy established is now confirmed at the European Union level. France enacted a similar framework in 2019. Germany has been moving in the same direction. Spain has begun consultations. The Court's ruling removes the most plausible legal challenge a platform could mount against any of these national-level frameworks. The likely result is that every member state with a substantive press-publisher constituency will, within twelve to twenty-four months, have a national regulator empowered to intervene in publisher-platform negotiations on roughly the Italian or French model. That is a structural change in the architecture of European news monetization.

Second. The Italian regime is, on its substantive structure, asymmetric — it imposes obligations on platforms, and it confers rights on publishers, and it equips the regulator with the tools to enforce that asymmetry. The directionality matters. A general counsel operating in Europe will, going forward, work in a regulatory environment that presumes platforms must compensate publishers, that the level of compensation is determinable, and that the regulator has the data-collection authority to determine it. The negotiating posture, for a publisher operating in any of the affected member states, has been meaningfully strengthened. The negotiating posture, for a platform, has been meaningfully constrained.

Third. The data-disclosure obligation — the requirement that the platform disclose traffic data, engagement metrics, and advertising-revenue data attributable to the publisher's content — is the operational lever. The Court's reasoning treats this as the lever because it is the input to the valuation. The valuation drives the compensation. The compensation drives the economic effect. For a publisher general counsel, the implication is that the data your business has long wanted from platforms — what engagement does my content drive, what advertising revenue does the platform monetize against it, what is the actual contribution of my content to the platform's revenue — is now a question the regulator can compel an answer to. That is a meaningfully different posture than the one publishers have operated in for the past fifteen years.

Now the three concrete actions for a media-company general counsel.

First. If you have European operations of any scale, the European-team legal calendar for the second half of this year should include a formal review of the neighboring-rights position across each member state where you operate. The Italian regime is now stable. The French regime is now stable. Germany, Spain, the Netherlands, and the Nordic countries are moving on shorter timelines. Your business teams should be in conversation with the national press-publisher associations in each of those countries about collective-bargaining frameworks, and your legal team should be aligned with that conversation on which national regulator is the relevant counterparty.

Second. The data-disclosure provisions are the operational lever. The regulators will, over the next eighteen months, develop the metrics they will require platforms to disclose. Your legal and business teams should be in those rulemakings. The rulemakings are the place where the operational shape of the compensation regime is determined. The rulemakings are not technical. They are the substance.

Third. The asymmetry of the regime is now confirmed. A publisher operating in Europe should not be in a contractual posture, on its content-licensing agreements with platforms, that disadvantages it relative to the regulatory baseline. If your existing content-licensing agreements with European platforms predate the national transpositions of the Directive, those agreements should be in renegotiation. The Court's ruling gives you the leverage. The regulator gives you the procedural support. Use both.

One additional note on transmission. The Italian regime, the French regime, and the emerging German regime are not equivalent in their substantive detail, even where they are equivalent in their architectural posture. The Italian regulator's procedural rules differ from the French regulator's procedural rules on the definition of a press publication, on the treatment of news aggregators that are not social platforms, and on the data-disclosure mechanics. A general counsel running a multi-jurisdictional European operation should expect to manage the negotiation country by country for at least the next two compliance cycles. The European Commission has not signaled an interest in harmonizing the procedural framework across member states. The Court of Justice's ruling does not require harmonization. The competing-jurisdiction reality is the reality you operate in.

[GC BENCH]

The legal-technology item this morning is a follow-on to the in-house artificial-intelligence adoption work I covered ten days ago. A second tranche of data has been released, and the picture sharpens.

The headline number is unchanged. Eighty-seven percent of general counsel report using generative artificial intelligence. The relevant new data point is that the trust gap — the gap between access and confidence — is now operationally measurable. Among the cohort of general counsel reporting high trust in artificial-intelligence outputs, eighty-nine percent report positive return on investment from their adoption. Among the cohort reporting low trust, twenty-seven percent report positive return on investment. That is a sixty-two-percentage-point difference, driven not by access — both cohorts have full access — but by workflow integration and by the rigor of the verification step the team applies to outputs.

The operational implication, for a media-company general counsel, is that the deployment question has moved past procurement. The deployment question is now whether your team has built the calibration and verification practice that converts access into return. The teams reporting high trust are doing three things consistently. They are running the artificial-intelligence output through a second-pass verification by the lawyer with substantive ownership of the matter. They are documenting the verification step. And they are training the team to recognize the failure modes of the tools they are using — the hallucination patterns, the citation-fabrication patterns, the doctrinal-drift patterns. The teams reporting low trust are skipping at least one of those three steps.

The procurement market is also consolidating. Harvey closed its two-hundred-million-dollar round in March at an eleven-billion-dollar valuation. Thomson Reuters' Westlaw Precision platform continues to extend integrations across the contract-management and legal-research surfaces. LexisNexis's Protégé deployment of four specialized agents is now in general availability. The legal-technology stack is consolidating around platforms that integrate research, drafting, document review, and matter-management on a single substrate. The procurement question, for a general-counsel team, is not which best-of-breed tool to buy. The procurement question is which platform to anchor on.

One additional operational note. The Bartz fairness hearing yesterday surfaced, again, the question of whether large-language-model training corpora that were lawfully sourced are treated differently from corpora sourced from pirated repositories. Judge Alsup's June 2025 ruling distinguished the two categories. The downstream cases — the Disney and Universal Midjourney complaint, the Reuters and Thomson actions, the Concord Music matter — all engage that distinction. For a media company building its own internal artificial-intelligence stack, the lesson is simple. Document the provenance of your training data. Document the licensing of your training data. Document the conditions under which you accept derivative output. The document trail is the asset.

United States intermodal volume came in at two-hundred-eighty-four-thousand-one-hundred-sixty-three containers and trailers, the fourth consecutive week of mid-single-digit year-over-year growth. The volume direction is confirmed.

The trucking side is now confirming the rail signal. FreightWaves SONAR has truckload spot rates, inclusive of fuel, holding at approximately two-dollars-eighty cents per mile nationally, up twenty-three percent from the same point a year ago. The Outbound Tender Reject Index is running at fourteen-point-four-three percent, twelve percent above its six-month average — a tightening signal. And less-than-truckload rates are showing their strongest upward pressure since the 2023 carrier exit, with new bids twelve-and-a-half percent higher year over year and twenty-nine percent above May 2021. International Roadcheck week ran Tuesday through Thursday this week with the standard regulatory enforcement focus, which removes capacity from the system temporarily and amplifies the tightening signal.

For Trains and FreightWaves editorial, the through-line continues to firm. The volume direction first appeared in late March. The rate direction first appeared in late April. Both are now confirmed by independent data streams. The manufacturing-and-construction freight thesis that has been the dominant 2026 story has additional space to run.
