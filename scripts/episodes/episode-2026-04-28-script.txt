[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up, and the anchor for today: *Commonwealth v. Meta Platforms*. On April tenth, 2026, the Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts unanimously ruled that Section Two Thirty of the Communications Decency Act does not immunize Meta from claims that the design of Instagram caused harm to children in the Commonwealth. The opinion, authored by Justice Dalila Argaez Wendlandt, affirmed a trial-court denial of Meta's motion to dismiss and held that, where a complaint alleges harm flowing from a platform's own design choices — rather than from third-party content posted by users — Section Two Thirty does not apply. This is the first time a state high court has held, sitting *en banc*, that Section Two Thirty does not shield design-feature claims. I will spend about ten minutes on this in the deep dive. For the moment, put the date on your calendar: April tenth, 2026 — *Massachusetts v. Meta*. The platform-liability ground has shifted.

Second: state privacy enforcement, in two pieces that read as one story. The California Privacy Protection Agency announced in February of this year a two-point-seven-five-million-dollar settlement against a major streaming company for opt-out failures, breaking the agency's previous record — a one-point-three-five-million-dollar settlement against Tractor Supply — in less than four months. Around the same time, a bipartisan Consortium of Privacy Regulators became formally operational, connecting state attorneys general and dedicated privacy enforcement bodies across roughly nineteen states with comprehensive privacy laws on the books. What this means in plain English: the operational agenda set by California's enforcement sweeps now travels. When California, or Texas, or Connecticut runs a sweep, the playbook moves to the other AG offices via the consortium within weeks. If your privacy program is calibrated to one state, you are calibrated for the wrong number. A single deficiency — a faulty opt-out, a missing data-protection-impact-assessment, an out-of-scope cross-context sharing — is now nineteen exposure surfaces, not one. Audit accordingly.

Third: the Department of Labor's joint-employer proposed rule. Comment period closes today, April twenty-eighth, 2026, on a Department of Labor proposal that would re-set the standard for when two entities are considered joint employers under the Fair Labor Standards Act. The proposed rule, published in the Federal Register on February twenty-seventh, would expand the joint-employer test to capture more contracting relationships — particularly arrangements in which one entity supplies workers and another directs the conditions of their work. For a multi-vertical publisher with creator agreements, freelance arrangements, contractor video-host engagements, and shared-services structures across a corporate family, the rule would meaningfully change the wage-and-hour exposure calculation. If you have not filed a comment, the window closes today. If you have not opened a file on whether your existing creator and contractor agreements would survive the proposed test, open one this week. If your portfolio includes any property that depends on a third-party software-development kit for analytics, attribution, or audience monetization, the contractual allocation of compliance liability — particularly in the indemnity, audit-rights, and termination-for-convenience provisions of those vendor agreements — needs a fresh look.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to *Commonwealth v. Meta Platforms*. This is where I want you to spend the next ten or twelve minutes, because the Massachusetts opinion is the most concrete signal we have, in twenty years, that Section Two Thirty's protective umbrella has a hole in it that publishers and platform operators need to plan around. And the hole is exactly where the engagement features live.

Let me set the scene. Section Two Thirty of the Communications Decency Act, enacted in 1996, has two operative parts. Subsection one provides that a provider of an interactive computer service shall not be treated as the publisher or speaker of information provided by another information content provider. Subsection two provides liability protection for good-faith content moderation — the part that shields platforms from being sued for taking content down. The first part, the publisher-immunity provision, is what generated three decades of doctrine that essentially made platforms unliable for what their users posted. *Zeran v. America Online* in 1997, *Barnes v. Yahoo* in 2009, *Doe v. MySpace* in 2008, and a long line of follow-on cases all said, in different vocabulary, the same thing — if the harm comes from third-party content, Section Two Thirty applies. End of inquiry.

For most of those twenty-eight years, the *because the harm comes from third-party content* line did all the work. Plaintiffs' lawyers tried to plead around it. Courts mostly rejected the workarounds. The doctrine, in practice, turned the publisher-immunity provision into a near-categorical rule. The few exceptions — the failure-to-warn theory in *Doe v. Internet Brands*, the duty-to-design theory floated in concurrences in *Lemmon v. Snap* — were narrow, fact-specific, and largely confined to the Ninth Circuit. None of them dislodged the basic structure of the immunity. Until now, no state high court had affirmatively endorsed a workaround at the appellate level. The Massachusetts opinion is the first.

Now to what changed. The Massachusetts complaint is not against user-generated content. It is against the platform itself. Specifically, the Commonwealth alleges that Meta designed Instagram with features intentionally engineered to maximize compulsive use among children — infinite scroll, algorithmic recommendation calibrated to engagement, push-notification systems, beauty filters, and other architectural choices — and that the company misrepresented to consumers the safety of the platform with respect to those features. The Commonwealth also alleges public-nuisance claims, unfair-business-practice claims under Massachusetts General Laws Chapter Ninety-Three A, and consumer-protection claims tied to the misrepresentations.

The Section Two Thirty question that came up to the Supreme Judicial Court was narrow. Does the statute immunize a platform when the asserted harm flows from the platform's own design choices, rather than from any specific piece of third-party content the platform hosted or transmitted?

The court's answer, unanimously, is no.

Justice Wendlandt's reasoning is doctrinally precise, and it is worth understanding correctly because it is going to be cited and recited and stretched and contested for the next decade. The opinion does three things. First, it draws a line between, on one side, claims premised on the platform's role as a publisher of user content — those, the court says, are still barred by Section Two Thirty — and, on the other side, claims premised on the platform's first-party conduct, including the design of features and the platform's affirmative representations about its own safety. Second, the opinion adopts what is sometimes called the *first-party conduct* theory, which several federal circuits have flirted with in recent years but no state high court has previously embraced *en banc*. Third, the opinion holds that even when first-party conduct is bound up with third-party content — which any algorithmic recommendation system is — the question is whether the gravamen of the claim is the platform's own behavior or the third-party speech. If the gravamen is the platform's own behavior, the opinion holds, Section Two Thirty does not control. The court frames this as a faithful reading of the statutory text, not a departure from it — the statute, the court reasons, addresses publication of third-party information, not the platform's own conduct in designing the system that does the publishing. That reading is contestable, and Meta will contest it. But it has the benefit of being doctrinally grounded rather than policy-driven, which makes it more durable in subsequent appellate review.

Three things to say about the doctrinal posture. One, the opinion is consistent with the Ninth Circuit's decision in *Anderson v. TikTok* — the Blackout Challenge case — and with the Third Circuit's separate analysis in *Estate of Anderson*. So the federal-circuit landscape was already moving in this direction. Massachusetts is the first state high court to align with that direction at the appellate level. Two, the opinion arrives in a posture that almost guarantees Meta will seek further review. Meta has already publicly indicated it is evaluating its options, including a possible petition for *certiorari* to the United States Supreme Court. Three, even if the Supreme Court takes the case on the federal question — which it has, so far, declined to do in earlier vehicles — the opinion will operate as binding law in Massachusetts in the interim, and as persuasive authority in every other jurisdiction. The complaint proceeds in state court. Discovery proceeds. Document custodians get preservation notices. Engineering team members get deposition subpoenas. The cost to Meta of defending this case, even if it ultimately wins, is now substantial.

A fourth observation, which I think is underrated. The Massachusetts opinion is careful to disclaim any holding on whether Section Two Thirty applies to algorithmic curation as a generic matter. Justice Wendlandt explicitly notes that the court is not deciding the broader algorithmic-recommendation question. What the court does decide is narrower — that a complaint focused on platform-design choices and on the platform's own representations is not a complaint about third-party speech. That narrowness is what makes the opinion durable. A broader holding would be more vulnerable on appeal. By staying inside the *first-party conduct* lane, the court has produced a ruling that is harder to overturn and easier for sister courts to follow without committing to a more aggressive proposition. Plaintiffs' counsel who try to stretch the opinion into a generalized algorithmic-immunity attack will likely lose ground; plaintiffs' counsel who replicate the precise pleading architecture that the Commonwealth used will likely gain ground. The strategic lesson, for both sides of the bar, is in the pleading.

Now to the practical implications for an in-house GC at a media company.

If your company operates a property with any of the following architectural elements, your Section Two Thirty risk has materially changed since April tenth. First, an algorithmic recommendation feed — what users see next is selected by software you control. Second, an engagement-optimization layer — interactions are tuned to maximize time spent, not user-stated preferences. Third, push notifications calibrated to bring users back. Fourth, social features that reward compulsive use — streaks, reactions, infinite scroll. Fifth, any age-gate or age-related representation that could be alleged to be inaccurate. The Massachusetts theory does not require that a child user be on the platform. The theory rests on the design choices and the representations. A general-audience platform with engagement-tuned features is exposed to the same kind of design-defect theory if a plaintiff can plead, with sufficient particularity, that the design causes a cognizable harm.

Three implications. Operational. Documentary. And contractual.

Operational. If your engineering and product organizations build engagement features, the Massachusetts opinion makes it predictable that those design choices will surface in litigation. Internal documents discussing the goals of engagement-tuning, the trade-offs around well-being, the testing of features with younger users, and the choice of metrics will become discovery exhibits. Run the same hygiene exercise you would run on safety-critical code, but on engagement-tuning code. Get product-design memos written by counsel-or-supervised counsel where reasonably feasible. Where not feasible — and most product-design memos are not feasible to privilege — make sure your communications culture around engagement is as professional as it would be at, say, a defense contractor.

Documentary. The misrepresentation prong of the Massachusetts claim is independent of the design prong. The Commonwealth alleges that Meta misrepresented Instagram's safety to consumers. If your company has made public representations — in privacy policies, on landing pages, in marketing copy, in press statements, in investor disclosures — about the safety of any platform feature, those statements need to be re-read against the operating reality of the feature. A statement that was true in 2022 may not be true now. A statement that hedges is more defensible than a statement that asserts. Have product, legal, and marketing align quarterly on a single set of representations, and document the alignment. The marketing-claim-substantiation file should live with the legal team, not on a marketing operations dashboard, and it should include the product or engineering memo that establishes the technical basis for each public claim. If the technical basis no longer holds, the claim comes off the page within thirty days. Build that workflow once, and run it every quarter.

Contractual. If you sell, distribute, license, or syndicate content into platforms with engagement features that you do not control — every major property does this — your contractual allocation of liability for engagement-derived harms becomes an open question. The platforms themselves will resist accepting that allocation. Your position needs to be supported by indemnification on the platform side for design-feature claims that flow from the platform's own architecture, with a carve-out where the harm is alleged to flow from your content. Renegotiate at the next renewal cycle. If the platform refuses, that refusal is itself a data point about where the platform thinks the risk lies.

Two pieces of doctrine I will close on. First, public-nuisance claims, which the Commonwealth pleads, are an aggressive theory that has been used with increasing frequency against industry actors over the past five years — the opioid litigation, the firearms litigation, the social-media litigation. *Massachusetts v. Meta* is the most prominent media-platform application of the theory at the state-high-court level. Public-nuisance theory does not require the same showing of individual injury as ordinary tort. Its expanding use changes the type of plaintiff who can sue and the type of remedy that can be obtained — including injunctive remedies that can compel design changes. Second, the opinion's reasoning on first-party conduct is exportable. Today it is design features. Tomorrow it could be ad-targeting choices, recommendation logic for advertising, or the architecture of subscription dark patterns. The doctrine has the kind of generality that lets it travel.

One forward-looking note before I move off the deep dive. Insurance. Every general counsel I have spoken to in the past two weeks has had the same second-order question — does my media-liability tower respond to a design-defect claim. The answer, in the policies I have read, is: not cleanly. Most media-liability and tech-errors-and-omissions policies are written around content-based torts — defamation, copyright, privacy, intellectual-property infringement — and around system-failure events. They are not written around the proposition that a user-interface choice constitutes the alleged defect. Some carriers have begun adding design-feature exclusions in renewal cycles since the Ninth Circuit's *Anderson v. TikTok* opinion. Other carriers are silent. Read your policies. Specifically, read the definition of *Wrongful Act* in your media-liability tower, the relevant exclusions, and any prior-acts language. If your renewal is in the next sixty days, start the conversation with your broker now, with the Massachusetts opinion in front of you. Coverage litigation arising out of design-defect claims is going to be a quiet, expensive subgenre of this story.

That is *Commonwealth v. Meta Platforms*. Read the opinion. Pay particular attention to the sections on public nuisance and on the misrepresentation prong, because those are the parts plaintiffs in other jurisdictions will copy first. The Justia citation is Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court docket SJC dash one three seven four seven, decided April tenth. The opinion is short enough to read in a single sitting, and I would recommend doing exactly that, before the next product-design meeting on your calendar.

[GC BENCH]

Now to GC Bench. Three items this morning, and I am keeping them practical.

The first item. Audit your content-platform contracts for design-feature liability allocation. This is the immediate-term work that the Massachusetts opinion creates. Pull every agreement under which your company places content on a third-party platform with engagement features — the obvious ones are the major social platforms, but also include short-video platforms, podcast distribution platforms, embedded-content arrangements, and any white-labeled platform partnerships. Read the indemnification, allocation-of-liability, and termination clauses with the *first-party conduct* doctrine in mind. Where the platform's design caused the alleged harm, you should be indemnified. Where your content caused the alleged harm, you indemnify. Today, most of these agreements are silent or skewed against the publisher. Build a list. Renegotiate at the next renewal cycle. If you are renegotiating now, this is leverage.

The second item. Departing-employee data control is an under-reported compliance and litigation risk surface, and the Spring is when it hits hardest. When an employee leaves — voluntarily or involuntarily — and your data-control architecture depends on that employee's compliance with internal policy, you have a problem. The fix is not a memo. The fix is to require, as a default of the productivity stack, that all work product live in an enterprise cloud system with manager-direct-report access permissions enforced by the system, not by the employee. This week, I would have your IT and legal-operations teams jointly run a tabletop on a specific recently-departed employee — pick a name, pretend they walked out with three terabytes of work product on a local drive, and walk through the recovery and litigation-hold steps. The exercise will reveal the gaps faster than any policy review. Do not skip it.

The third item. The Department of Labor's joint-employer comment closes today. If you have not filed a comment and you have a meaningful contractor or creator population, the comment has already been written for you by your trade association — probably the Digital Content Next coalition, the Online Publishers Association, or the Association of Magazine Media. Find it. Read it. If it represents your position, sign on. If it does not, file your own — even a short letter. Comment-period activity becomes part of the administrative record. The administrative record drives the inevitable Administrative Procedure Act challenge. Be in the record.
