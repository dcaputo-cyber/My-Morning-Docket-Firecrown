[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. Colorado's new AI act. On Thursday, May fourteenth, Governor Jared Polis signed Senate Bill twenty-six dash one-eighty-nine, repealing and replacing the original Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act — Senate Bill twenty-four dash two-oh-five — that had been generating compliance anxiety among technology companies and regulated industries since its passage in 2024. The original act, which was scheduled to take effect June thirtieth of this year, was stayed by a federal magistrate judge on April twenty-seventh after a constitutional challenge, leaving the enforcement calendar in suspension. The governor's signature on the replacement law resolves the suspension and resets the clock. The new law does not take effect until January first, two thousand and twenty-seven, and it makes several structural changes that matter for any in-house counsel who has been preparing for the original act. First, the new law narrows the scope from the broad high-risk artificial-intelligence-system framework to a targeted regime covering automated-decision-making technology that processes personal data used to materially influence a consequential decision. Risk management programs, annual impact assessments, and the duty to use reasonable care to avoid algorithmic discrimination — the three load-bearing elements of the original act — are removed. Second, before the Colorado Attorney General may bring an enforcement action, the state must provide written notice of the alleged violation and allow a sixty-day cure period, if cure is possible. The original act had no such grace period. The practical implication for a media company is that any prior-year compliance work done against the original act's framework needs to be reviewed against the new act's narrower scope. The work is not wasted — the new law still creates obligations in the automated-decision-making space — but the risk architecture is different, the timeline is extended to January first, and the cure-period structure meaningfully changes the enforcement posture. The Colorado framework, now rewritten, joins the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act — with enforcement powers commencing August second — as the two operative AI governance instruments on the second-half calendar. We will return to both on GC Bench.

Second. The Meta publisher copyright suit. On Tuesday, May fifth, five major publishing companies — Elsevier, Cengage, Hachette Book Group, Macmillan Publishers, and McGraw-Hill — together with novelist Scott Turow, filed a putative class-action complaint against Meta Platforms and its chief executive, Mark Zuckerberg, in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York. The complaint alleges that Meta committed willful copyright infringement by obtaining millions of copyrighted books, journal articles, and other written works from known piracy sites — the BitTorrent-adjacent infrastructure that has been the subject of prior litigation against other frontier-model defendants — for use in training the Llama large-language models. The Zuckerberg personal-liability theory alleges that he personally authorized and actively encouraged the training-data acquisition despite being briefed on the legal risks. Meta has characterized the suit as contrary to the correct understanding of fair use and has stated it will fight the action aggressively. Two elements of the complaint warrant particular attention for an in-house bar tracking the AI-copyright docket. First, the complaint is the most concentrated single-filing of major commercial publisher claims against a frontier-model defendant in the Southern District to date, and it joins a docket that already includes the consolidated Times-OpenAI multidistrict litigation before Judge Sidney Stein and the separately pending Concord Music action against Anthropic. Second, the complaint includes a count under Section twelve-oh-two of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act, alleging that Meta stripped copyright management information from the acquired works to conceal their origins during the training process. The DMCA stripping theory is the same theory that the Concord Music supplemental complaint pioneered earlier this year against Anthropic, and it is now the second major copyright-management-information count in the AI-copyright portfolio. A successful stripping count expands the statutory-damages architecture in a way that a bare copyright-infringement count does not. The in-house counsel tracking this docket should be watching the procedural calendar in the Meta matter, which the Southern District has not yet set.

Third. The consolidated OpenAI copyright litigation — specifically, the preservation order dispute. The matter is In re OpenAI, Incorporated, Copyright Infringement Litigation, the multidistrict docket in the Southern District of New York. This morning, Magistrate Judge Ona Wang issued a clarification to a standing preservation order, confirming that ChatGPT Enterprise is excluded from the preservation obligation. OpenAI had, in the weeks prior, filed a motion for reconsideration of the preservation order with Judge Wang and has additionally appealed the order to District Judge Sidney Stein. The substantive question underlying both the motion and the appeal is whether OpenAI can be compelled to retain user-conversation data that it characterizes as constituting a privacy intrusion on its customers if retained indefinitely. Judge Stein has previously affirmed — in January of this year — an order compelling OpenAI to produce twenty million anonymized ChatGPT logs, rejecting OpenAI's argument that it should be permitted to search those logs by keyword and produce only conversations implicating the plaintiffs' specific works. The cumulative picture is of an AI-copyright defendant managing a discovery docket that has now expanded into a multi-level preservation dispute on the merits, a reconsideration motion at the magistrate level, and an interlocutory appeal to the district court, all simultaneously. The operational signal for any in-house counsel whose company is contemplating AI-tool deployment in a regulated workflow is the preservation-versus-privacy tension. If your organization deploys a generative-AI tool that retains user interactions, the litigation-hold question — whether those interaction logs are discoverable in a copyright, trade-secret, or other proceeding — is not a hypothetical. It is the question OpenAI is litigating this morning.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning is the Federal Communications Commission's broadcast regulatory offensive against Disney-owned ABC, and what the convergence of three distinct regulatory tools on a single broadcaster signals for any general counsel who works at or advises a company holding FCC broadcast licenses.

I want to take the three tools in sequence, then examine the architecture of the pressure campaign, then walk through four operational consequences for a media-company general counsel, and then give three concrete actions.

The first tool: early license renewal. Broadcast licenses in the United States expire on a rolling eight-year cycle organized by region. The Disney-owned ABC television stations would have come up for renewal beginning in two thousand twenty-eight — the standard cycle for the stations in the relevant markets. On April twenty-eighth of this year, the FCC's Media Bureau ordered Disney to file all ABC station license renewal applications by May twenty-eighth of this year — tomorrow — years ahead of the normal cycle. The authority invoked is Section three-oh-nine of the Communications Act, which authorizes the Commission to require license renewal filings consistent with the public interest standard. The Bureau cited ongoing concerns about Disney's diversity, equity, and inclusion practices and the investigation into the company's news operations. The mechanism of early renewal is not novel as a legal matter. The Communications Act has always permitted the Commission to trigger early review. But the last time the Commission exercised the mechanism aggressively was approximately fifty years ago, in the nineteen-seventies, when the Commission ordered early hearings on license renewals for stations where civil-rights complaints had been filed. The deployment of early renewal now, against a major broadcast network group, is the first use of the mechanism in a half-century, and it is the use of a sleeping procedural tool as an active pressure instrument.

The second tool: the equal-time rule and The View. The equal-time rule — more precisely, the equal-opportunities requirement under Section three-fifteen of the Communications Act — requires a broadcast licensee to give every legally qualified candidate for the same office equal broadcast time if the licensee gives time to any one candidate. The rule has historically included a series of bona-fide-news-program exemptions: regularly scheduled newscasts, bona fide news interview programs, documentaries, and on-the-spot news coverage of genuine news events are exempt. The exemption for bona fide news interview programs is the exemption that The View has operated under since a 2002 FCC Media Bureau ruling that declared the program exempt. FCC Chairman Brendan Carr announced, in the months leading to the current cycle, that the Commission would no longer grant blanket exemptions to programs on the basis of their claimed news function, and specifically targeted programs whose coverage, in the Chairman's characterization, reflected political motivations rather than genuine news-interview function. Disney filed its petition for a declaratory ruling on May twenty-second — five days ago — asking the FCC to confirm that The View remains a bona fide news interview program exempt from the equal-opportunities rules. The FCC's Media Bureau has set a comment-and-reply schedule with initial comments due June twenty-second and replies due July sixth. The consequence of a ruling against the exemption would be that, in any election cycle in which a candidate appears on The View, every other legally qualified candidate for the same office would have a legally enforceable claim to equivalent airtime on ABC-owned stations. The programming and scheduling consequences would be immediate, significant, and operationally disruptive for any station carrying the program.

The third tool: the news-distortion policy and the mandamus petition. The FCC's news-distortion policy, adopted in nineteen forty-nine as part of the Fairness Doctrine framework and retained even after the Fairness Doctrine was rescinded in nineteen-eighty-seven, allows the Commission to investigate and potentially revoke a broadcaster's license on the finding that the broadcaster has willfully misrepresented news or deliberately slanted news content. The policy has rarely been invoked to produce an actual enforcement sanction. It has historically served as a background deterrent — a tool the Commission can use to open an inquiry but rarely presses to a conclusion. In November of two thousand twenty-five, a bipartisan coalition of seven former FCC chairs and commissioners, including five Republicans, and a group of senior former staff filed a formal petition asking the Commission to rescind the news-distortion policy on the ground that it has become, in the current environment, a tool for chilling protected editorial speech. The FCC did not respond to the petition. On April twenty-eighth of this year, the same coalition filed a petition for a writ of mandamus with the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, asking the court to compel the FCC to act on the news-distortion rescission petition. A three-judge panel of the D.C. Circuit ordered the FCC to file a response within thirty days — which means the FCC's response to the mandamus petition is due in approximately four days from this morning. The mandamus petition is not a challenge to a specific FCC enforcement action against a specific broadcaster. It is a challenge to the institutional existence of a regulatory tool that the petitioners — including former Republican commissioners — characterize as inherently incompatible with the First Amendment in its current deployed form.

Those are the three tools. Now the architecture. The power of the current FCC posture is not in any single tool taken in isolation. The early-renewal order, standing alone, is a procedural inconvenience — Disney files the applications, the Commission reviews them against the public interest standard, and the licenses are either renewed or subjected to a hearing process that has its own legal constraints. The equal-time challenge to The View, standing alone, is a manageable administrative proceeding with a standard comment-and-reply calendar. The news-distortion inquiry, standing alone, has never produced a license revocation and has historically been resolved without formal sanction. The architecture of the pressure is in the simultaneity and in the cumulative effect on decision-making inside the broadcaster. A broadcaster facing three regulatory proceedings at once — even if the probability of adverse outcome on any individual proceeding is low — faces a different risk calculus than a broadcaster facing one. The transaction cost of simultaneous defense, the reputational cloud over the license portfolio, the chilling effect on editorial decisions in the period before each proceeding resolves — these are the operational consequences of the multi-tool architecture. They are also the consequences that the bipartisan mandamus coalition is putting before the D.C. Circuit as evidence of the news-distortion policy's incompatibility with the First Amendment.

Four operational consequences for a media-company general counsel.

First. The license-renewal calendar is no longer a background item. Broadcast license renewals have, for the past thirty years, been essentially administrative formalities — the FCC's renewal grant rate for stations not facing documented public-interest challenges has been effectively one hundred percent. The deployment of the early-renewal tool against Disney changes the planning assumption. Any public company whose legal department supports a broadcast-license portfolio should now treat license renewal as an active risk-management function rather than a background compliance item. The post-filing review process in the current Commission environment is the process worth investing in — both in terms of ensuring the license file is complete and defensible against a public-interest challenge and in terms of having a well-prepared regulatory-affairs strategy before the filing is made.

Second. The equal-time exemption analysis for news programs is now a live compliance question. Every news-interview program, news magazine, and news-adjacent program that runs on a broadcast station holding an FCC license now operates in an environment in which the historically granted exemption for bona fide news content is under active challenge. The compliance question for any broadcaster is whether each of its programs is independently defensible as a bona fide news interview program or news documentary under the Commission's current analytical framework, not just whether the program held an exemption in a prior year's Bureau ruling. The prior ruling, in the Commission's current posture, is not dispositive. The program's current editorial function, documented with contemporaneous evidence, is the defense.

Third. The litigation-hold and document-preservation obligations around editorial decisions have expanded in scope. An FCC proceeding addressing news distortion, a license-renewal proceeding addressing the public-interest standard, or an equal-time proceeding addressing the bona fide news function of a specific program all generate document-preservation obligations at the moment the proceeding is opened. Editorial communications, producer decisions, booking decisions, interview protocols, and the internal guidance documents that govern how on-air editorial content is produced are all potentially within scope. The litigation-hold architecture for a broadcaster that is simultaneously managing an FCC license-renewal proceeding, an equal-time inquiry, and a news-distortion inquiry needs to cover all three. The hold documents need to be coordinated. The custodian list needs to reflect all three proceedings. The failure to coordinate the hold across simultaneous regulatory proceedings is the failure that produces an evidence-destruction problem in the proceeding where the documents matter most.

Fourth. The D.C. Circuit mandamus proceeding is the most consequential structural development on the broadcast-regulatory calendar this year. The mandamus petition is asking the court to compel the FCC to take a binding institutional position on whether the news-distortion policy should be rescinded. If the D.C. Circuit orders the FCC to act and the FCC rescinds the policy, a structural element of the broadcast regulatory apparatus that has existed since nineteen forty-nine is removed. If the FCC declines to rescind and is forced to defend the policy, the Commission will need to articulate a theory of how the policy is consistent with the First Amendment in the current media environment — a theory that the bipartisan petitioner group has argued cannot be coherently articulated. For any broadcaster in the current environment, the mandamus proceeding is the proceeding worth tracking closest, because its resolution will determine whether the most historically problematic element of the FCC's editorial-oversight toolkit survives into the next administration.

Three concrete actions for a general counsel or regulatory counsel at any media company with broadcast assets.

Action one. Commission the license-file audit now, before the May twenty-eighth deadline passes and the Disney situation becomes the ambient backdrop. The audit should confirm that the public-interest record for each licensed station — the political-programming logs, the public-file disclosures, the ownership disclosure filings, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission compliance documentation — is current, complete, and defensible against a public-interest challenge. The audit is not a crisis response. It is the organizational discipline that makes the crisis response possible if one is required.

Action two. Brief the board or the audit committee on the equal-time compliance posture for each news-interview and news-adjacent program in the broadcast portfolio. The briefing should address three questions: which programs currently rely on a bona fide news exemption, what the documentary basis for that exemption is, and what the operational consequence of a successful equal-time challenge to each program would be. The briefing should be a working session, not a presentation. The board's informed understanding of the regulatory risk is the predicate for a timely and adequate response if the risk materializes.

Action three. Assign a regulatory-affairs resource — internal or external — to track the D.C. Circuit mandamus proceeding on a real-time basis. The FCC's response is due in approximately four days. The D.C. Circuit's next procedural action — whether it grants the writ, denies it, or orders additional briefing — will set the structural framework for the news-distortion question for the remainder of the year. The first substantive filing after the FCC's response will be the document that defines the terms of the engagement. For any broadcaster, the mandamus proceeding is the regulatory story that matters most in the second quarter, and the monitoring function is a low-cost, high-return investment in situational awareness.

[GC BENCH]
The legal technology and AI governance item this morning is the Colorado AI Act replacement and what it means for the AI governance compliance calendar that every in-house legal team is currently managing.

Colorado's replacement statute, signed May fourteenth and effective January first, two thousand and twenty-seven, is the third major AI governance instrument that has moved in the past ninety days. The first was the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act, whose enforcement powers over general-purpose-model providers commence August second — sixty-seven days from this morning. The second was the Connecticut Data Privacy Act amendments, which become effective July first — thirty-five days from this morning — and which include an explicit requirement that privacy notices disclose whether the organization uses personal data to train large-language models. The third is now Colorado, which has replaced its original broad framework with a narrower regime focused on automated-decision-making technology effective January first.

The sequencing problem these three instruments produce together is this. Each instrument has a different effective date, a different covered-activity scope, and a different enforcement mechanism. The EU Act's general-purpose-model obligations and August second enforcement commencement apply if the company is a covered provider or a downstream deployer of a covered model. The Connecticut amendment's large-language-model training-data disclosure applies if the company processes Connecticut residents' personal data in connection with a model training workflow. Colorado's new ADMT regime applies if the company uses automated systems that materially influence consequential decisions affecting Colorado residents. For a media company of meaningful scale operating in all three jurisdictions — which most do — the compliance work is not three separate projects. It is one project with three different implementation dates, three different covered-activity mappings, and three different enforcement profiles. The company that has approached this as three separate work streams will miss the cross-jurisdictional dependencies. The company that has a unified AI governance framework — a register of AI tools and use cases, a data-flow map, a coverage-determination worksheet for each jurisdiction, and a single implementation timeline — is the company that arrives at each effective date with the documentation and the workflows in place.

One procurement observation. The unified AI governance framework is not a document project. It is a workflow project. The framework only exists, in an operationally meaningful sense, if the in-house team has the tooling to maintain it — to add new AI tools as they are deployed, to update coverage determinations as new regulations take effect, and to produce the required disclosures and assessments on the schedules each regime imposes. The legal-technology vendors that are positioning well for the second half of this year are the vendors whose products address the governance layer — the register, the workflow, the documentation — rather than just the task layer. The distinction is worth making explicit in any second-half procurement conversation.
