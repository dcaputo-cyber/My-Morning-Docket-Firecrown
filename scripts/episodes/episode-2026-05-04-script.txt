Second. Advertising. We have flagged the holding-company settlement in the Northern District of Texas across the past two weeks. As of mid-April, the consent orders against W-P-P, Publicis, and Dentsu have been entered and finalized by the district court. The orders prohibit each of the three holding companies from entering into agreements with horizontal competitors that set common brand-safety standards or that restrict advertising based on what the Commission has characterized as biased or politically motivated criteria. The Global Alliance for Responsible Media as a coordinating body, the Advertiser Protection Bureau, the cross-holding-company brand-suitability mechanics that publishers and ad-tech vendors have been operating against for the better part of a decade — those structures are now subject to a permanent injunction against the three largest holding companies in the world. The implication for any publisher whose advertising business runs through holding-company demand is that the floor categories your sales team and your trust-and-safety team have been operating against are about to change. Specifically, the Misinformation category, which was added to the Brand Safety Floor in June 2022, is the lead exhibit in the FTC's complaint. That category will not survive in its current form. Your inventory pricing model, your suitability-tier negotiation model, your Open Internet versus Walled Garden mix — all of it should be re-modeled this quarter against a world in which holding companies are not coordinating around uniform exclusion lists. The Federal Trade Commission, in joining with eight state attorneys general on this complaint, made clear that the bipartisan posture on coordinated brand-safety conduct is settled. There will not be an administration-driven reversal here.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning is on European age assurance, and specifically on the four-document, four-deadline picture that has assembled itself across the past three weeks.

Let me set the scene. The Digital Services Act has been in effect since 2023. The general obligations apply to all online platforms operating in the European Union. The additional obligations applicable to Very Large Online Platforms — the V-L-O-Ps designation — apply to a defined set of platforms. The relevant general-obligation provision for our purposes today is Article Twenty-Eight, which requires platforms accessible to minors to take appropriate and proportionate measures to ensure a high level of privacy, safety, and security for them. The relevant V-L-O-P-specific provision is Article Thirty-Four, which requires very large platforms to assess systemic risks arising from the design or functioning of their services, including risks to the protection of minors. And the relevant Audiovisual Media Services Directive provision, which applies parallel to the Digital Services Act, is Article Twenty-Eight-B, requiring video-sharing-platform providers to take appropriate measures to protect minors from harmful content.

April fifteenth. The European Commission released the feature-ready version of the EU age-verification solution. The mechanics, briefly. The application allows a user to register once, with an electronic identity document or a national digital identity, to a privacy-preserving age-verification token. When a platform requests an age check, the application returns a zero-knowledge proof — a yes-or-no answer to the platform's question, "is this user over thirteen," or "is this user over fifteen," or "is this user over eighteen" — without disclosing date of birth, identity, or any other personally identifying information. The proof is bound to the device and is not portable across devices in a way that would create a tracking surface. The application is interoperable with the European Digital Identity Wallet ecosystem the union is rolling out across member states.

April twenty-second. Greece and the Flemish Government announced a coordinated push at the Council of the European Union for an under-fifteen social-media floor. The political context, briefly. France has been signaling support for fifteen as the threshold across the past two months. Spain has been at fifteen rhetorically since 2024. The Greek and Flemish announcement is the first formal coordinated motion by member states to bring the question to council level. There is no immediate vote. There is a developing political consensus.

April twenty-ninth. The Commission issued its preliminary finding against Meta. Specifically, the Commission found that Meta's age-assurance measures on Facebook and Instagram are inadequate to prevent under-thirteen users from accessing those services, and that the inadequacy is a systemic risk under Article Thirty-Four. Meta has the opportunity to respond. The Commission has the authority to issue a non-compliance decision and to assess penalties of up to six percent of global annual turnover. Meta will respond. This is the second preliminary finding against Meta in fifteen days. The first, on April fifteenth, addressed the Notice and Action regime and researcher access to platform data. The second, on April twenty-ninth, addresses age. Both are still preliminary. The compounding effect on Meta's regulatory position in the union, however, is now meaningful.

April twenty-ninth, same day. The Commission urged member states to accelerate rollout of the age-verification application, with a stated goal of citizen availability across all twenty-seven member states by the end of 2026.

The implication for any publisher with an EU-facing property — a website, a streaming service, a podcast distribution endpoint, a newsletter signup form, an interactive game embed — is that age assurance is moving from a question your trust-and-safety team has been able to defer, on the basis that effective tools did not exist, to a question for which the Commission has now released the toolkit and is openly questioning the inadequacy of incumbent solutions. If the Commission finds that Meta's age-assurance measures are insufficient under Article Thirty-Four, the implication for smaller platforms, which are not designated as V-L-O-Ps but which are subject to Article Twenty-Eight, is that the Commission's expectation of what is sufficient under the proportionality principle is now considerably higher than it was twelve months ago.

Four concrete general-counsel actions, before the deep dive closes.

One. Inventory your EU-facing properties this quarter. For each, identify whether it is reasonably accessible to minors, and apply the Article Twenty-Eight assessment. The "reasonably accessible to minors" test is the relevant statutory test and it is broader than "designed for minors." If your fitness magazine has a YouTube channel embedded in its homepage, that channel is reasonably accessible to a minor. If your boating publication runs a comments section without registration gates, those comments are reasonably accessible to a minor. The threshold question is whether the property is reasonably accessible. If it is, you have an Article Twenty-Eight obligation. Document it.

Two. Build a relationship with the EU age-verification application now, even if you do not yet plan to deploy it. Member states will customize the reference application across the next eight months. Your engineering team will, at some point in the next twelve to eighteen months, integrate against it. Get in front of the integration documentation. Run a small proof-of-concept on a non-revenue property in the third quarter, so that when the council action moves — and it will move — you are not building from a cold start.

Three. Update your privacy notices and your data-mapping documentation to reflect age-assurance data flows, even before you deploy a tool. The German supervisory authorities, the Italian Garante, and the French C-N-I-L have all signaled in the past four months that they expect platforms with European users to have anticipatory documentation for age-assurance processing. The privacy notice that says "we do not knowingly collect personal information from children under thirteen" is no longer the state of the art. The state of the art, on the European side, is a notice that explains affirmatively how the platform verifies age, what data flows are involved in that verification, and what the user's rights are with respect to that data flow.

Four. Identify which of your current vendor relationships will need to interoperate with a member-state-customized age-verification flow. Your single-sign-on provider. Your comment-system vendor. Your video-platform partner. Your advertising consent-management partner. Each of those vendors will, sometime in the next twelve months, ship an integration with the EU age-verification application. The vendor that ships first will have a meaningful competitive advantage, and your contractual posture toward each of those vendors should anticipate the transition. If your master service agreement does not include a forward-compatibility clause for material regulatory changes, this is the renewal cycle to add it.

The summary I would offer the in-house bar this morning is that age assurance is now on the timeline. The implementation deadline is not in the statute. The implementation deadline is in the next preliminary finding, against the next platform, that this Commission issues. And from the cadence of the past three weeks — three preliminary findings against major platforms in fifteen days — that next finding is not far away. The platforms that respond first, with documented, defensible, proportionate measures, will set the bar against which everyone else is measured. The platforms that wait will be measured against a bar set by someone else.

One additional jurisdictional note before I close the deep dive. The United Kingdom is on a parallel but distinct track. The Office of Communications — Ofcom — issued its final guidance on highly effective age assurance in January under the Online Safety Act. The U-K threshold question is whether a service is a Part Three or Part Five service under the Act, and the obligations attach differently. If your organization operates content that would trigger the Part Five primary-priority-content categorization in the U-K, your age-assurance obligations there are more stringent than the European Union's. Your trust-and-safety team should be running parallel matrices for the two jurisdictions, not assuming that European Union compliance covers the U-K.
