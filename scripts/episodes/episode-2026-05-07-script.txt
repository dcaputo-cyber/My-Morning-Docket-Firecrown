[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. The California Privacy Protection Agency. The Agency's preliminary comment period on data-broker audits closes today at five o'clock Pacific. The audit regime under the Delete Act does not commence until 2028, but the comment file is the document that will shape the rule. The Agency is asking, in plain language, what frequency the audit should run on, what the scope should look like, who counts as an independent third-party auditor, and how the audit interacts with the existing Delete Request and Opt-out Platform — the D-R-O-P system — that goes live for forty-five-day deletion processing on August first. For any publisher whose audience-data operations come close to the data-broker definition, the comment is worth filing today. The Agency reads its own comments, and the questions it asks are the leading indicator of the rule that will follow. If your organization is uncertain whether you are within the definition, that is also a matter the comment file is the right place to surface.

Second. The New York Times. The company posted first-quarter results yesterday before the open. The numbers, in summary. Total revenue, six hundred and ninety-three million dollars, up nine point one percent year over year. Digital-only subscription revenue up sixteen percent. Digital-only subscriber count at twelve million four hundred fifty thousand — modestly below the consensus on subscriber count, modestly above on revenue per subscriber, which is the line the company has been telling investors to watch. The company reaffirmed its full-year guidance. Two implications worth carrying. First, the rate of digital-revenue growth at the largest U-S news subscriber base remains in the mid-teens four years after the post-COVID surge ended. That is the single number against which every other premium publisher's pricing strategy is now measured. Second, the company is continuing to extend subscription monetization into adjacent inventory — premium podcasts, the Cooking and Athletic verticals, the Wirecutter affiliate book — and the bundled subscriber, who reads or listens to multiple properties under one subscription, is the highest-retention cohort in the report. For any publisher running a single-property paywall, the bundling strategy is the conversation to put on the second-half calendar.

Third. The discovery order in New York Times against OpenAI. Judge Sidney Stein in the Southern District of New York affirmed an earlier order, in a ruling published in late January and now widely cited, compelling OpenAI to produce twenty million anonymized ChatGPT conversation logs. The reasoning is the part that publishers should read carefully. The court found that the output logs — even logs that do not, on their face, contain reproductions of Times articles — are nonetheless discoverable because they bear on OpenAI's fair-use defense. The theory is that patterns in the output corpus may reveal whether ChatGPT outputs compete with or substitute for the copyrighted source material, which is the fourth fair-use factor under the statute. The order has two operational consequences. First, it expands the discovery footprint of any pending generative-A-I copyright case. The defendant cannot effectively argue that user outputs are private. Second, it pushes the fair-use analysis toward an empirical inquiry — what users actually did with the model — rather than a doctrinal inquiry. That is a meaningful shift, and it has implications for the discovery posture in the Disney and Universal Midjourney case, in the Authors Guild and Sarah Silverman matters, in the Concord Music and Universal Music actions, and in the Reuters and Thomson cases. Trial dates have not been set in the Times matter. The discovery posture is, however, now a matter of doctrine.

Fourth. The Securities and Exchange Commission and climate disclosure. Two threads to keep in mind. On the federal side, the Commission has discontinued its defense of the March 2024 climate-related disclosure rule. Chairman Atkins has signaled that the staff is not going to expend resources reviving it. The administrative process is still being worked through, but for planning purposes the federal rule is, at minimum, dormant. On the state side, the picture is the opposite. New York State enacted a climate-disclosure law late last year that requires designated heavy-emitting categories — waste, fossil-fuel suppliers, and electric utilities — to collect 2026 emissions data and submit to the Department of Environmental Conservation by June 2027. New York is the second U-S jurisdiction with a binding climate-disclosure law on the books. California's S-B two-five-three remains the first. Two implications for a publicly traded media company. The capital-markets disclosure work has not gone away — it has migrated to state-level filings, voluntary disclosures used by investors, and the European Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive for any property with European operations. And the existing 10-K and proxy disclosures still need to address material climate-related risks under the long-standing materiality framework, which has not changed.

Fifth. C-S-X. The Class One railroad reported first-quarter results this morning. The intermodal-revenue line came in modestly positive year over year, against the carload weakness that has characterized the operating landscape for most of the past eighteen months. C-S-X is the second Class One read on the intermodal turn, after Norfolk Southern's report earlier this week. Both calls confirmed the volume direction the Association of American Railroads' weekly data has been showing. The week-of-April-twenty-fifth print — Week Seventeen — had intermodal containers and trailers up four point nine percent year over year, the strongest single-week intermodal print of 2026. For Trains and FreightWaves editorial, the through-line continues to firm. The manufacturing-and-construction freight thesis is the dominant 2026 story, and intermodal is now confirmed as following.

Sixth. The Federal Trade Commission and negative-option rulemaking. The Commission's Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking — the formal restart of the regulatory process after the Eighth Circuit vacated the original Click-to-Cancel rule — closed its comment file on April thirteenth. The Commission has indicated that a proposed rule will follow. While the rulemaking proceeds, the Commission has been actively litigating individual matters under existing authority — the suit against Uber expanded to include civil-penalty claims and state co-plaintiffs, and the actions against L-A Fitness and several education-technology providers have advanced. The signal for any subscription-driven business, including any subscription-driven publisher, is that the absence of a finalized federal rule does not change the enforcement posture. The Commission is treating cancellation-process friction as a deceptive practice under existing Section Five authority. The municipal New York City Click-to-Cancel hearing tomorrow is the local-level companion to that federal posture. If your subscription product sells into New York City, the rule is the rule, the Eighth Circuit's ruling on the federal version notwithstanding.

[DEEP DIVE]

The deep dive this morning is Disney and Universal versus Midjourney, and what the procedural posture of that case tells you about content-licensing strategy at a media company.

The complaint was filed on June eleventh, 2025, in the Central District of California. The plaintiffs are The Walt Disney Company, Universal City Studios, Marvel Studios, Lucasfilm Limited, Twentieth Century Studios, DreamWorks Animation, and three corporate affiliates. The defendant is Midjourney, Inc. — the artificial-intelligence company that operates the prompt-to-image service of the same name. The complaint runs one hundred and ten pages. The factual allegation, in plain language, is that Midjourney trained its image-generation models on Disney and Universal copyrighted character images — Shrek, Homer Simpson, Darth Vader, the Marvel and Pixar catalogs in their entirety — and that the resulting service produces, on prompt, infringing reproductions and derivative works. The complaint pleads direct copyright infringement, contributory infringement, and vicarious infringement. The statutory damages exposure, if all named works are credited at the maximum statutory amount, runs into the billions of dollars. Midjourney filed a forty-three-page motion to dismiss, which was denied. The case is now in discovery. A trial date has been set in the fall of 2026.

Three things about the procedural posture are worth understanding for an in-house counsel.

The first thing. The plaintiffs are not seeking a settlement of the kind that resolved Bartz against Anthropic. They are seeking a final adjudication on the merits. Disney and Universal have, in their public communications and their court filings, framed the case as a referendum on whether unlicensed training on copyrighted character imagery can ever qualify as fair use. The settlement leverage that Anthropic had — the leverage of being the only major frontier-model developer facing a class of more than four hundred eighty thousand authors — does not translate cleanly to Midjourney. Midjourney is a smaller defendant, with a more concentrated user base, and the plaintiffs have a significantly stronger ability to sustain litigation costs than the defendant does. The case is, in other words, structured for verdict rather than settlement.

The second thing. Midjourney's motion-to-dismiss arguments are useful as a window into the defenses the artificial-intelligence industry will try to advance. Midjourney argued that its training process was protected fair use under the four-factor analysis. That the plaintiffs themselves use generative-A-I tools — citing public statements by Disney's chief executive, who has called the technology an invaluable tool for artists, and arguing that the plaintiffs cannot have it both ways. And that any infringement that does occur is the work of users prompting the system, not Midjourney itself. The fair-use argument is the substantive one. The user-prompt argument is the indemnification argument, and it is the one that publishers and content owners need to watch most carefully. If a court accepts that the model developer is not the proximate cause of infringement when the model itself is trained on infringing inputs, the entire architecture of the licensing market changes. Disney and Universal's complaint, and the court's denial of the motion to dismiss, are pushing back against that argument. The summary-judgment ruling, if the case gets there before the trial date, will be the most consequential A-I copyright ruling on the calendar this year.

The third thing. Class composition. The Midjourney case is not a class action. Disney and Universal are individual plaintiffs, with corporate affiliates joined for jurisdictional purposes. That matters because the relief, if granted, is specific to the named plaintiffs. It does not extend to other rights-holders by operation of law. A favorable ruling for Disney and Universal would, however, become persuasive precedent in any subsequent case brought by another studio, publisher, or rights-holder. And the procedural posture would make a follow-on case meaningfully easier to bring. The publisher community should think of this case as the doctrinal exhibit, not the recovery vehicle. The recovery vehicles for individual publishers and individual content owners are still being assembled.

Now the three concrete actions for an in-house counsel of a media company.

Action one. Run an internal audit of your archive. The question is not whether your content is in the public training corpora. The question is whether you can prove it. The audit needs to identify, for every piece of content your company has published in the past ten years, three things — the date of publication, the canonical U-R-L, and the metadata that uniquely identifies it. That archive needs to be in machine-readable form. The economic value of that archive, as a licensing asset, is now several multiples of what it was a year ago. The companies that have done this work are positioned. The companies that have not are not.

Action two. Assess the licensing posture. If your company has been approached by a frontier-model developer for a content-licensing deal — and many publishers have — the negotiating posture is now meaningfully different than it was eighteen months ago. The leverage is on the rights-holder side. Two factors drive that. First, the Bartz settlement, which on next Thursday's calendar will be on the verge of final approval, establishes a per-work valuation floor — three thousand dollars per work — that will be cited in negotiation. Second, the Midjourney case, depending on how it resolves, may establish that unlicensed training on copyrighted material is not fair use. If that ruling lands, the licensing market becomes the only market. Existing licensing agreements should be reviewed against that possibility. New agreements should preserve renegotiation rights tied to that case's outcome.

Action three. Update your terms of service and your editorial-licensing language. Many media companies' terms of service still permit downstream commercial use of content, including by third parties whose use cases were not contemplated when the terms were drafted. That language needs to be updated to expressly prohibit use of company content for the training of generative-A-I models without written license. The update is not retroactive — content already scraped is already scraped — but the prospective effect matters. It changes the legal posture in any future scraping-or-licensing dispute, and it establishes the contractual foundation for direct enforcement.

One additional observation on framing. The Midjourney case is being characterized in the trade press as a dispute about fan art and pop culture. That framing understates what is at stake. The dispute is about whether the training corpus of a commercial generative-A-I product is the kind of corpus that a content owner is entitled to license. If the answer is yes, the licensing market is the future of A-I-and-content economics. If the answer is no, the open-corpus model continues. The publisher community has a direct interest in the first outcome. The motion-to-dismiss order suggests the court has at least preserved the question. The summary-judgment briefing, when it lands, will be the inflection point. Expect filings in the second half of the year. The Disney and Universal complaint is one to read in full. The character imagery cited in the exhibits is the most legally adversarial set of training inputs that any frontier-model developer has had to defend in court. If they cannot defend that set, they cannot defend the rest.

[GC BENCH]

A short item this morning on artificial intelligence in the in-house function.

The question I keep getting from in-house teams is whether the multi-agent legal-A-I platforms — Lexis-plus with Protégé, Harvey, the in-house-specific entrants — are mature enough to commit to as the spine of a 2026 contract-review program. The honest answer is that the synthesis quality is, increasingly, table-stakes. The differentiation is now in two places. First, the audit trail. Second, the contractual posture on training-data exclusion. The audit trail is the artifact that matters for in-house liability management — the trace of which agent did what, against which data, against which authority, with citations back to the source. The training-data exclusion is the artifact that matters for preservation of the privilege. If your contract corpus is being used to train your vendor's model, the privilege analysis gets complicated, and the complications surface in discovery, not in the contracting cycle. Two specific questions to put to any vendor in this category. First, what does the trace look like for a single contract review — not the marketing screenshot, the actual production trace? Second, what is the contractual language on training-data exclusion, and is the exclusion verifiable through a third-party attestation? The first question is about defensibility of the work product. The second is about preservation of the privilege. If both answers are clean, the platform is ready for the spine role. If either is muddy, the platform is ready for a pilot but not for the spine.

Now the freight read.

Two threads. First. Trucking. SONAR data through late April, on the spot side, has the truckload tender rejection rate running near fourteen point four percent — a level that has not been consistently held since the immediate post-pandemic period of 2022. International Roadcheck Week — the Commercial Vehicle Safety Alliance's annual safety blitz — runs the week of May twelfth this year. Historically, that week pushes rejection rates into the mid-to-high teens, occasionally to the seventeen-percent range, as non-compliant trucks exit the network. The expectation should be that the published numbers for that week are read with the seasonal effect in mind. The underlying trend is that the open-deck and spot truckload markets are tightening into peak season, which is consistent with Craig Fuller's repeated commentary in late April that the multi-year freight recession has ended. For the FreightWaves audience, the editorial line through May should be that the recovery is real, but is being read against a noisy week of data. For commercial planning, the second-quarter advertising cycle for industrial-economy advertisers is the cycle to lean into.

Norfolk Southern's first-quarter results, released earlier this week, included an intermodal-revenue line that was modestly positive year over year, against a backdrop of weak coal volume and continued discipline on operating costs.
