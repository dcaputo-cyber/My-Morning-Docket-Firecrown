[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up. The Department of Justice's Google ad-tech remedies. On March thirty-first, Judge Leonie Brinkema in the Eastern District of Virginia let her own self-imposed deadline for the remedies ruling pass without a public filing. It is now April thirtieth. The ruling is expected at any moment. To recall the posture: in April of 2025, Judge Brinkema found that Google had illegally monopolized two markets — publisher ad servers, the product known as DoubleClick for Publishers, and the AdX ad exchange — and had illegally tied the two products together. The remedies phase concluded with closing arguments earlier this year. The Department of Justice asked for divestiture of AdX, open-sourcing of the DFP final auction logic, and conditional divestiture of the remainder of DFP. Google argued for behavioral remedies — bid-data sharing commitments, no divestitures. The court is now writing the order. Two practical implications for media-company general counsel. One. If your company is a publisher running advertising on either side of the Google ad-tech stack, your post-ruling integration roadmap is a legal item, not a commercial item. Sales operations and legal ops should be on the same call the morning the ruling lands. Two. The remedy, whatever it is, will reshape the brief-and-bid-data flow that every downstream demand-side platform depends on, and that means contractual exposure on every multi-year programmatic agreement your sales team has signed since 2024. Pull those contracts to the desk now and run change-of-control and material-adverse-change clauses against the realistic remedy scenarios. The ruling will not give you time to do that work after it lands.

Second. The California Privacy Protection Agency's data-broker enforcement push. CalPrivacy — the agency now uses that name in its public materials — opened 2026 with a strike-force posture on the Delete Act. The most recent round of public actions, brought in early April, includes more than ten enforcement matters against unregistered data brokers, with one matter, against a Texas-based reseller of personal information operating as Datamasters, settling for forty-five thousand dollars. The fines under the statute are two hundred dollars per day for failure to register, and the agency has been collecting them. Two operationally important dates beyond what's already on your calendar. The annual registration window for 2026 closed on January thirty-first — already passed — but if your company has any business-to-business data flow that meets the statutory definition of a data broker and you did not register, the daily-fine clock has been running for ninety days. Pull the registration question to a definitional memo this quarter. The other date that matters: starting August first, 2026, registered data brokers will be required to process consumer deletion requests through CalPrivacy's Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform — the DROP — every forty-five days. That is the operational obligation, and it is ninety-three days out. If your company is a registrant, the technical readiness work needs to be on a calendar.

Third. Connecticut. Attorney General William Tong this month released the office's updated report on the Connecticut Data Privacy Act, the second annual report since the law took effect. Two findings worth pulling out. One. By the end of 2025, the office had issued dozens of notices of violation and warning letters, finalized multiple data-breach settlements, and resolved its first formal enforcement action under the statute — an eighty-five-thousand-dollar settlement with a ticket seller over an unreadable privacy notice and a broken opt-out mechanism. The number is small. The precedent is what matters. Two. The Attorney General disclosed, for the first time, that the office has multiple active and ongoing investigations into the safety of children and teens online — across messaging platforms, gaming platforms, and chatbot services. That is a meaningful disclosure for any publisher of youth-adjacent content. Connecticut is the second-most-active state attorney general's office on consumer privacy after California, and the under-eighteen population on Trains, FLYING, Boating, and the model-railroading and junior-sailing community properties is exactly the cohort the office is investigating. The amendment to the Connecticut Act — lowering applicability thresholds and expanding the definition of sensitive data to include anything related to the processing of minors — takes effect later this year. Read the report. It is short. It is calibrated. It tells you what to expect.

Fourth. The Bartz settlement objections. On April eighth, Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín — the judge who took over the case after Judge Alsup's reassignment — moved the fairness hearing to May fourteenth at two p.m. Pacific. That date stands. On April fourteenth, the court ordered several previously sealed objections unsealed. The contents are now on the public docket. Three themes are worth flagging. First, notice failures. At least one objector certifies under penalty of perjury that they did not receive notice of the settlement until early March of 2026 — almost a full month after the opt-out deadline had expired. Second, the registration-collapsing methodology. Under the settlement formula, multiple books registered under a single group registration number collapse into one quote, claimable work, end quote. The objection is that authors who registered five novels as a group are being compensated for one. Third, the precedent objection. Several objectors argue, in essentially these words, that the settlement allows a multi-billion-dollar AI company to escape massive piracy at a discounted rate, setting a dangerous precedent that it is cheaper to steal human intellectual property and settle later than to license it fairly. That third objection is the one the courts in the parallel cases against OpenAI, Meta, Google, X-A-I, and Perplexity will be reading carefully. Fairness review is a substantive review. The judge has tools available to her short of approving or rejecting the settlement outright. She can require additional notice. She can require modification of the methodology. She can defer ruling. The May fourteenth hearing is not a sign-off ceremony. Plan around that.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to Maryland. The Maryland Online Data Privacy Act — MODPA — became enforceable on April first, 2026. The Act took technical effect on October first, 2025, but the legislature deferred enforcement by six months to give the Maryland Attorney General time to issue guidance and to give businesses time to comply. April first was the end of that runway. We are thirty days into enforcement. I want to spend the next ten or twelve minutes on it because, of the twenty state privacy laws now in effect in the United States, the Maryland statute is, in important respects, the most demanding — and the publisher implications are not the same as for any of the prior laws.

Let me set the scene. The Act, formally Senate Bill Five Forty-One of the 2024 session, was signed by Governor Wes Moore in May of 2024. The applicability threshold is the standard one — a business that controls or processes the personal data of at least thirty-five thousand Maryland consumers, or the personal data of at least ten thousand consumers and derives more than twenty percent of gross revenue from the sale of personal data, in a calendar year. The headline distinguishing features are five. First, a strict data-minimization standard. Second, a categorical prohibition on the sale of sensitive personal data, full stop. Third, a categorical prohibition on the sale of personal data of minors under eighteen and a categorical prohibition on processing the personal data of minors under eighteen for targeted advertising. Fourth, restrictions on consumer-health data and on geolocation data. And fifth, an algorithmic-decision-making transparency requirement that goes beyond what most state statutes require. Enforcement is by the Attorney General. There is a sixty-day cure period for first-time violations until October first of this year, after which the cure period sunsets. Civil penalties run up to ten thousand dollars per first violation and twenty-five thousand dollars per subsequent violation. There is no private right of action.

Let me take each of those distinguishing features in turn.

First, data minimization. Most state privacy laws frame the data-minimization principle as a soft proportionality test — process only what is reasonably necessary in relation to the purposes for which it is processed, with broad latitude in the definition of necessary. Maryland inverts the framing. The statute requires that the controller's collection of personal data be limited to what is reasonably necessary and proportionate to provide or maintain a specific product or service requested by the consumer. The anchor is the consumer-facing product or service. Not the controller's broader business. Not adjacent purposes. Not the platform-level economics. The product or service requested. That's a tighter test than appears in any other state law, and it is the test the Maryland Attorney General will be applying in the first wave of enforcement matters. For a multi-vertical publisher, the implication is concrete. Data collected for one product cannot quietly migrate into adjacent products without a separate consent transaction. Data collected by Trains cannot be repurposed for FreightWaves or for cross-property recommendations without express analysis. Each property needs to carry a Maryland-specific minimization argument that connects the data being collected to the consumer-facing product the consumer is using on that property.

Second, the categorical prohibition on the sale of sensitive personal data. Most state laws require opt-in consent before the sale of sensitive data. Maryland says no — the controller may not sell sensitive personal data, period, regardless of consent. The definition of sensitive data is broad and tracks the modern consensus — racial or ethnic origin, religious beliefs, mental or physical health diagnosis, sexual orientation, citizenship or immigration status, genetic or biometric data, precise geolocation, and personal data collected from a known child. For publishers, the operational question is whether your downstream sale architecture — your data partnerships, your audience-segment licensing, your contextual-syndication agreements — touches any of those categories at any point. Many of them do. Sensitive-data segments are quietly stitched into custom audience products at the platform level, and the contracts the publisher signs with the platform often contemplate use of that data. The Maryland prohibition does not turn on whether the publisher knew. It turns on whether the data, as a matter of fact, includes sensitive data, and whether it is being sold within the meaning of the statute. Both terms have technical definitions worth pulling.

Third, minors under eighteen. This is the provision that most acutely affects publishers. Maryland prohibits, categorically, both the sale of personal data of consumers known or who should be known to be under eighteen, and the processing of such consumers' data for targeted advertising. The age threshold — under eighteen — is meaningfully higher than the federal Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule, which applies to under thirteen, and meaningfully higher than the threshold in many state laws, which frame minor-protections at sixteen. The eighteen-year line means a much broader population is covered. The constructive-knowledge standard — should be known — means publishers cannot defend by saying the form did not collect age. If a property is reasonably likely to attract under-eighteen users, the publisher must treat the population as if some of those users are minors and operate the property accordingly. The Civil Air Patrol coverage on FLYING. The youth-enthusiast pages on Trains. The junior-sailing content on Boating. The career-day features on FreightWaves. Each of those is, on a Maryland-specific analysis, a property that should be known to attract under-eighteen users. Targeted advertising on those properties, served to Maryland users, is now categorically off the table. That is a revenue conversation as much as a legal one. Have the conversation.

Fourth, health data and geolocation. Maryland imposes strict consent requirements on the processing of consumer-health data and on the use of precise geolocation data. The consumer-health-data definition tracks the Washington My Health My Data Act framing — meaning, in practical effect, any data linked to a person that is reasonably interpretable as relating to physical or mental health, including data inferred from non-health sources. The breadth of the definition is the point. A reader's article-consumption history on a magazine property, if it includes a meaningful volume of health-adjacent content, can become consumer-health data under the statute. The publisher's obligation is to either limit the inference or to obtain affirmative consent for the processing. For most publishers, limiting the inference is the easier path. For larger publishers with more sophisticated data products, the consent path may be required, and it should be designed.

Fifth, algorithmic-decision transparency. Maryland requires controllers to disclose, in their privacy notice, the categories of personal data processed using profiling that produces decisions with legal or similarly significant effects on the consumer, and to provide consumers with the right to opt out of such profiling. For publishers, profiling that produces legal or significant effects is most often the personalization engine on subscription paywall pricing, the targeting algorithm on retention offers, and the eligibility logic on promotional offers. Each of those, depending on architecture, can fall within the scope. Document the analysis. The Maryland Attorney General will ask.

Three operational conclusions for a Firecrown-scale publisher.

The first conclusion. The Maryland statute is not a copy of any prior state law. The compliance work is not a check-the-box update to your existing California or Virginia or Connecticut posture. It is incremental work, and in some respects — the under-eighteen targeting prohibition, the categorical sensitive-data ban, the Maryland-specific minimization standard — it is materially harder. Run the gap analysis property-by-property this quarter. Do not assume your existing privacy program covers it.

The second conclusion. The cure period sunsets on October first. Until then, first-time violations get sixty days to cure. After October first, they don't. The next five months are the operational runway, and the runway is shorter than it sounds because the Attorney General has signaled that complex matters — the matters with regulatory implications across multiple jurisdictions — will not get the most generous read of the cure provision. The window is real. Use it.

The third conclusion. Maryland is the leading edge of a state-by-state pattern. The under-eighteen targeting prohibition has been adopted, in slightly different form, by Connecticut amendments now taking effect later this year, and is being introduced in legislatures in New York, Washington, and Illinois. The strict data-minimization framing in Maryland is showing up in proposed bills in Vermont, Massachusetts, and Oregon. The Maryland template is the new template. Build the program against Maryland and you are building it against the next four years of state legislation.

Read the statute. It is fifty-eight pages. Read the Maryland Attorney General's January twenty-eighth, 2026, guidance document, which is twenty-two pages and which sets out the office's enforcement posture. Then run the gap analysis. April first was the start of enforcement. October first is the end of the cure period. Five months.

[GC BENCH]

Now to GC Bench. Two items this morning.

The first item. Lexis-plus with Protégé became generally available in the United States in the third week of April. The release replaces the prior Lexis-plus AI product. The architecture is the part that's worth noting. Protégé deploys an orchestrator agent that decomposes a research task and routes the sub-tasks to three specialized agents — a legal-research agent, a web-search agent, and a customer-document agent. The agents collaborate, and the orchestrator manages the synthesis and the citation back to the user. For an in-house team, the meaningful change is not the underlying model. It is the workflow architecture. The first generation of legal AI tools — Lexis-plus AI, Casetext CoCounsel, the early Harvey configurations — were single-agent products. The user prompted, the model returned, the user iterated. The new generation is multi-agent. The user defines the task, the orchestrator manages the decomposition, the specialized agents run in parallel, and the result includes the trace. That trace — the audit trail of which agent did what, with which data, against which authority — is the artifact that matters for in-house liability management. If you are evaluating Protégé, the question is not whether the synthesis is good. The early reads from corporate-counsel teams suggest it is. The question is whether the trace, in your specific workflow, supports the documentation requirements your function has under outside-counsel guidelines, regulator expectations, or audit committees.

The second item. The Harvey funding round. Two hundred million dollars at an eleven-billion-dollar valuation, closed on March twenty-fifth, four months after the prior round. The capital is funding three things, in order of magnitude — engineering headcount, model-training compute, and an enterprise-sales build-out targeting Fortune-500 in-house teams. The eleven-billion-dollar valuation has implications for the broader market. It establishes Harvey as the platform-level competitor to LexisNexis and to Thomson Reuters in the legal-AI segment, and it tells you that the venture market believes the in-house buyer is the dominant buyer of legal AI for the next cycle, not the law-firm buyer. The data confirms the thesis. Corporate adoption moved from twenty-three percent to fifty-two percent year over year.
