First. The verdict in Musk versus Altman. On Monday afternoon, May eighteenth, an advisory jury sitting in the Northern District of California returned a verdict in favor of Sam Altman, OpenAI, and Microsoft, after less than two hours of deliberation. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers adopted the advisory verdict immediately from the bench. The jury did not reach the substantive question of whether the alleged 2015 agreement to operate OpenAI as a charitable nonprofit had been breached. The jury found, and the court adopted, that Mr. Musk's claims were barred by the three-year statute of limitations. Without the predicate claim for breach of charitable trust, the secondary claim against Microsoft for aiding and abetting failed as well. Mr. Musk has publicly characterized the ruling as a calendar technicality and has indicated an intention to appeal to the Ninth Circuit. The damages sought at trial were as much as one hundred thirty-four billion dollars, payable to the OpenAI nonprofit. For the in-house bar, the operational signal is not in the merits. It is in the speed. Less than two hours of deliberation, in a matter that turned on a single threshold question, in a courtroom where the substantive merits were the headline story going in. The lesson for any in-house counsel sitting on a long-tail breach-of-fiduciary or breach-of-trust theory is that the statute-of-limitations review is the first review, not the last review.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning is the Take It Down Act and what the first week of federal enforcement means for a media-company general counsel. I want to give you the architecture, the operational compliance components, the enforcement posture as the Federal Trade Commission has now telegraphed it, and three concrete actions.

The architecture first. Section three of the Take It Down Act, the section the Federal Trade Commission began enforcing on May nineteenth, imposes a notice-and-takedown obligation on what the statute calls covered platforms. The covered-platform definition is broad. It reaches consumer-facing websites, online services, online applications, and mobile applications that primarily provide a forum for user-generated content, that publish, curate, host, or make available content of nonconsensual intimate visual depictions. There is a small-platform exception, and there are exclusions for internet service providers, email providers, and certain platforms that exist primarily for direct messaging without a public-content function. For a media-company general counsel, the operative question is whether any of your owned-and-operated properties — your magazine websites, your enthusiast forums, your reader-comment sections, your reader-submitted-content tools, your podcast-comment sections — meet the covered-platform definition. The answer for most general-audience publishers is yes, at least with respect to some surface of your business. The audit question worth asking your product and engineering teams this morning is which surfaces, and what each surface's current intake architecture looks like.

The covered content is broader than the public framing suggests. It is not limited to images of identifiable adults. It reaches digital forgeries — images that were created or altered using software, an application, or artificial intelligence. The statutory definition does not require the depicted person to be identifiable to the general public. It requires that the depicted person be identifiable to a reasonable person from the image or the surrounding context. That definition was drafted with the contemporary generative-image environment in mind. The practical consequence is that a notice from a victim, accompanied by a synthetic image that the victim asserts depicts her, triggers the same removal obligation as a notice accompanied by a photograph. The platform is not the verifier of authenticity. The platform is the operator of a removal process that responds within forty-eight hours to a facially valid request.

The forty-eight-hour removal requirement is the headline operational obligation. The clock starts on receipt of a request that contains the statutorily required elements: a physical or electronic signature from the depicting person or her authorized representative, identification of the intimate visual depiction sufficient to permit the platform to locate it, a brief statement of the reason the depicted person has a good-faith belief that the content was published without consent, and the requestor's contact information. The platform must remove the content, and must remove identical or known-identical copies of the content, within forty-eight hours of receipt. The Federal Trade Commission's compliance guidance, published with the activation, is explicit that known-identical-copy removal includes hash-matching across the platform's surface, not only the specific uniform-resource-locator from which the notice was received. For platforms with a hash-matching capability already deployed for child-sexual-abuse-material compliance, the operational addition is bandwidth, not architecture. For platforms without that capability, the architecture work is the project.

The Commission's enforcement posture is consequential, and Chairman Ferguson made it explicit on Monday. The reminder letters to the fifteen major platforms framed compliance as a baseline expectation. The warning letters to the twelve nudify companies framed continued operation without a removal-request mechanism as a per-se violation. The Commission's compliance guidance is unusually prescriptive for first-week enforcement. The enforcement penalty under Section five of the Federal Trade Commission Act, as applied to Take It Down Act violations, is fifty-three thousand and eighty-eight dollars per violation. The Commission has not yet announced its first formal enforcement action. The expectation among the bar, based on the warning-letter cohort, is that the first enforcement target will be one of the nudify companies, not one of the major platforms, but the major platforms' compliance status is now a federal-enforcement matter rather than a self-regulatory matter.

Two operational components are worth emphasizing. The first is the false-request risk. The statute includes a provision protecting platforms that act in good faith on requests that turn out to be invalid. The Federal Trade Commission's guidance reinforces that the platform is not required to adjudicate authenticity. The platform is required to operate a removal process. The corollary, however, is that bad-faith takedown requests — requests aimed at suppressing lawful, non-intimate, non-victim-related content — are an operational risk for any platform whose intake architecture does not include a sanity check at the front end. The platforms that have been operating notice-and-takedown systems under Section five-twelve of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act for the past quarter-century have well-developed sanity-check procedures. The platforms that are building this capability from scratch this month are working under a tighter compliance window than the Digital Millennium Copyright Act regime allows. The second operational component is the cross-jurisdictional overlap with state laws. Texas, Florida, and Illinois already have nonconsensual-intimate-imagery statutes with private rights of action and state-attorney-general enforcement. The Take It Down Act does not preempt those statutes. The Take It Down Act creates a federal enforcement floor. The state-law layer, with its private-cause-of-action exposure and its damages calculations, remains. The compliance architecture you build for the federal regime needs to be the same architecture that satisfies the state regimes, or the compliance work fragments at the operational layer.

Three concrete actions for a general counsel, before this deep dive closes. First, audit the covered-platform status of each of your owned-and-operated properties. Document the analysis. The covered-platform determination is the first question any future enforcement action will ask, and the contemporaneous documentation of your reasoned analysis is the first line of defense if the determination is later contested. Second, confirm the existence and the operational responsiveness of your notice-and-takedown intake mechanism for nonconsensual intimate imagery on every surface that has been determined to be a covered platform. The forty-eight-hour clock is short. The intake mechanism cannot be a generic abuse-reporting form that is routed through a manual content-moderation queue with a multi-day service-level agreement. The Take It Down Act regime requires a dedicated intake path, a defined service-level agreement, and a logging mechanism that produces the evidence necessary to defend a future enforcement action. Third, confirm with your platform-engineering or vendor-management team that hash-matching across the surface is functioning, that the hash library is updated on the receipt of each takedown, and that the cross-surface matching captures the known-identical-copy population the statute requires. If any of those three components are not in place this morning, the project plan for putting them in place needs to be on your desk by close of business today.

On GC Bench this morning, a note on contract review and the adoption gap, drawn from the cohort of in-house-counsel surveys that landed during the first quarter and through April. The headline data point is that fifty-two percent of in-house legal teams are now using or actively evaluating artificial intelligence for contract review, with active usage having nearly quadrupled since 2024. Eighty-seven percent of the surveyed in-house cohort report that artificial intelligence would benefit contract review and redlining, the highest category-specific endorsement in the survey set. Seventy-nine percent report reduced time on routine legal tasks. Sixty-seven percent report faster response to internal business clients. The figure I have flagged in prior briefings — eighty-seven percent of general counsel reporting some use of generative artificial intelligence — has now stratified into a more useful set of sub-figures by use case.

The strategic data point, however, is different. Sixty-four percent of in-house teams now expect to reduce outside-counsel spend as a function of in-house artificial-intelligence deployment. That is the figure that should be on the agenda of your next outside-counsel-management conversation. The historical posture of in-house artificial-intelligence procurement — the question being whether the in-house team would buy tools that the law firms would also buy — is being inverted. The question is increasingly whether the in-house team will use its own tools to perform work that the firms have historically performed, and whether the firms' billable-hour leverage will adjust to accommodate the shift. For a media-company general counsel managing a panel of three to five primary firms, the conversation worth having before the end of the second quarter is the conversation about which work continues to be sent out, which work moves in-house against the new capability, and what the corresponding rate-card and engagement-letter adjustments look like. The firms that are positioning well are the firms that have already moved their internal staffing model to anticipate the shift. The firms that are positioning badly are the firms whose response to the question is to discount the rate without restructuring the work. The procurement signal to send the panel, in writing, before the end of June, is the signal that defines the relationship for the back half of the year.

One observation on procurement. The current substrate-versus-application question — the question of whether to anchor on a frontier-model vendor's enterprise legal product, or to anchor on an established legal-technology suite from Thomson Reuters or LexisNexis, or to maintain a multi-vendor posture across both — is the most consequential procurement decision an in-house technology budget will make in 2026.
