[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. The General Motors settlement. On May eighth, the California Attorney General's office, joined by several county district attorneys and the California Privacy Protection Agency, announced a twelve-million-seven-hundred-fifty-thousand-dollar settlement with General Motors resolving allegations that the company sold the driving data of hundreds of thousands of California motorists to data brokers without consumer consent. The settlement is the largest California Consumer Privacy Act penalty to date and — this is the part to carry — the first stand-alone data-minimization enforcement action in the state's history. The theory of the case is that General Motors collected more data than was reasonably necessary for the stated purpose of the consumer relationship, and that the resale of that data was, on that theory alone, a Consumer Privacy Act violation independent of any consent or notice failure. The settlement also covers the General Motors-affiliate OnStar telematics business and includes a five-year compliance audit obligation supervised by the Attorney General's office. For any publisher whose audience-data operations collect telemetric, behavioral, or device-level data at granularity beyond what the product actually requires — and that is most large-scale publisher operations — the operational implication is immediate. The proportionality question is now an enforcement question. The first scoping conversation belongs on the legal-operations calendar before the end of this quarter, not at year-end.

Second. Claude For Legal. Anthropic launched its enterprise legal product on Tuesday, May twelfth, in a tightly coordinated rollout that included twelve practice-area-specific plug-ins — corporate, commercial, regulatory, employment, privacy, intellectual property, litigation, plus modules for legal clinics and law students — and integration agreements with more than twenty established legal-technology suppliers, including the Thomson Reuters CoCounsel platform, the LexisNexis Protégé family, and the Harvey workflow surface. Freshfields, Quinn Emanuel, Holland and Knight, and Crosby Legal were named among the launch customers. More than twenty thousand legal professionals registered for the announcement webinar. The strategic picture, in plain language, is that the frontier-model defendant in the largest copyright settlement in United States history is now also the substrate vendor that every Am Law firm and most in-house departments will be evaluating against an existing CoCounsel or LexisNexis subscription within the next two procurement cycles. We will spend more time on this on GC Bench. For now, the operational signal is that the procurement question worth asking is not which best-of-breed tool to buy, but which substrate to anchor on.

Third. Texas. The Office of the Attorney General issued enforcement press releases on May eleventh and May twelfth involving privacy and biometric-data matters, continuing what is now a sustained cadence of public-facing action under the Texas Data Privacy and Security Act and the parallel biometric and data-broker statutes. The Texas enforcement frame, for those tracking the comparative posture across states, differs from California's in three ways. First, the Texas Attorney General has exclusive enforcement authority — there is no parallel agency to the California Privacy Protection Agency. Second, the civil-penalty cap is seven-thousand-five-hundred dollars per violation, and the Office has been counting violations aggressively in initial demands. Third, the enforcement priorities have weighted toward biometric-identifier matters, toward direct-to-consumer genetic testing, and toward the recently active data-broker registration framework, which sent compliance notices to more than one hundred companies in February. If your audience-data operations touch Texas residents and include any biometric capture — face match, voice match, fingerprint, retina, any of the protected modalities — the Texas posture is increasingly the second regulatory exposure to manage alongside California.

Fourth. The Federal Trade Commission. The Commission's Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on negative-option practices, which was published on March eleventh and which restarted the regulatory process after the Eighth Circuit's vacatur of the original Click-to-Cancel rule last summer, is now in the staff-evaluation phase. The Commission has not signaled a target date for a proposed rule. In parallel, the Commission has continued to litigate negative-option matters under existing Section Five authority, with the Match Group and OkCupid action filed in March now in active discovery, the Uber suit expanded with civil-penalty claims, and the Match case in particular elevating the question of how dating, social, and subscription-content services treat the cancellation pathway as a deceptive practice. For any subscription-driven publisher, the operative posture is unchanged. The absence of a finalized federal rule does not soften the enforcement posture. The cancellation-workflow audit, if it has not been run in the past twelve months, is overdue.

Fifth. The Court of Justice of the European Union and the Meta-AGCOM follow-on. Following last Tuesday's ruling in the Meta versus AGCOM matter — which we covered in last Friday's deep dive — the Hungarian referral in Like Company versus Google Ireland is now scheduled by the Court for oral argument in the second half of this year. The Like Company case asks the Court a different but parallel question: whether the reproduction and public display of press content by a large-language model — the kind of output a generative-search interface presents to a user — infringes copyright and related rights. The Court's eventual ruling will not arrive before the end of 2026. The relevance for an American publisher general counsel is that the European Union is now the jurisdiction most actively building case law on the boundary between training-side and serving-side liability for generative-search products. The European doctrinal output will, over the next eighteen to twenty-four months, be the most cited reference frame in the parallel American cases — including the consolidated Times-OpenAI matter and the pending Concord-Music and Universal-Music actions.

Sixth. The California Privacy Protection Agency's Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform — the system known by the acronym D-R-O-P — has been live for consumer use since January first. The August first compliance deadline is seventy-five days from this morning. From that date forward, every registered data broker in California must access the platform at least once every forty-five days, must process consumer deletion requests within ninety days, and must report request status back into the system. The data-broker registry stands at approximately five hundred companies as of the most recent agency disclosure. The proportionality question I noted under the General Motors item — whether the data your business collects is in fact necessary for the stated purpose — is also the gating question for whether your business meets the statutory definition of a data broker. If the answer is uncertain, the time to clarify is before the August first commencement, not after.

[DEEP DIVE]

The deep dive this morning is the General Motors settlement and the data-minimization theory of liability — the architecture of the case, the three operational consequences, and three concrete actions for a media-company general counsel.

The architecture first. General Motors, through its OnStar telematics subsidiary, equips its vehicles with the in-cabin sensors and connected-services architecture that the modern automotive industry has built around: location, route history, hard-braking events, speed profiles, accelerometer data, seat-belt usage, fuel and battery telemetry. The data is captured continuously during vehicle operation. General Motors' position, before this settlement, was that the data collection was within the scope of the consumer relationship — the consumer purchased a vehicle, the consumer activated OnStar, the consumer received the value of the connected services in exchange for the data. The Attorney General's investigation, opened in 2024, concluded that the practical relationship was different. The consumer did not understand the secondary commercial uses of the data. The consumer did not consent to the resale of driving-behavior data to data brokers, including data brokers selling to insurance carriers that then used the data to set personalized auto-insurance rates. And — this is the new theory — even if some level of telemetric data was reasonably necessary to the connected-services value proposition, the volume and granularity of the data collected exceeded that necessity. The collection was, on that theory alone, a violation of the Consumer Privacy Act's data-minimization provision.

That is the architecture. Now the three operational consequences.

First. The data-minimization theory is the first stand-alone enforcement theory in the four-year operational history of the California Consumer Privacy Act. Every prior enforcement action — Sephora, Honda, Tractor Supply, the Disney and ABC matter, the streaming-service action in February that briefly broke the Tractor Supply record — proceeded on a consent, notice, opt-out, or rights-request theory. Each of those theories required the agency to prove that the consumer had been deprived of a procedural right. The data-minimization theory does not require that proof. It requires only that the agency show the data collected was more than was reasonably necessary for the purpose. That is a substantively different burden, and it shifts the operational defensibility of an audience-data program from a notice-and-consent question to a necessity question.

Second. The settlement requires General Motors to implement a five-year compliance audit obligation supervised by the Attorney General's office. The audit framework, when it becomes public, will be the de facto compliance template the office and the Privacy Protection Agency apply to every subsequent matter of comparable scope. Watch the audit framework closely. The framework will define, in operational language, what evidence of necessity will be required, what evidence of data destruction will be required, and what evidence of restriction on secondary use will be required. The framework will be cited in private litigation as well — the consumer-class actions that follow the public enforcement matters will use the audit framework as the benchmark.

Third. The data-broker resale element of the case is operationally distinct from the collection element. The Attorney General's complaint emphasized the resale of driving-behavior data to insurance-industry data brokers. The economic harm — consumers whose insurance rates rose because the data broker had sold information about hard-braking events to the carrier — was the consumer-facing injury that gave the case its public weight. For a publisher operating an audience-data program, the analogous element is the resale chain. If your audience data flows to a downstream measurement, attribution, or audience-extension vendor that subsequently sells the data into a market segment where the consumer experiences economic consequences — and the prototypical example is the placement of those audiences into insurance, employment, credit, or housing decisions — the resale chain becomes the heightened exposure surface.

Now the three concrete actions for a general counsel.

Action one. Run the necessity audit. The audit should be conducted by your privacy team in conjunction with your audience-development and analytics teams, and it should identify, for every category of data collected on every consumer-facing property, the stated purpose of that collection, the actual operational use of the data, and the gap between the stated purpose and the actual use. The output is a heat map. The heat map is the prioritization document. The categories where the gap is largest, where the operational use is least defensible against the stated purpose, are the categories to triage first.

Action two. Review the downstream chain. The contractual question to put to every measurement, attribution, audience-extension, and identity-resolution vendor on your site is whether the vendor's downstream resale practices include any segment that maps to a consumer-facing economic decision. The contractual question is — in plain English — does this vendor sell my audience to anyone who is going to use it to set a price for a financial product, an insurance product, a job offer, or a housing offer. If the answer is yes, or if the answer is uncertain, the contracting language needs to include either a categorical exclusion or a notice-and-opt-out at the source.

Action three. Document the framework. The General Motors audit obligation will produce, over the next twelve to twenty-four months, a documentary record of what a defensible necessity analysis looks like. Your privacy team should be tracking that documentary record as it becomes public. The benchmarks set in that record will become the standard the agencies apply in every adjacent matter. The companies that have built their compliance posture against the published benchmark are positioned. The companies that have built their compliance posture against an internal best guess are exposed.

One additional note on jurisdiction. The General Motors settlement is a California matter, but the data-minimization theory is now also the leading edge of the Texas, Oregon, Connecticut, and Colorado privacy frameworks. The Texas Attorney General's enforcement priorities, the Oregon Department of Justice's recent rights-request guidance, and the Connecticut Attorney General's pre-effective-date posture on the July amendments all engage the necessity question. The General Motors theory is exportable. The General Motors theory will, within twelve months, be the operative theory of liability in three additional state-enforcement frameworks.

The legal-technology item this morning is the Claude For Legal launch — and the question is what it means for a media-company general counsel making a procurement decision in the second half of this year.

The product architecture first. Claude For Legal is, in substance, twelve practice-area plug-ins — corporate, commercial, regulatory, employment, privacy, intellectual property, litigation, plus modules for clinics, law schools, and underserved-population matters — running on top of the Claude foundation model, with more than twenty integration points into the existing legal-technology ecosystem. The integration list includes Thomson Reuters CoCounsel, LexisNexis Protégé, the Harvey workflow surface, the iManage document-management substrate, the Litera and NetDocuments families, and the major contract-lifecycle-management platforms. The launch customer list — Freshfields, Quinn Emanuel, Holland and Knight, Crosby Legal — covers the Am Law upper tier. The market positioning is explicit. Anthropic is not competing with Thomson Reuters and LexisNexis at the workflow layer. Anthropic is positioning Claude as the substrate that Thomson Reuters, LexisNexis, Harvey, and the workflow vendors will compete to build on top of.

The procurement implication, for a media-company general counsel, is that the substrate decision is the foundational decision. The workflow layer is where most legal-technology evaluations have been spent over the past two years — which platform drafts contracts, which platform reviews documents, which platform conducts research. The substrate decision underneath those workflows is the decision that will, over the next thirty-six months, determine which workflow vendors a legal department can integrate cleanly with, and at what cost. The frontier-model field has, for practical procurement purposes, narrowed. Anthropic's launch consolidates one substrate. OpenAI's parallel positioning, through the partnership with Microsoft on the Copilot family and through direct enterprise sales of GPT-class models, consolidates another. The procurement evaluation needs to address both.

The compliance angle is also worth noting. Anthropic has been, since the Bartz settlement, the frontier-model developer with the most explicit posture on training-data provenance, on output-fidelity testing, and on enterprise indemnification — the contractual commitments that, for an in-house counsel, are the gating questions on whether a tool can be deployed in a regulated workflow. The Claude For Legal launch documentation, which is now public, contains specific contractual language on those questions. The language is a useful comparison reference for any evaluation of a competing substrate.

One additional operational note. The eighty-seven-percent adoption number that I covered earlier this month — general counsel using generative artificial intelligence in some form — is now matched, on the trust-gap data, by a measurable bifurcation. The cohort reporting high trust shows an eighty-nine-percent positive return on investment. The cohort reporting low trust shows a twenty-seven-percent positive return. The bifurcation is not driven by access. It is driven by the rigor of the verification step the team applies. The Claude For Legal launch, in its product design, is built around an explicit verification surface — the user can see, on every output, the source citations and the model's confidence assessment. The design choice is operationally significant. The verification step is the conversion mechanism between access and return.

A freight note before the closing brief.

The Association of American Railroads' weekly traffic report for the week ending May ninth, published on Wednesday, reported United States intermodal volume at two-hundred-eighty-four-thousand-one-hundred-sixty-three containers and trailers, up four percent year over year and the fourth consecutive week of mid-single-digit growth. The week-ending-May-second print was almost the same — two-hundred-eighty-three-thousand-seven-hundred-twenty-four units, also up three-point-nine percent. Cumulative intermodal for the first eighteen weeks of 2026 is up six-tenths of a percent against the prior-year cumulative, which had been negative through most of the first quarter. The volume direction is confirmed.

The trucking side carries an additional data point. FreightWaves' tender-rejection series — the operational signal for capacity tightness in the truckload spot market — bottomed on May fourteenth, according to commentary that ran on the platform over the weekend. The reading on tender rejections, which had been declining as capacity returned to the market through April and early May, has begun to firm. National spot rates are holding at approximately two-dollars-eighty cents per mile inclusive of fuel, up twenty-three percent from the same point a year ago. The combination of a tender-rejection inflection and an intermodal-volume confirmation is, in plain language, the cleanest signal of a freight-cycle turn since the post-pandemic correction began.

For Trains and FreightWaves editorial, the manufacturing-and-construction freight thesis continues to firm. The volume direction first appeared in late March. The rate direction first appeared in late April. The tender-rejection inflection on May fourteenth is the third independent confirmation. The aviation read remains the United Parcel Service hearing tomorrow, where the substantive question is mechanical and the editorial demand is high. The marine read remains, on the commercial side, the National Marine Manufacturers Association's mixed-conditions guidance — recreational powerboat sales projected on par to slightly up against a 2025 base of two-hundred-fifteen to two-hundred-twenty-five thousand units, with affordable aluminum-fishing and value-segment categories continuing to outperform.
