[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]
First up, and the anchor for today: the Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule amendments and the paired age-verification policy statement. The amendments became effective on April twenty-second. They are the first revisions to the rule since 2013. Four operational changes are worth carrying around. First, a strict data-retention prohibition. Operators may no longer retain children's personal information indefinitely, and may retain it only for as long as reasonably necessary to fulfill the specific purpose for which it was collected. Second, an expanded definition of personal information that covers biometric identifiers and government-issued identification numbers. Third, a separate verifiable-parental-consent requirement for the disclosure of children's personal information to third parties for targeted advertising and similar purposes — the prior rule treated disclosure as part of the same consent transaction as collection, and the amendments split them. Fourth, new transparency obligations on Safe Harbor programs, which must now publicly disclose membership lists and submit triennial reports on technological capabilities to the Commission. The policy statement, issued February tenth, is the part that has not gotten enough attention. The Commission stated that it will not bring enforcement actions under the rule against operators who collect, use, and disclose personal information for the sole purpose of determining a user's age via age-verification technologies. That is, in plain language, a federal-level safe harbor for age-gating operations that do nothing else with the data. I will spend more time on this in the deep dive.

Second. The Google Play Store settlement. On April thirtieth, the federal court approved the seven-hundred-million-dollar multistate settlement resolving the antitrust action brought by fifty-two state attorneys general against Google over Android app distribution and in-app billing. The Delaware Attorney General's office confirmed final approval in an announcement on Monday, May fourth. The settlement covers consumers who made purchases on the Google Play Store between August 2016 and September 2023. The behavioral relief — which is the part that matters operationally — requires Google to give users greater choice in how they download apps and how they pay for them on Android, including expanded permissions for third-party app stores and alternative billing systems. For publishers running Android applications, the practical question is whether your distribution model has been depending on the Play Store choke points the settlement is opening. If it has, the next ninety days are the window in which to evaluate whether to add a direct-distribution channel, an alternative payment processor, or both. Apple is on a parallel trajectory but on a slightly later timeline, and the Northern District of California's continuing supervision of the Epic injunction is the docket to watch there.

Third. The California Privacy Protection Agency. The Agency's preliminary comment period on data-broker audits closes tomorrow, Thursday May seventh, at five o'clock Pacific. We have flagged this date for ten days. The substantive question on the table — what the audit regime under the Delete Act will require, in terms of frequency, scope, internal-versus-external auditor designation, and the role of the data-broker registry — is the operational shape of CalPrivacy enforcement against the registrant population for the next twelve to eighteen months. If your organization is registered as a data broker, or if your audience-monetization stack puts you within shouting distance of the statutory definition, the comment is worth filing. The Agency reads its own comments. The shape of the final rule will reflect the operational realities the registrants describe.

Fourth. Connecticut. The amendments to the Connecticut Data Privacy Act, signed into law in 2025 and becoming effective on July first of this year, will fundamentally change the posture for publishers reaching minors in the state. Two provisions matter. First, a ban on targeted advertising directed at minors under sixteen, regardless of consent. Second, a categorical prohibition on the sale of minors' personal data, again under sixteen, regardless of consent. The Connecticut framework is, in this respect, ahead of the broader federal regime — the Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule amendments still treat thirteen as the operative threshold — and aligned with Maryland's eighteen-year line. The Connecticut Attorney General's office has signaled in three communications since February that the office is opening active investigations into platforms that exposed children and teens to privacy and security risks online. The combined picture, for any publisher with an audience that reasonably reaches Connecticut users under sixteen, is that targeted-advertising revenue from that segment becomes operationally untenable on July first. The conversation between sales and legal needs to happen before the end of this quarter.

Fifth. Bartz. The fairness hearing in the Northern District of California, before Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín, is on Thursday, May fourteenth, at two o'clock Pacific. Eight days from this morning. There is a fresh procedural development worth flagging. On April fourteenth, the court ordered several previously sealed objections unsealed in response to a motion brought by Professor Lea Victoria Bishop, herself an objector. The unsealed contents now provide the public, and the parallel litigation against OpenAI, Meta, Google, X-A-I, and Perplexity, with the most concrete picture available of the substantive challenges to the settlement methodology — particularly the registration-collapsing rule under which multiple books registered as a group collapse into one quote, claimable work, end quote. Class members who have timely objected may join the hearing by Zoom. The judge has the full range of fairness-hearing tools available — approve, modify, defer, or reject. Treat the May fourteenth date as the substantive review the court has signaled it will be.

Sixth. The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act. The Commission's enforcement powers under the Act commence on August second. That is eighty-eight days from this morning. The Act has been in staged commencement since 2024. The general-purpose AI obligations entered into application in August of last year. What changes on August second is the Commission's ability to issue penalties, which under the Act may run up to seven percent of global annual turnover for the most serious violations involving prohibited practices, and up to three percent for violations of obligations applicable to providers of general-purpose AI models. For publishers, the operational angle is narrower than the headline number suggests, and it is worth understanding. If your organization is a provider of a general-purpose AI model — which most publishers are not — the obligation set is direct. If your organization is a deployer of such a model in the European Union, your obligations attach through the systems integrator, but contractual flow-down terms matter. The relevant contracting move, if you have not already made it, is to require your AI vendors to certify their compliance with the Act's general-purpose-model obligations, and to flow that certification through to the deployer-side documentation you maintain.

Seventh and final headline. Aviation. The ALERT Act passed the House on April fourteenth, three hundred ninety-six to ten, and is now in conference with the Senate's Rotorcraft Operations Transparency and Oversight Reform Act — known in shorthand as the ROTOR Act — which cleared the Senate in late 2025. The conference is staffed and active. The reconciliation question is mostly about scope. The House bill addresses ADS-B In requirements and helicopter-route safety; the Senate bill goes further on rotorcraft operational reporting. The likely emergence of a final bill is on a calendar that runs through the end of June, in advance of the National Transportation Safety Board investigative hearing on the January 2025 Reagan National midair collision, which is on the calendar for May nineteenth and twentieth. Adjacent in the FAA's calendar this month is an airworthiness directive against Boeing seven-eighty-seven aircraft, effective April sixteenth, that addresses loss of transponder function in the presence of continuous-wave interference — a different issue from the cargo-liner-tape, fuel-tank-sealant, and cabin-temperature directives I covered yesterday, and one that touches a population of approximately one hundred fifty United-States-registered aircraft. For aviation publishers, the FAA docket continues to thicken.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning is the Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule amendments and the paired Federal Trade Commission policy statement on age-verification technologies. I want to give you the architecture, the four operational changes, the policy-statement implications, and three concrete actions for a media-company general counsel.

The architecture first. The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act was enacted in 1998. The implementing rule has been amended once, in 2013, to extend the rule's coverage to mobile applications and to expand the definition of personal information. The 2026 amendments, which became effective April twenty-second, are the second amendment cycle. The Federal Trade Commission published the final rule in the Federal Register on April twenty-second, 2025, with a one-year compliance runway. That runway has now closed. The amendments apply to operators of websites and online services directed to children under thirteen, and to operators with actual knowledge that they are collecting personal information from a child under thirteen. The applicability test is unchanged. What changes is what the rule requires.

Now the four operational changes.

Change one. Data retention. The prior rule was silent on retention. The amendments are not. Operators may retain children's personal information only for as long as reasonably necessary to fulfill the specific purpose for which it was collected. The published commentary is explicit that indefinite retention, retention for unspecified future business purposes, or retention beyond the conclusion of the consumer relationship that triggered the collection, is no longer permitted. The operational implication, for a publisher with any audience-data store that includes data collected from children — registration data, contest entries, fan-submission archives, comment-section histories, photo galleries, prior-product enrollment records — is that you need a retention schedule that maps each data category to a specific purpose and to a specific retention horizon. The schedule needs to be in writing, it needs to be enforced by a data-management process, and it needs to be auditable. The Commission has signaled that retention is the first item on its enforcement priority list under the amended rule. If your organization does not have a children's-data retention schedule today, that is the first project, and the project cannot wait through the end of the quarter.

Change two. The expanded definition of personal information. The amendments add biometric identifiers and government-issued identification numbers to the definition. Biometric identifiers include voiceprints, gait patterns, and facial-geometry data — the categories that have proliferated in connected devices, in smart-toy platforms, and in voice-interface integrations. The implication for publishers is most direct for any property that runs interactive video features, voice-activated content, or smart-device integrations targeted at younger audiences. If your model-railroading platform has a voice-controlled engine demonstration, the voiceprint is now within scope. If your sailing publication runs a youth-skill-tracking interactive that uses gait or motion data, the data is now within scope. Do the inventory.

Change three. The separate parental-consent requirement for third-party disclosure for targeted advertising and similar purposes. Under the prior rule, an operator could obtain a single verifiable parental consent that covered both the operator's own use of the personal information and the disclosure of that information to third parties for advertising and analytics. The amendments require those consents to be separated. A parent must be able to consent to the operator's use of the data and to refuse consent to third-party disclosure for advertising — and, critically, the operator must continue to provide the underlying service without conditioning service provision on the third-party-disclosure consent. The advertising-revenue implication is direct. If your monetization model on a child-directed property depended on the prior single-consent regime, the model needs to be re-architected. The technical implementation requires consent-management infrastructure that can capture, store, and propagate the disjunction. Most off-the-shelf consent-management platforms now support this; some do not. The vendor-evaluation question is whether your platform supports separate, granular consent for COPPA-scoped third-party disclosures, with audit trail.

Change four. Safe-Harbor program transparency. The Commission has long approved Safe Harbor programs — operated by industry trade groups — that provide a self-regulatory pathway for COPPA compliance. The amendments require approved Safe Harbor programs to publicly disclose membership lists, to submit triennial reports on technological capabilities, and to disclose annually disciplinary actions taken against member operators. For media organizations that are members of an approved Safe Harbor program, the practical implication is that membership in such a program is now a matter of public record. The reputational calculus around Safe Harbor membership changes when membership is published. Confirm your status. Confirm whether the program you are in remains a fit, given the disclosure regime.

Now the policy statement on age-verification technologies. The Commission issued the statement on February tenth. The substantive content is short and the operational content is meaningful. The Commission stated that it will not bring enforcement actions under the rule against operators who collect, use, and disclose personal information for the sole purpose of determining a user's age via age-verification technologies — provided that the data is used only for that purpose, is not retained beyond what is necessary to perform the verification, and is not disclosed to third parties for other purposes. The statement is, in effect, a federal-level safe harbor for age-gating data flows. It does not preempt state law, and the state-law mosaic — Maryland's eighteen-year line, Connecticut's sixteen-year line, the European Union's evolving age-assurance posture — continues to operate. But it removes a meaningful federal-level legal risk for operators who deploy age verification at the front door of their service.

The implication for publishers is the one I want to end on. If your organization has been deferring age-gating because the data flow itself triggered COPPA exposure, the policy statement removes that consideration. The rule, as amended, now treats age-verification data as functionally separate from substantive personal-information data, provided the operator handles it in the prescribed way. That changes the cost-benefit on age-gating implementation, and it should change the calendar.

Three concrete general-counsel actions, before the deep dive closes.

One. Build the children's-data inventory and retention schedule before the end of the second quarter. The retention obligation is the leading edge of the Commission's enforcement posture. An auditable schedule, with named owners and bounded horizons, is the documentation that will matter when the Commission asks. If your organization does not have one, build one. If your organization has one that pre-dates the amendments, refresh it.

Two. Pull every consent flow on every child-directed property and confirm the separation of consents. The single-consent flows that were compliant on April twenty-first are non-compliant on April twenty-second. The remediation work is technical, but the gating function is legal. Your trust-and-safety team and your engineering team need a deadline, and the deadline should be the end of the second quarter.

Three. Take a fresh look at age-gating. The policy statement opens a door that was either closed or assumed to be closed. The deferred age-gating projects that have been in the engineering backlog — the ones that got deprioritized because the data flow was assumed to trigger COPPA — should come off the bench. The federal-level cost has come down. The state-level pressure, particularly from Maryland and Connecticut, has gone up. The implementation calendar should reflect both.

One additional jurisdictional note. The amendments do not directly address the question of teens fourteen through seventeen, which the federal Kids Online Safety and Privacy Act would have addressed but which has not passed. The state-level pattern — Maryland, Connecticut, and the proposed bills in New York, Washington, and Illinois — is the operative framework for the teen population. The rule amendments are the federal floor. The state-level regime, particularly for advertising and for sale of personal data, is the operational ceiling. Build to the ceiling.

The number — one and a half billion dollars, three thousand dollars per claimable work, ninety-one-point-three percent of the eligible class claimed — is a reference price. The second note. Documentation discipline. We have been talking about written technology roadmaps for in-house teams in the past several editions. One adjacent observation. The volume of preservation-relevant material that crosses an in-house team's desk in 2026 is materially higher than it was two years ago. Discovery in the AI-training litigation has produced opinions in the Northern District of California that read carefully on what counts as a litigation hold trigger, on what counts as a foreseeable-litigation-related document, and on the affirmative duty to preserve in the context of generative-artificial-intelligence outputs. The OpenAI privacy-gambit ruling — twenty million ChatGPT logs ordered produced — is the most concrete recent example. The implication is operational. Your in-house team's litigation-hold protocols, your preservation notices, your custodial-interview templates — all of this should have been refreshed in the past twelve months.
