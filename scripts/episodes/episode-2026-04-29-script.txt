[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up, and the anchor for today: the European Commission's preliminary findings against TikTok and Meta. On April fifteenth, 2026, the Commission preliminarily found both platforms in breach of their obligations under the Digital Services Act on two fronts. First, both companies were found to have obstructed researcher access to public platform data, in apparent breach of Article Forty of the Act, which requires Very Large Online Platforms to provide vetted researchers with access to data necessary to study systemic risks. The Commission's preliminary view is that the application interfaces and request workflows the platforms built were burdensome, slow, and produced partial or unreliable data. Second — and this finding applies to Meta specifically, on Facebook and Instagram — the Commission preliminarily found that the platforms failed to provide users with simple, accessible mechanisms to flag illegal content under the Notice and Action regime, and that the design of those mechanisms relied on patterns the Commission characterized as dark patterns. Maximum fines under the Act run up to six percent of annual worldwide turnover. The preliminary findings are not a final order. The platforms will respond. But the direction of travel is the second enforcement wave we have been flagging for the past three weeks. It has arrived. I will spend the deep dive on what it means in practice.

Second: Regulation S-K. The Securities and Exchange Commission's comment window on the comprehensive Reg S-K review closed on April thirteenth. The Division of Corporation Finance is now processing what counts of the public file already suggest is the largest comment volume on a disclosure-architecture rulemaking since the early 2000s, with seventy-plus comment letters specifically on Item Four-Oh-Two, the executive compensation disclosure rules, on top of the broader Reg S-K file. The signal from Chairman Atkins's January thirteenth statement and from staff commentary at the spring securities conferences is that the proposed rule is on the calendar for late second quarter or early third quarter — meaning, on the most aggressive schedule, potentially before July. Two pieces of operational guidance for any in-house team with public-company responsibilities. One. Pull the comment letters from the asset managers — BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street — and from the Chamber of Commerce as soon as they post to the public file. Those are the views the staff actually weights. Two. If you have not already, put the proposed-rule release on your calendar for the back half of June and assume your 2027 proxy preparation work begins the day the proposed rule lands, not the day it becomes final. The compliance runway for the larger Reg S-K reforms will likely extend into 2027, but the executive-compensation revisions specifically may move on a shorter timeline. Plan for both.

Third: the Federal Trade Commission's five-year strategic plan. On April third, 2026, the Commission published its fiscal year 2026-through-2030 Strategic Plan, the document that ordinarily attracts very little attention outside the antitrust and consumer-protection bar but which, in this cycle, deserves close reading. Three priority areas matter for media companies. One, the plan elevates children's privacy and online safety as a tier-one consumer-protection priority — consistent with what we have seen in the COPPA rulemaking and in Commissioner Meador's public comments. Two, the plan names ad-fraud and deceptive ad-tech practices as a strategic focus, which reads cleanly against the WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu consent order from April fifteenth. Three, the plan includes an antitrust focus on, in the agency's language, dominant digital platforms, which is the Commission's posture for the next four years regardless of which administration is in office. Read the plan. It is twelve pages. It is the closest the Commission gets to telegraphing what it will and will not investigate. If your 2026 compliance calendar does not at least gesture at all three priority areas, the calendar needs another draft.

Fourth: publisher economics. Yesterday, April twenty-eighth, The New York Times Company held its annual meeting of stockholders. The headline numbers, for those who did not see them: twelve million seven hundred and eighty thousand total subscribers, of which roughly twelve million two hundred thousand are paid digital-only. Subscription revenue now constitutes nearly seventy percent of total revenue. Digital advertising made up about seventy-three percent of 2025 ad revenue. The company posted nine-point-two percent revenue growth in 2025 with margin expansion of roughly one hundred and ninety basis points. Strategic goal stated by management: fifteen million subscribers by year-end 2027. At least half of subscribers are now on the bundle, which is the metric that matters operationally because bundled subscribers engage more, stay longer, and pay more over time. Here is the implication for any magazine or vertical publisher in the room: the reader-revenue flywheel is not in any meaningful sense slowing. It is broadening, and it is being captured by publishers that have built differentiated bundles. If your subscription product is single-tier and not bundled, the gap between your unit economics and the unit economics at the top of the market is widening, not narrowing. That is the revenue conversation. The legal corollary — automatic renewal compliance, multi-jurisdiction cancellation flows, retention-offer architecture — has been on this show before, and the New York City municipal Click-to-Cancel rule we have been tracking is on a virtual hearing date of May eighth. Your operations team and your legal team should be sitting in the same room for that one.

Fifth: rail. The Association of American Railroads published Week Fifteen data on April twenty-second. Total U.S. carloads for the week ending April eighteenth, 2026: two hundred thirty thousand seven hundred and forty-nine, up three percent year over year. U.S. weekly intermodal volume: two hundred seventy-seven thousand five hundred and fifty-four containers and trailers, up two-point-two percent year over year. Combined, total U.S. rail traffic was five hundred eight thousand three hundred and three units, up two-point-five percent. That is the second consecutive week of positive intermodal year-over-year growth — the first sustained move into positive territory in 2026. Year-to-date through fifteen weeks, cumulative carloads are up roughly three-point-nine percent and intermodal is now essentially flat at negative point one percent, having clawed back ground from the early-quarter weakness. For Trains and FreightWaves editorial, the manufacturing-and-construction thesis is now confirmed by carload mix — nonmetallic minerals, petroleum, and grain leading. For legal, this is mostly background, but the trajectory matters for sales planning across the freight-adjacent verticals.

Sixth: Anthropic. The Bartz fairness hearing is now fifteen days out — May fourteenth, two p.m. Pacific, before Judge William Alsup in the Northern District of California, San Francisco. The settlement remains at one and a half billion dollars total with claims paid out at roughly three thousand dollars per work to the nearly one hundred and twenty thousand authors who have submitted timely claims. Several objections to the settlement have been unsealed on the docket since I last covered the case, and the court has set the hearing for substantive review rather than a pro-forma sign-off. Two practical takeaways still hold. One, the settlement, if approved, becomes the operational floor for AI training-data infringement settlements, not the ceiling. Two, the parallel individual suits brought by opt-out plaintiffs against OpenAI, Meta, Google, xAI, and Perplexity are now in case-management posture in the same district, and several of them have set initial pre-trial conferences for the back half of the second quarter. The training-data-litigation story has moved from a single landmark case to a portfolio of cases. If any property in your company is in the AI-training data flow — as a source, as a licensee, or as a counterparty to an aggregator — the case management orders coming out of those proceedings over the next four to six weeks are worth tracking.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to the Digital Services Act second wave. This is where I want you to spend the next ten or twelve minutes, because the regulatory architecture in Europe has moved into a new posture this month, and most of the publisher and media-company reactions I have read so far underestimate what the second wave actually requires.

Let me set the scene. The Digital Services Act, Regulation 2022/2065, is the European Union's omnibus law on intermediary services online. It entered into force in November of 2022, with the obligations on Very Large Online Platforms and Very Large Online Search Engines becoming applicable in August of 2023 and the full set of obligations becoming applicable to all intermediary services on February seventeenth, 2024. The Act covers a remarkably wide range of services — hosting, online platforms, online marketplaces, search engines — and imposes, in very rough order of severity, transparency obligations, due-diligence obligations on illegal-content handling, risk-assessment and risk-mitigation obligations for systemic risks, advertising transparency requirements, dark-pattern prohibitions, recommender-system transparency, and a researcher-access regime. Maximum fines run up to six percent of annual worldwide turnover. The Commission has direct enforcement jurisdiction over the Very Large Platforms and the Very Large Search Engines. National Digital Services Coordinators, in each member state, enforce the Act with respect to other intermediary services and coordinate with the Commission.

Two things changed in April. First, on April fifteenth, the European Board for Digital Services convened in Brussels for its eighteenth meeting and outlined the supervisory measures that the National Digital Services Coordinators are taking, in coordination with the Commission, against the next tier of platforms. The Board is the formal coordinating body. The Coordinators are the national regulators. The combination — Commission plus Board plus Coordinators — is now active. We have been describing this for several weeks as the second enforcement wave. The Brussels meeting confirmed it is operational. Second, on April fifteenth, the Commission published preliminary findings against TikTok and Meta. The findings rest on two distinct theories. The first theory is researcher-access obstruction under Article Forty. The second theory, applied specifically to Meta on Facebook and Instagram, is the design of the Notice and Action mechanism — the way users flag illegal content — and the use of patterns the Commission has labeled dark patterns in the design of those flows. Both theories are doctrinally precise. The first theory rests on Article Forty's express requirement of researcher access and on the data-quality criteria that the Commission has begun to articulate in delegated acts. The second theory rests on Article Sixteen, which governs notice-and-action mechanisms, read together with Article Twenty-Five, which governs platform interfaces and dark patterns.

The reasoning is exportable. Let me explain why that matters.

If you operate any EU-facing property at scale — any property, in any vertical, that is accessible to EU users and that meets the definition of an intermediary service under the Act — the second-wave story is not about whether the Commission will name your specific platform. It is about whether the Coordinators in the member states where your users live will read the same Article Forty and Article Sixteen and Article Twenty-Five and apply it to your service. The Commission's enforcement against TikTok and Meta is the methodological template the Coordinators will apply downstream. The Commission's preliminary findings will be cited, page and paragraph, in subsequent national investigations. The investigations have already begun in several member states, although none have produced public preliminary findings yet. The cycle from a Coordinator opening a file to a public preliminary finding is on the order of six to nine months in the prior enforcement wave. We are six to nine months from a Coordinator-level enforcement story landing on a property smaller than a Very Large Platform.

Now to what a publisher of Firecrown's scale needs to be doing, specifically.

First, scope. The threshold question is whether any of your properties qualify as intermediary services, hosting services, or online platforms under the Act. The definitions are technical. Most publishing properties — articles, video, podcasts, owned-and-operated content — are not intermediary services in the strict sense. But many publisher properties have features that arguably are intermediary services. Comment sections. User-generated content forums. Reader photo galleries. Community boards. Classifieds. Marketplace integrations. Job boards. Embedded social-media login. Each of those is a candidate intermediary service. The first work, this quarter, is a property-by-property scoping memo: for each Firecrown property accessible to EU users, identify each candidate intermediary-service feature, classify it under the Act's definitions, and document the analysis. The exercise is mechanical. It takes one good lawyer two to three weeks for a portfolio the size of Firecrown's. Do it before any Coordinator asks.

Second, illegal-content handling. The Act requires intermediary services to maintain a notice-and-action mechanism that allows users to flag illegal content. The mechanism must be easily accessible, user-friendly, and capable of producing a structured notice. The acknowledgement back to the user must be timely. The takedown decision must be communicated. The user must have a path to challenge a moderation decision. The Commission's preliminary findings against Meta drill into each of those steps. If your comment section, your community forum, or your user-generated-content feature does not meet each of those steps, that is operational work. Do not assume your existing community management workflow is compliant. Test it.

Third, dark patterns. Article Twenty-Five prohibits intermediary services from designing interfaces in ways that deceive or manipulate users or otherwise materially distort their ability to make free and informed decisions. The Commission's analysis of Meta's Notice and Action flows is, in substance, an Article Twenty-Five analysis layered on top of an Article Sixteen analysis. The interface design is the regulatory exhibit. If your subscription-cancellation flow, your privacy-preferences interface, your consent banner, your account-settings page, or any other user-facing flow on an EU-accessible property uses patterns that an attentive regulator would call deceptive — confirmshaming, hidden defaults, unequal weighting of options, asymmetric friction between accept and decline — those patterns are within the regulatory perimeter under Article Twenty-Five. They are also, separately, within the regulatory perimeter under various national consumer-protection regimes and under the European Data Protection Board's guidance on consent. The exposure is layered. The remediation is one workstream.

Fourth, researcher access. This one is the most operationally novel obligation, and the one most publishers I have spoken to have not begun to think about. Article Forty requires Very Large Platforms — and, by extension, will likely require larger intermediary services as the Coordinators interpret the Act — to provide vetted researchers with access to public data necessary to study systemic risks. The mechanism is an application interface, governed by Commission-delegated acts that are still being finalized. The Commission's preliminary findings against TikTok and Meta criticize the request workflows the platforms built. If your property scales into Very Large Platform territory — forty-five million average monthly active users in the EU — Article Forty applies directly. Below that threshold, the Coordinators will likely begin to ask about analogous mechanisms in any meaningful intermediary-service property. This is the kind of obligation where the implementation runway is long but the discovery process is short. Build the policy now. The technical work of building the application interface comes later.

Fifth, the advertising transparency obligations. The Act requires intermediary services to maintain a public repository of advertising shown on their platforms, including parameters for targeting, the principal commissioner of the ad, and the period during which the ad ran. TikTok this month accepted binding commitments on advertising transparency in response to a separate Commission investigation, including a commitment to update its repository within twenty-four hours and to provide targeting criteria selected by advertisers. That commitment is now the operational benchmark. Read it. If your property carries advertising and is in the intermediary-services scope, your transparency repository — if you have one — will be measured against TikTok's commitments. If you do not have one, the question is whether your scope analysis correctly excludes you, or whether you have been operating in an unrecognized risk posture.

Three closing observations.

The first observation. The Commission has been deliberate. Each preliminary finding it has issued — TikTok in May of 2025 on advertising, the four pornographic platforms last year on protection of minors, Meta and TikTok this month on researcher access and Notice and Action — is methodologically careful. The fines have not yet been substantial because the cases have not yet reached the final-decision stage. They will. The Commission's operating tempo, slow but consistent, is not a bug. It is the design. Every preliminary finding becomes a doctrinal exhibit for the next investigation. The downstream enforcement, by Coordinators, will move faster because the Commission has built the precedent. Plan for that pacing.

The second observation. The Act's compliance logic is documentation-heavy. Risk assessments under Article Thirty-Four. Risk mitigation under Article Thirty-Five. Compliance reports under Article Thirty-Seven. Independent audits under Article Thirty-Seven. Annual transparency reports. The supervisory regime will pull on these documents first. The most expensive thing your company can do this quarter is not produce them. The second most expensive is produce them informally, without legal review, without versioning, without ownership. If you have not assigned a single named person to be the compliance owner for each property in scope, that assignment is this week's work.

The third observation. The Act is one of three frameworks the EU is now actively enforcing on digital media services. The General Data Protection Regulation, the Digital Services Act, and, increasingly, the Digital Markets Act. They overlap in substantive scope, in procedural posture, and in remedy architecture. A general counsel running an EU-facing program against any one of them needs to be running it against all three. The Coordinators talk to the Data Protection Authorities. The Commission talks to the Board. The enforcement landscape converges. Your compliance program should converge with it.

That is the Digital Services Act second wave. Read the April fifteenth Commission press release. Read the Board's eighteenth meeting communication. Read Articles Sixteen, Twenty-Five, and Forty of the Act. Then run the scoping exercise for your portfolio. Sixty days is not a long runway.

[GC BENCH]

Now to GC Bench. Two items this morning.

The first item. The Association of Corporate Counsel Legal Operations Conference convened in Chicago last week, April twentieth through twenty-second. Three takeaways from the conference floor that are worth surfacing. One. The dominant operational trend is consolidation of the legal-tech stack. In-house teams that ran twelve to fifteen point solutions in 2024 are reporting, in 2026, that they have moved to four to six platforms. The drivers are integration cost, vendor management overhead, and the simple fact that the larger platforms — CoCounsel, Lexis Protege, Harvey, the in-house-specific platforms — have built out the workflow breadth that smaller tools used to own. If you are evaluating tooling, the question is no longer best-of-breed in each category. The question is which platform reduces total integration cost while delivering acceptable workflow coverage. Two. The investment thesis on agentic legal workflows has shifted from speed to consistency. I made this point on Thursday's show. The conference floor confirmed it. The teams that are reporting genuine productivity gains describe it in terms of error reduction and audit trail, not turnaround time. Three. The intake-triage workflow is the highest-leverage automation candidate, with sixty-one percent of surveyed teams citing it as the next workflow to automate if resources allowed. We covered this on Tuesday. The conference data is consistent with the Bloomberg Law survey numbers we covered last week. The signal is strong. The vendors are credible. The remaining question is sequencing.

The second item. A practical observation on agentic workflow risk. As legal teams chain agents — research, drafting, review, redlining — the accountability questions get harder. The platform vendor, in most contracts I have read, is contractually shielded against indirect and consequential damages. The legal team operating the workflow, as a matter of professional responsibility, owns the work product. That gap is where the risk lives. The practical response is not to refuse the technology. It is to insist on a logged audit trail at every checkpoint and on human review at the points where downstream consequences are concentrated — final approval of a contract, final review of a regulatory filing, final clearance of a public statement. If your agentic workflow design does not have human review at those points, fix the design before the technology fails on a transaction that mattered.
