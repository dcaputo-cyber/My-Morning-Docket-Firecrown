[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First. The Securities and Exchange Commission's executive-compensation rulemaking. The Commission released, on the afternoon of Tuesday, May nineteenth, a notice of proposed rulemaking that would substantially restructure Item Four Oh Two of Regulation S-K, the executive-compensation-disclosure framework that has governed the American proxy statement in its current form since 2006. The proposal is, in substance, the most material executive-compensation rulemaking in nearly two decades. The principal reductions, drawn from the published notice. The Summary Compensation Table would carry two years of pay history rather than three. The Compensation Discussion and Analysis narrative is preserved but would be permitted to focus on material elements rather than the comprehensive treatment current practice has required. Several supplementary tables — including the pay-versus-performance table introduced under the Dodd-Frank Act implementation cycle — would be subject to format-and-content amendments aimed at reducing duplication. The proposal also includes amendments to the director-compensation tabular requirements and to the perquisites disclosure threshold. The notice opens a sixty-day public-comment window. The Commission has signaled that final rules could be adopted by year-end. The proxy-season planning consequence is that 2027 proxy statements will, in most cases, be the first prepared under the revised framework — which puts the implementation work on the back-half-of-this-year calendar for every public-company legal team.

Second. National Public Radio's voluntary-buyout program. The voluntary-election deadline for the program announced on Monday, May eighteenth, closes today, Tuesday, May twenty-sixth. The program targeted up to thirty positions across the newsroom, with the organization signaling that involuntary reductions would follow if voluntary elections did not reach the target. The closing of the voluntary window today opens the next phase. If voluntary elections meet the target, the involuntary-reduction risk is removed. If they fall short, the Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act analysis that the in-house team has been running in parallel becomes the operative document. The broader operational frame, which yesterday's deep dive examined at length, is that the newsroom-compression cycle of the second quarter — Washington Post, Columbia Broadcasting System, the closure of CBS News Radio after ninety-nine years, the Daily Wire, Business Insider, and now National Public Radio — is the most material change to the American newsroom labor market in a single quarter since the pandemic-era compression of 2020. The cycle is not over. The next-shoe-to-drop posture is the operating posture for the in-house bar at the major publishers for the remainder of the second quarter.

Third. The Texas Attorney General and Netflix. The Office of the Attorney General announced, on Monday, May eleventh, the filing of a lawsuit against Netflix, Incorporated, in Texas state court under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices and Consumer Protection Act. The complaint alleges that Netflix collected personal information in a manner the Attorney General has characterized as deceptive — specifically, that the platform's representations regarding data collection and use, made through the user-facing privacy disclosures, did not match the underlying collection practices. The action is one of several enforcement matters opened in May by the Texas Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division under the umbrella of the privacy and data initiative the office formalized earlier in the year. The Netflix action sits alongside the Wednesday-the-twentieth inquiry into Meta Platforms over the Ray-Ban-branded smart-glasses product that I covered yesterday. The pattern for an in-house counsel at a media company operating in the Texas market is clear. The Texas Attorney General's privacy and data initiative is producing a sustained cadence of enforcement filings, and the substantive theory of the cases — the alignment between the user-facing disclosure and the underlying collection practice — is the theory that any publisher's privacy posture needs to be re-audited against before the next quarterly compliance review.

Fifth. The Connecticut Data Privacy Act amendments. The amendments enacted as Senate Bill Twelve-Ninety-Five in June of 2025 become effective on Wednesday, July first — thirty-six days from this morning. Three structural changes worth carrying for the in-house team. First, the applicability threshold has been lowered. The previous one-hundred-thousand-consumer threshold has come down to thirty-five thousand, and new no-threshold triggers tied to specific processing activities have been added. The applicability analysis a media company performed in 2023 or 2024 is, as of July first, an obsolete analysis. Second, the privacy-notice requirements have been expanded. The amendments require disclosure of whether the entity uses or sells personal data to train large-language models. The disclosure is mandatory, and the format is prescribed. Third, an impact-assessment obligation has been added for any profiling activity that produces a legal or similarly significant effect, with the assessment requirement applying to processing activities created or generated on or after August first. The sensitive-data definition has also been expanded to include neural data, financial details, gender-identity status, medical-disability and treatment information, biometric-and-genetic-derived information, and government-issued identification numbers. The implementation work needs to be in the file before the July first effective date. Thirty-six days.

[DEEP DIVE]
The deep dive this morning is the Securities and Exchange Commission's executive-compensation rulemaking. I want to take the architecture first, then the four operational consequences for a media-company general counsel, then three concrete actions for the back-half-of-2026 calendar.

The architecture first. Item Four Oh Two of Regulation S-K is, in its current form, the product of a 2006 rulemaking that overhauled the executive-compensation-disclosure framework after a decade of incremental amendments. The current framework requires a Compensation Discussion and Analysis narrative, a Summary Compensation Table covering three years of pay history for the named executive officers, a set of supplementary tables addressing grants of plan-based awards, outstanding equity awards, option exercises, pension benefits, nonqualified deferred compensation, and post-employment payments, plus disclosure of the Chief Executive Officer pay ratio, the pay-versus-performance table introduced under Dodd-Frank, and the perquisites threshold of ten thousand dollars per category. The framework is, as anyone who has prepared a proxy in the past five years can attest, comprehensive. The architecture has come, over the last decade, to support a roughly thirty-page executive-compensation section in the median large-cap proxy statement, with the largest issuers running fifty pages or more. The Securities and Exchange Commission's May nineteenth notice of proposed rulemaking is the first comprehensive revisitation of that architecture since 2006.

The reductions, in summary. The Summary Compensation Table would be reduced to two years of pay history. The Compensation Discussion and Analysis would be preserved but with a narrower required scope — material elements only, rather than the comprehensive narrative that current practice has required. Several supplementary tables would be consolidated or eliminated. The director-compensation tabular requirement would be amended to align with the revised Summary Compensation Table format. The perquisites threshold would be raised. The pay-versus-performance table would remain but with a narrower data set. The Chief Executive Officer pay-ratio disclosure would remain in form but with modifications to the methodology requirements. The collective reduction in the median proxy statement's executive-compensation section is, on the Commission staff's own estimates set out in the release's economic analysis, on the order of twenty to thirty percent of current pages and roughly forty percent of preparation hours. The reduction is consequential.

The structural redirection, however, is the more interesting element of the proposal. The Commission's notice characterizes the rulemaking as a refocusing of Regulation S-K on disclosure a reasonable investor would consider important for an investment or voting decision. The phrase is doctrinally significant. The current framework reflects a decade of accretive additions — the Dodd-Frank pay-ratio rule, the Dodd-Frank pay-versus-performance rule, the various Sarbanes-Oxley-era amendments — that the Commission has, under Chairman Atkins's direction, characterized as the source of the current framework's bulk. The redirection is toward a leaner, principles-based regime that places more weight on the Compensation Discussion and Analysis narrative and less weight on tabular comprehensiveness. The redirection is also, by implication, a shift away from the say-on-pay institutional-investor-proxy-advisory analytical model that the supplementary tables were designed to support. The institutional-investor community has, in the comment cycles leading up to the proposal, signaled a mixed reception — the Council of Institutional Investors, the Investor Stewardship Group, and the principal proxy advisors have generally opposed reductions in the comprehensive-disclosure regime — and the comment-window record on the proposal will be the document that determines the final-rule shape.

That is the architecture. Now the four operational consequences for a media-company general counsel.

First. The 2027 proxy-season planning cycle is, as of this morning, structurally different from the 2026 cycle. The current proposal opens a sixty-day comment window from the May nineteenth publication, which closes on Saturday, July eighteenth. Final-rule adoption, on the Commission's signaled timeline, is targeted for year-end. The earliest practical effective date for a final rule adopted by year-end is the 2027 proxy season — calendar-year filers will, in most cases, be the first cohort to prepare a proxy under the revised framework. The compensation-committee planning work for that cycle needs to commence in the third quarter, not the fourth. The institutional knowledge that has accreted around the current framework over twenty years does not transfer cleanly to the new framework. The drafting work, the cross-referencing work, the controls-and-procedures work, and the disclosure-committee work all need to be re-scoped against the proposed framework before the year-end audit cycle begins.

Second. The compensation-committee-charter implications need attention. The current framework's tabular requirements have, over the last fifteen years, become embedded in the compensation-committee-charter language of most public companies, and in the related-policies architecture that the compensation committee operates under — the clawback policy, the stock-ownership-guideline policy, the hedging-and-pledging policy, the executive-severance policy, the change-in-control policy. The proposed reductions do not automatically obsolete that architecture, but they do produce a re-alignment opportunity. The charter-and-policy review that most public companies execute on an annual cycle should, this year, be re-framed against the proposed rule structure, so that the charter language and the disclosure framework arrive at the 2027 proxy season in coordinated form.

Third. The institutional-investor-engagement calendar shifts. The proxy-advisory recommendations that have been calibrated against the current framework — the Institutional Shareholder Services Quality Score, the Glass Lewis benchmark policy, the major asset-manager voting guidelines — will need to be re-calibrated against the new framework. The engagement-cycle work that compensation committees do in the third quarter of each year — meeting with the major institutional holders, walking through the compensation philosophy, responding to questions about pay-versus-performance — will be a different conversation in 2026 than it was in 2025. The conversation will, in most cases, include an investor-side concern about the reduction in disclosure transparency, and the company-side response will need to be prepared. The investor-relations team and the in-house team need to coordinate the response posture before the engagement cycle opens.

Fourth, and most consequential for media companies specifically. The executive-compensation framework intersects the press-and-publishing function in a way that is distinctive to the media sector. The compensation arrangements of senior media-company executives — the chief executive officer, the chief financial officer, the publisher, the editor in chief in some structures, the chief revenue officer — are, in the media-trade-press environment, perpetual subjects of journalistic coverage. The reduction in tabular disclosure will, paradoxically, increase the importance of the narrative disclosure as the principal source of public information about executive compensation. The Compensation Discussion and Analysis becomes, under the proposed framework, the document that the trade press will work from. The drafting standard for that document needs to anticipate that role. The pre-publication review of the Compensation Discussion and Analysis by the general counsel's office, in coordination with the corporate-communications team, becomes a more substantial review than it has historically been.

Three concrete actions for the back-half-of-2026 calendar.

Action one. Schedule the compensation-committee briefing on the proposed framework before the end of June. The briefing should cover the proposal in substantive detail, the comment-window dynamics, the expected final-rule shape, and the calendar for the 2027 proxy-season implementation work. The briefing should be a working session, not a presentation. The compensation committee's input on the comment-letter posture — whether the company will submit a comment, whether the company will participate in joint comments through industry associations, what positions the company will support — needs to be on the record before the July eighteenth comment-window close.

Action two. Run the institutional-investor-engagement-calendar audit. The third-quarter engagement cycle is the cycle in which most companies meet with their major holders to discuss compensation philosophy and any changes contemplated. The 2026 cycle will engage the proposed framework as a substantive topic. The talking points need to be drafted, the responses to expected investor questions need to be prepared, and the coordination between the in-house team, the investor-relations team, the corporate-communications team, and the chief financial officer's office needs to be established before the first meeting on the calendar.

Action three. Commence the 2027-proxy implementation work in the third quarter. The implementation work includes the disclosure-controls-and-procedures review, the cross-functional drafting protocol, the timeline for the audit-committee and compensation-committee review cycles, and the engagement of outside counsel and outside compensation consultants. The implementation work historically begins in the fourth quarter. The 2026 cycle is the cycle in which it needs to begin in the third quarter, because the framework is new, the institutional knowledge in the office has been calibrated to the old framework, and the lead time required to produce a coordinated, well-drafted, defensible 2027 proxy under a new framework is meaningfully longer than the lead time required to produce a 2026 proxy under the framework the office knows.

A closing observation on the rulemaking. The Securities and Exchange Commission under Chairman Atkins has been, since the January thirteenth Regulation S-K-modernization announcement, signaling a structural reorientation of the disclosure regime — leaner, more principles-based, more focused on material disclosure to a reasonable investor, less focused on the accretive tabular framework that twenty years of incremental rulemaking has produced. The May nineteenth executive-compensation proposal is the first concrete output of that reorientation. The proposal is also unlikely to be the last. The companion rulemakings on environmental disclosure, on cybersecurity disclosure, on the climate-related disclosure rule that the Commission rescinded earlier this month, and on the broader Regulation S-K framework will follow in the second half of the year and into the first half of 2027. The in-house team that has built the playbook for the executive-compensation proposal is the in-house team that has the institutional infrastructure in place when the next proposal lands.

[GC BENCH]
On GC Bench this morning, two related legal-technology releases from Harvey, the legal-artificial-intelligence vendor that closed its two-hundred-million-dollar round earlier this year at an eleven-billion-dollar valuation. The first release, announced Wednesday, May sixth, is the Legal Agent Benchmark — the acronym is LAB — an open-source benchmark for legal-artificial-intelligence agents covering more than twelve hundred tasks across twenty-four legal practice areas. The benchmark is meaningful because the legal-technology procurement conversation, for the last eighteen months, has been operating without a shared performance baseline. Every vendor has produced its own evaluation framework. Every customer has produced its own assessment methodology. The market has, in consequence, been impossible to compare on substantive grounds. The Legal Agent Benchmark, if it gains industry adoption, becomes the comparative substrate. The vendor that has released the benchmark is, naturally, the vendor that has the most confidence in its own product's performance against the benchmark — but the structural significance of an open-source benchmark is independent of which vendor released it. The procurement conversation, in the second half of 2026, will engage the benchmark as a reference point.

The second release, announced Wednesday, May twentieth, is Command Center — a peer-based dashboard that allows law firms and in-house teams to share their Harvey usage data with their peers in the customer base. The structural significance is the introduction of comparative-performance data at the customer level. An in-house legal team can now see how its Harvey utilization compares to peer in-house teams. A law firm can see how its Harvey utilization compares to peer law firms. The dashboard is, on its face, a peer-benchmarking tool. The dashboard is, in substance, a behavioral-modification tool — the team that sees its utilization lagging the peer set is the team that, on the next budgeting cycle, gets the question from the chief financial officer about whether the licensing investment is producing the expected return. The peer-benchmarking conversation between the in-house team and the chief financial officer is the conversation the dashboard is designed to facilitate, and the structural consequence is that the legal-technology budget conversation has moved from the abstract — the case for adoption — into the comparative — the case for utilization. The procurement question for the second-half budget cycle, in addition to the questions about per-action consumption pricing that I covered yesterday, is the question of whether the in-house team has the workflow architecture in place to convert the licensing investment into measurable utilization against the peer-benchmark data the dashboard now produces.
