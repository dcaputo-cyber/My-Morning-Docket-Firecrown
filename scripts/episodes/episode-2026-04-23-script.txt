[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up: New York City. On April eighth, Mayor Zohran Kwame Mamdani and Consumer and Worker Protection Commissioner Samuel Levine announced a proposed municipal Click-to-Cancel rule, published in the City Record that same day. The proposal requires that if a consumer can sign up for a subscription online, the consumer must be able to cancel online, using a path that is, in the rule's language, at least as simple as the enrollment path. Thirty-day public comment period. Virtual hearing scheduled for May eighth. If adopted, New York City becomes the first municipality in the United States to have its own cancellation rule on the books. That matters, because it arrives in the vacuum left when the Eighth Circuit vacated the FTC's national Click-to-Cancel rule last July on procedural grounds. More on both of those developments in the deep dive.

Second: the Department of Justice's remedies order against Google. On April fourteenth, the District Court for the District of Columbia entered the remedies order in the Google search monopolization case. The order prohibits Google from entering into exclusive distribution contracts for Search, Chrome, the Google Assistant, and the Gemini app. It compels Google to share its search index and certain user-interaction data with qualifying rivals, subject to oversight by a technical committee. Google has filed its notice of appeal and moved for a stay pending review. The Justice Department filed a cross-appeal by its February third deadline, seeking broader remedies, including divestitures that the district court declined to order. Two takeaways for any publisher. One: the data-sharing obligations create, for the first time, a programmatic path for non-Google search providers to close the quality gap. That is not a six-month story, but it is a story worth building a strategy around. Two: the consent mechanics of the data-sharing regime will be litigated. Any Firecrown property that ever furnished data to Google's index — which is all of them — is going to want to understand how downstream sharing affects its own terms with Google. That is a conversation for the second half of this year, not today, but put the date on the calendar.

Third: Anthropic. The Bartz versus Anthropic fairness hearing, which had been calendared for today, April twenty-third, has been moved. On April eighth, Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín issued an order rescheduling the hearing to May fourteenth, 2026, at two p.m. Pacific, at the San Francisco Federal Courthouse, Courtroom twelve, nineteenth floor. Class members who have timely objected may appear by Zoom. The settlement terms have not changed — one and a half billion dollars, roughly three thousand dollars per work, approximately one hundred and twenty thousand claims filed to date. What has changed is the schedule, and a number of objections have now been unsealed on the docket. If you were planning to listen in today, today is not the day. Reset your calendar for May fourteenth.

Fourth: Oregon and Connecticut. Yesterday I promised a return to the universal opt-out story, so here it is. Oregon's universal opt-out mechanism, required under the Oregon Consumer Privacy Act, went live on January first, 2026, and on January twenty-eighth, Oregon Data Privacy Day, Attorney General Dan Rayfield took the unusual step of publicly highlighting the opt-out tool and publishing a plain-English handout explaining how Oregon residents can use it. The cure period — the brief grace window during which businesses could fix a violation without penalty — has now expired. Oregon businesses and nonprofits are fully accountable for compliance. Connecticut's universal opt-out, recall, took effect a year earlier, on January first, 2025, and the Attorney General's office has exclusive enforcement authority there, with civil penalties of up to five thousand dollars per violation under the Connecticut Unfair Trade Practices Act. Twelve states now require businesses to recognize a universal opt-out signal. That list is now Connecticut, Oregon, California, Colorado, Delaware, Maryland, Minnesota, Montana, New Jersey, New Hampshire, and Texas, plus Nebraska coming online. The implication for a national media operation is simple. Your consent platform either supports universal opt-out signals reliably across all of those jurisdictions, or it does not. There is not a good third option.

Fifth: Texas. On April fifteenth, Attorney General Ken Paxton's office sent formal notices to more than one hundred companies warning of apparent non-compliance with the Texas Data Broker Law. The Texas statute requires data brokers — defined broadly — to register annually with the Secretary of State and to publish specific disclosures on their websites. Paxton's office identified entities that appear to meet the definition and have not registered. No fines have been announced. These are letters, not enforcement actions. But Paxton's office has been the most aggressive state-level privacy enforcer in the country this year. The one-point-three-seven-five-billion-dollar settlement with Google from last May is the headline. The data-broker sweep is the pattern. If any Firecrown vertical operates audience data products — and several do, across the publishing and events business — confirm that registration is current in Texas, and pull the disclosures to make sure they reflect the current practice. Do not assume that because you have never thought of yourselves as a data broker, you are not one under the Texas definition. The definition is broader than most GCs assume.

Sixth: the United Kingdom. The Information Commissioner's Office continues to work through its enforcement docket. On March twenty-fifth, the ICO fined Police Scotland sixty-six thousand pounds and issued a formal reprimand for what the regulator called serious failures in the handling of sensitive personal data — specifically, the extraction of the entire contents of a victim's mobile phone and subsequent unlawful disclosure. That is a public-sector matter, but it is a useful calibration point. Two months earlier, on February twenty-fourth, the ICO fined Reddit fourteen-point-four-seven million pounds for using children's personal data unlawfully, with the specific finding that self-declared age gates do not constitute robust age assurance. The direction of travel at the ICO is unmistakable. Fewer total fines this cycle — but the average fine has moved from a hundred and fifty thousand pounds to over two-point-eight million. The ICO is picking targets and going harder. Separately, the Data Use and Access Act Section 103 complaints deadline — June nineteenth — is now fifty-eight days away. If you do not have a documented complaints process for UK data subjects, that is this month's work.

Seventh, and our final headline: the freight economy. New numbers from FreightWaves CEO Craig Fuller. On April fourth, Fuller published that the National Truckload Index, the seven-day moving average of booked dry van spot rates including fuel, broke out to three dollars and ten cents a mile — the highest level since March of 2022. The flatbed equivalent, the Flatbed Trucking Index, hit three dollars and ninety-five cents a mile, the highest level the series has ever recorded. The Association of American Railroads reported that for the week ending April eleventh, total U.S. carloads came in at two hundred and twenty-eight thousand, six hundred and sixty-six — up five-point-one percent year over year. Intermodal was two hundred and seventy-one thousand, three hundred and seventy-four units — down one-point-one percent. Carloads up. Intermodal still soft. Same divergence we saw in March, now confirmed in April. Manufacturing is back. Consumer goods flow is not. If you advise FreightWaves, Trains, or any of the industrial-demand-facing properties, that is the editorial frame that is consistent with the numbers.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to the subscription law landscape. This is where I want to spend the next ten or twelve minutes, because the legal architecture around how a media company charges recurring money has changed more in the last ninety days than it has in the preceding three years, and the shape of the change is not what I would have predicted twelve months ago.

Let me set the scene. In October of 2024, the Federal Trade Commission under Chair Lina Khan finalized the Click-to-Cancel rule — formally, the amended Negative Option Rule. The final rule required, among other things, that any business offering a recurring subscription provide a cancellation path as simple as the enrollment path, that consent to auto-renewal be express and specific, and that material terms be clearly and conspicuously disclosed before billing. The rule had a compliance deadline in July of 2025. In July of 2025, just days before that deadline, the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit unanimously vacated the rule. The panel did not reach the merits. It held, on procedural grounds, that the Commission had failed to comply with certain statutory requirements in the rulemaking process — specifically, the threshold analysis triggered when a proposed rule's estimated impact exceeds a statutory dollar threshold.

So: the rule is dead as a federal rule. And yet the enforcement landscape has arguably intensified since. That is worth understanding.

First, the FTC has made clear it is not backing off the underlying practices. Since January of 2025, the Commission has initiated at least five new matters and approved at least six settlements involving allegedly deceptive negative-option practices. The list of respondents includes Match Group, Chegg, Cleo AI, and Amazon — the Amazon matter resolving at roughly two and a half billion dollars, which at the time was the largest such settlement in American history. Every one of those matters proceeds under the FTC Act's core prohibitions on unfair or deceptive acts or practices, and on the existing Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act. ROSCA, which is the 2010 statute underlying most of the negative-option case law, is unaffected by the Eighth Circuit's ruling. ROSCA requires, in substance, clear and conspicuous disclosure, express informed consent, and a simple mechanism to stop recurring charges. If you run a subscription business, those three requirements are your compliance floor, still. The vacated rule would have put specific operational requirements on top of ROSCA. The rule is gone. The statute is not.

Second, the Commission has restarted the rulemaking. On March eleventh, 2026, the FTC issued an advance notice of proposed rulemaking — an ANPRM — seeking public input on a replacement negative-option rule. The ANPRM is a signal, not a rule. But the signal is that the Commission intends to build back a negative-option rule and to do it with the procedural care that would survive appellate review this time. If you have a subscription product, now is the window to submit comment. Comments from the publisher community have been underrepresented in the record to date relative to the ecommerce and SaaS communities. That is an asymmetry worth correcting.

Third — and this is the part that has surprised most GCs I talk to — the states and the cities have stepped into the federal gap with enthusiasm. California's Automatic Renewal Law, amended in 2024, is the most mature. It requires clear and conspicuous disclosure, affirmative consent to any material change in the terms of an auto-renewing subscription, and online cancellation for subscriptions that are initiated online. New York State has had its own Automatic Renewal Statute since 2021. Fifteen other states have statutes in the same family. And now, New York City. The Mamdani administration's proposed municipal rule, which I mentioned in the roundup, goes farther than the state law in specific ways. The proposed rule requires that cancellation be available through at least two channels, one of which must be online for any subscription sold online. It requires specific disclosures at two points: at enrollment, and again when a price increase or material change is about to take effect. It requires that the cancellation path not impose what the rule calls quote, unnecessary friction, end quote — and it defines that phrase with reference to specific UX patterns, including dark patterns that have been the subject of FTC settlements.

If you sell a subscription product in New York City — and if you operate a publisher of any scale, you do — the NYC rule is going to require UX and legal review in parallel before its effective date. Public comment closes in early May. The virtual hearing is May eighth. Realistic effective date, based on how DCWP has moved in prior rulemakings, is late summer to early fall. That gives you perhaps four months. Not eighteen. Four.

Four practical actions for an in-house GC at a publisher that sells subscriptions.

One. Map your subscription products by jurisdiction of consumer. Do this now if you have not. At minimum: which product is sold into California, New York State, New York City specifically — which is a subset of New York State — and any other state with a signed auto-renewal statute. The Lowenstein Sandler piece and the Goodwin piece that came out last week both have useful matrices, and I will link them in the shownotes. Your subscription marketing team probably already has this data but has not organized it by the legal framework. Ask for it organized that way.

Two. Pull your cancellation flows and screen-capture them. For each subscription product, capture the enrollment flow and the cancellation flow side by side. Count the clicks. Count the fields. Count the dead ends. If the enrollment flow is three clicks and the cancellation flow is seven, or if the cancellation flow routes through a retention offer the user cannot dismiss, that is an enforcement exposure under existing law, let alone under any new municipal rule. The FTC and the state AGs have increasingly begun these matters by screen-recording enrollment and cancellation flows, and offering those recordings as exhibits. You want to be the GC who has already done that exercise.

Three. Audit your retention offers. California's ARL and the proposed NYC rule both treat certain retention-offer patterns as material terms requiring consent to proceed. A retention offer that substantially changes the subscription — lower price, added benefits, different term length — is, in the eyes of the regulators, a new contract, not a preservation of the old one. If your cancellation flow automatically triggers a retention offer without clear decline paths, you are accepting legal risk.

Four. Review any third-party billing or identity vendors in the cancellation path. Chargebee, Stripe Billing, Recurly, any of the retention platforms — your contractual terms with those vendors probably do not allocate liability for rule-compliance failures in the cancellation UX. Those terms were drafted before Click-to-Cancel, before NYC's proposal, and before the recent state-law amendments. This quarter is a good window to revisit those MSAs and add representations, warranties, and indemnification language specific to subscription-law compliance.

One final observation before we move off this topic. The political framing of Click-to-Cancel has been about consumer rights and dark patterns, and those are real. But the quieter story — and the one that matters for publisher economics — is that subscription regulation is now being written in a patchwork. Federal. State. Municipal. That patchwork favors large publishers with in-house privacy and consumer-protection resources. It disfavors the smaller titles. The 100k Club we talked about yesterday is going to continue to be the list of publishers who can afford the compliance infrastructure that the 10k list cannot. If you are a mid-sized publisher planning to grow into the 100k range, you are planning into a regulatory surface area that has tripled since 2023. Price that in.

[GC BENCH]

Now to GC Bench. This week, one item and one note.

The item. Earlier this month, IAB Tech Lab formally convened a new Programmatic Governance Council, bringing in founding members that include Omnicom Media Group, Amazon Ads, and a short list of other holding-company and demand-side-platform participants. The stated purpose is to improve transparency and standards in programmatic advertising, which now represents more than two hundred billion dollars of annual U.S. advertising spend. I note this for two reasons.

One: the timing is not coincidental. The Programmatic Governance Council announcement landed within days of the FTC consent order against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu that we covered yesterday. The industry read of the council is that it is a structured response to the enforcement action — an effort to create a transparent, governable, auditable mechanism for the things GARM used to do informally, while avoiding the horizontal coordination theory that got GARM disbanded in 2024 and that drove the Commission's complaint. Whether this particular structure avoids the coordination trap is a question the FTC monitors will have a view on. The structure matters. Read it carefully when the council publishes its charter.

Two: for a publisher, this is a moment to get on the list of participants or consulted parties. Programmatic governance is being re-architected. The shape of the next decade of ad-ops rules is being written now. If Firecrown's demand-side presence — through FreightWaves, Trains, FLYING, Boating and Sailing — has not had a conversation with IAB Tech Lab about council representation, that is a conversation worth having. Self-interest aside: publishers have historically been under-represented in these governance bodies relative to agencies and platforms. That imbalance has costs.

The note. One piece of legal-tech news worth flagging. Several of the major in-house AI platforms have started shipping what they are calling agentic workflow orchestration — the ability to chain a series of tasks across different specialized agents, with checkpoints for human review. Lexis Protege, CoCounsel from Thomson Reuters, and a handful of startup platforms are all pushing this direction. The operational benefit, based on what peers are reporting, is not speed. It is consistency. An agentic workflow that does the same five-step review on every inbound NDA, with a logged audit trail, produces fewer errors than a single human reviewer doing the same work at three p.m. on a Thursday when five other things are on fire. That is the actual case for this technology. Not that it is faster. That it is more consistent. If you are evaluating tools, evaluate them on the consistency metric. That is where the value lives.

Quickly now, three items.

Freight. I covered the NTI and flatbed numbers in the roundup. One further point. Craig Fuller's commentary from his April fourth piece — the industrial recovery is real, manufacturing is on course for one of its best markets in years, and flatbed, which carries the inputs to factory construction, is the leading indicator. That is the editorial frame. It also has a legal adjacent implication: if your clients' freight volumes are rising in line with this trend, their CCPA and state-privacy exposure is rising with it, because volume correlates with the number of consumer interactions. A good moment to do a targeted outreach to your top industrial advertisers with a privacy-readiness offer. Not cynical. Just useful.

Rail. I mentioned the April eleventh AAR numbers — carloads up five-point-one percent, intermodal down one-point-one percent. Year to date through fourteen weeks, carloads are up four percent, intermodal essentially flat. The divergence is the story. For Trains readers and advertisers, the bullish carload data is the editorial hook. For legal purposes, the intermodal weakness suggests continued margin pressure on the Class I railroads' intermodal segments, which is going to influence how those railroads price ad sponsorships and partnership deals at Trains properties for the back half of the year. Worth flagging for revenue planning.

Aviation. The Mental Health in Aviation Act, Senate Bill three-two-five-seven, was unanimously advanced by the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation on April fourteenth. The bill would direct the FAA to implement, within two years, the recommendations from the agency's 2024 Mental Health and Aviation Medical Clearances rulemaking committee. It encourages voluntary reporting, expands approved treatment options, and addresses backlog in the special-issuance process. It also appropriates fifteen million dollars annually through 2029 to the FAA Office of Aerospace Medicine. For FLYING's editorial side, this is a significant policy story. For legal, it is worth noting that any FAA rulemaking that follows this bill will generate a public comment record the aviation community should engage with. Pilots, AMEs, mental-health professionals, and aviation publishers all have standing. FLYING's editorial voice there is an asset. Coordinate with editorial on timing and framing so neither the legal nor the editorial arm walks into a conflict with the other.
