[HEADLINE ROUNDUP]

First up: the FTC and the holding companies. On April fifteenth, the Federal Trade Commission announced a proposed consent order, filed in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas, resolving a complaint against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu over what the Commission called collusive coordination on brand safety standards. Eight state attorneys general joined the complaint — Florida, Indiana, Iowa, Montana, Nebraska, Texas, Utah, and West Virginia. The theory of the case is that the three agencies, together with Omnicom and Interpublic, operated through industry trade associations to establish a common brand-safety floor that effectively demonetized disfavored content. The proposed order lasts ten years. It bars the agencies from entering into any agreement that restricts ad placement on the basis of, quote, political or social commentary. And it subjects the agencies to five years of oversight by FTC-approved independent monitors. WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu neither admitted nor denied the allegations. The settlement has not yet been approved. I'll spend more time on this in the deep dive. For the moment, put the date on your calendar: April fifteenth, 2026. The rules changed.

[DEEP DIVE]

Now to the FTC consent orders. This is where I want you to spend the next ten minutes. If you sit at a media company that is a publisher on one side of the ledger and a buyer of advertising on the other — and most of us do — these orders touch both sides. And the implications are not obvious.

Here is what the Commission alleged, distilled. Since 2018, the three largest holding companies in advertising — WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu — working alongside Omnicom and Interpublic, coordinated through industry trade associations, most notably the Global Alliance for Responsible Media, known as GARM, and relied on external rating shops like NewsGuard and the Global Disinformation Index, to establish a common brand-safety floor. The floor identified categories of content — hate speech, incitement, misinformation, and so on — below which participating advertisers agreed not to spend. In the Commission's telling, that floor became an exclusion mechanism. It produced effectively identical media plans across the largest advertisers in the United States, demonetized particular publishers and viewpoints, and, because it was coordinated through trade associations rather than negotiated bilaterally, crossed into per-se coordination under the Sherman Act.

The proposed order, which runs for ten years, does three things you should read carefully. First, it bars the agencies from entering into agreements with competitors to set common brand-safety standards. Not bars them from having brand-safety standards — bars them from agreeing with competitors on what those standards are. Second, it bars them from restricting advertising placement based on what the order calls political or social commentary. Third, it imposes five years of oversight by independent monitors approved by the Commission, with access rights to agency internal communications.

Four takeaways for a general counsel at a media company.

One. If you are a publisher, your standing with the ad-buying side of the market has just improved. Agencies that previously could point to a GARM category and decline to buy will have a harder time doing that now. That does not mean you will get more revenue overnight. It means your argument at the table is stronger. Your programmatic inclusion list will see more inbound requests for unblocking. Your sales team should be trained on the specific language of the consent order so they can reference it in negotiation. The line to know, rough paraphrase, is that the Commission has barred coordinated content-based exclusion through trade-association mechanisms. That is the line. Arm your sellers with it.

Two. If you are a buyer of advertising — through e-commerce, through demand-gen, or any other channel — you need to audit your agency contracts. Specifically: any contractual incorporation of GARM categories, any reference to NewsGuard ratings or Global Disinformation Index scores as a buying filter, any clause that requires your agency to apply a common brand-safety floor across accounts, is now an operational risk. Not a legal risk to you, directly. But an execution risk: your agency is going to be reconfiguring its standards framework over the next twelve months, and the sooner you understand how, the less disruption you take. Ask your account lead, this week, for a written summary of what changes and when.

Three. Watch what the five-year monitoring produces. The Commission's monitors will have access to internal agency communications. Historically, antitrust monitorships produce slow-drip evidentiary revelations — settlements with ad tech companies, clarifications of coordination that was not charged, and eventually, in many cases, private follow-on litigation. If you are on the receiving end of an advertising decision that feels like retaliation for editorial content, the monitorship record will, in three to five years, be a searchable resource. Flag incidents now. Keep correspondence.

Four. Prepare for follow-on civil litigation, because it is coming. The pattern in Sherman Act consent orders is predictable. Within twelve to twenty-four months of the final order, plaintiffs' firms begin filing private actions under Section 4 of the Clayton Act, which allows treble damages for injuries caused by antitrust violations. The most natural plaintiffs here are the publishers that were excluded under the brand-safety floor — conservative outlets, yes, but also independent political commentators, fringe-subject enthusiast publications, and any media property that found itself categorically demonetized in 2020 through 2024. The statute of limitations under Section 4 runs four years from the date of injury, which means claims going back to 2022 are live now. If you are in-house at an ad-supported publisher that was on the wrong side of a brand-safety exclusion list during that period, you have a claim to assess. I am not telling you to file. I am telling you to assess. The economics of follow-on antitrust litigation — plaintiff-side contingency, deep discovery, treble damages — favor aggressive claimants. Your competitors will be assessing too. Do not be the publisher that assessed two years too late.

One more observation, which I'll offer carefully. The political framing of this case — that the FTC was targeting ideological demonetization — has dominated the coverage, particularly in the right-leaning press. The legal theory of the case, as filed, is a Section 1 Sherman Act theory: horizontal coordination among competitors to restrict output. That theory does not depend on the political framing and will outlive it. If you are reading coverage that reduces this to a culture-war case, you are reading coverage that is going to mislead you about what the order does. What the order does is restrict coordination, full stop. The underlying content categories — political commentary, social commentary, misinformation, adult, violent, and the rest — remain matters each agency can address internally, so long as it does not coordinate. Plan accordingly.

A brief historical note, because it is worth understanding how we got here. GARM — the Global Alliance for Responsible Media — was launched in June of 2019 under the auspices of the World Federation of Advertisers. Its member list at the peak included every major holding company, every major platform, and most of the Fortune one hundred. In July of 2024, GARM dissolved itself, abruptly, after the House Judiciary Committee issued a report and after Elon Musk sued it and several of its members in the Northern District of Texas. The FTC complaint now in front of Judge Albright picks up where those earlier actions left off. The through-line is the same: coordination among competitors to exclude content, irrespective of whether the exclusion criteria are defensible. The Commission's position is not that brand-safety judgments are impermissible. It is that coordinated brand-safety judgments, made through a multilateral mechanism, are per-se illegal. If you had counseled a client into GARM in 2020, and many in-house teams did, that advice was not wrong in 2020. But it is wrong now. The facts on the ground have changed. Your playbook needs to update.

Finally: the Omnicom-and-Interpublic question. Both holding companies were named in the Commission's complaint as coconspirators but did not settle. Omnicom is mid-merger with Interpublic — the deal closed, as a matter of form, last year. The merged entity is now the largest advertising holding company in the world by revenue. The Commission's complaint against WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu is, by implication, a shot across Omnicom's bow. If I were in-house at the merged Omnicom-Interpublic, I would expect follow-on civil discovery, at minimum. If I were writing a contract with Omnicom today, I would include a representation-and-warranty clause specifically addressing pending FTC monitorship obligations. That is the kind of clause your counterparty does not want to give, and the kind of clause that, if it is given, tells you something about how they rate their own exposure.
